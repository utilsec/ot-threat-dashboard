Top Story

“FortiBleed” credential theft now feeds active ransomware — and those FortiGate firewalls front the remote access food and beverage plants rely on

CISA warns the FortiBleed campaign has harvested working administrator and VPN credentials from tens of thousands of internet-facing FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways — tied to roughly 74,000 exposed Fortinet devices inside an operation that collected over 110 million credentials since mid-June. On 2 July researchers linked the stolen access directly to the INC Ransom and Lynx ransomware crews, with at least a dozen deployments traced to it. Food and beverage processors are attractive ransomware targets because production and cold-chain downtime spoils product fast, and these appliances front the remote access and IT/OT segmentation that reach plant PLCs, packaging lines and refrigeration controls. Because valid credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, no patch closes the gap and an intruder logs in looking legitimate — treat any exposed FortiGate fronting plant OT as already compromised.

Active — confirmed exploitation; now feeding INC/Lynx ransomware (12+ deployments) CISA alert 18 Jun; DHS IoCs 2 Jul 2026
Mitigations available (Fortinet / CISA) Action: Treat every internet-facing FortiGate/SSL-VPN fronting plant OT as compromised: terminate active sessions, rotate all admin and VPN credentials, reissue certificates, enforce MFA, remove management interfaces from the internet, and hunt for unauthorized config pulls using the DHS-shared IoCs.
Suggested SLA: Session kill, credential reset & IoC sweep — 24h (active credential abuse on OT-bridging edge) Owner:

Critical Active Exploitation

“CitrixBleed” returns: NetScaler flaw exploited within 24 hours, leaking secrets from gateways that front plant and distribution remote access

Citrix patched CVE-2026-8451 on 30 June — an unauthenticated memory-overread in NetScaler ADC and Gateway appliances configured as a SAML identity provider — and researchers saw in-the-wild exploitation within 24 hours, aided by a same-day public proof-of-concept. Like the original CitrixBleed, it lets a remote attacker read appliance memory and harvest live session tokens, credentials and TLS keys. Food manufacturers and distributors often use NetScaler to publish remote access to MES, ERP, ordering and warehouse systems, so leaked secrets can hand an attacker authenticated entry toward production and logistics networks. Assume any internet-facing, unpatched NetScaler had its memory read after 1 July.

Active — exploited within 24h of disclosure; public PoC Citrix NetScaler ADC / Gateway (SAML IdP)
Patch available (Citrix, 30 Jun) Action: Patch NetScaler to the fixed build now, terminate all active sessions, and — assuming memory disclosure — rotate every session token, credential and TLS private key on the appliance; remove management interfaces from the internet and hunt for anomalous session reuse.
Suggested SLA: Patch, session kill & secret rotation — 24h (active exploitation on OT-bridging edge) Owner:

High Incident

Ransomware at Dairy Farmers of America processing plants leaks personal data

Dairy Farmers of America (DFA), one of the largest US dairy cooperatives, confirmed a ransomware attack that affected multiple manufacturing plants within its network, with a ransomware group taking credit. DFA has begun filing breach notifications — disclosing to Maine regulators that the personal information of several thousand people was exposed — and worked to restore affected operations. The case is a reminder that dairy and perishable processing are squarely in the ransomware crosshairs, where downtime threatens both food safety and highly time-sensitive product. Processors should verify IT/OT segmentation, confirm offline backups for plant systems, and prepare breach-notification and continuity plans before an incident, not during one.

Critical Active Exploitation

Actively-exploited Lantronix EDS5000 flaw hands attackers root on serial-to-IP bridges used in processing and cold-chain control

CISA says CVE-2025-67038, a command-injection flaw in Lantronix EDS5000 serial-to-IP device servers, is being exploited in the wild and added it to the KEV catalog on 23 June (advisory ICSA-26-069-02). An unauthenticated attacker can inject OS commands via the username parameter and run them as root, fully controlling a device whose job is bridging legacy serial gear — PLCs, meters, refrigeration and monitoring sensors — onto IP networks. Forescout showed such bugs can even manipulate sensor readings to hide dangerous conditions — a real concern where those readings govern cold-chain temperature or process safety in food production.

Active — honeypot exploitation observed (Forescout); targeted fingerprinting In CISA KEV — deadline 26 Jun 2026 (ICSA-26-069-02)
Vendor patch available (Lantronix) Action: Inventory Lantronix EDS5000 and similar serial-to-IP servers in processing and cold-chain control, apply the vendor fix, remove internet exposure, restrict management to a segmented OT network, and hunt for command injection via the username parameter and unexpected root processes.

Siemens urges immediate patching of RUGGEDCOM switches as a critical SINEC OS flaw (CVSS 9.8) draws a public exploit

CISA advisory ICSA-26-188-05 (published 7 July) flags a critical CVSS 9.8 vulnerability in Siemens SINEC OS before V4.0, the operating system on RUGGEDCOM industrial networking gear. A memory-corruption bug in a bundled third-party component lets a remote attacker compromise unpatched devices, and a public exploit has already been disclosed. Alongside Siemens SIMATIC controllers, RUGGEDCOM networking appears in larger food and beverage plants, sitting in the path that segments and moves control-system traffic. No confirmed in-the-wild ICS attacks are reported yet, but a public exploit plus a 9.8 score on this backbone gear warrants emergency patch prioritization.

Rockwell Automation released fixes for multiple vulnerabilities spanning its Logix controller family, RSLinx communications software and FactoryTalk suite — including a stack-based buffer overflow in RSLinx that could allow remote code execution. CISA now targets September 2026 for the final CIRCIA rule — 72-hour incident and 24-hour ransom-payment reporting ahead

CISA is expected to finalize the CIRCIA rule this fall, with reporting now targeted for around September 2026. Once effective, covered critical-infrastructure entities — food and agriculture among the 16 sectors — would have to report substantial cyber incidents within 72 hours and ransom payments within 24 hours to CISA. Given how often food and beverage firms face ransomware (see Threat Intelligence), the practical work is building an incident-classification and reporting playbook now, so a real event does not become a missed federal deadline. Firms should track the final rule text for exact covered-entity thresholds, and align it with Food and Ag-ISAC reporting practices.
