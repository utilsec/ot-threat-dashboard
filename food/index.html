Ransomware halts all US production at Coca-Cola’s Fairlife dairy

A ransomware attack forced Coca-Cola’s Fairlife dairy subsidiary to halt all US production on 16 July, one of the most consequential food and beverage cyber incidents of the year. Coca-Cola disclosed in an SEC filing that attackers reached systems including production-related infrastructure, prompting a precautionary shutdown across Fairlife’s US plants while Canadian operations continued. The company says there is no indication product safety or quality was affected and has engaged external experts and law enforcement. For food and ag operators the lesson echoes the 2021 JBS attack: perishable, just-in-time production means an IT intrusion becomes spoiled inventory, missed deliveries and revenue loss within hours, giving attackers unusual leverage. Whether ransomware crossed into OT or the stoppage was purely precautionary, the operational result — idle lines and disrupted supply — is the same.

High Incident — Nation-State

Iran-affiliated actors are disabling the PLC alarm and shutdown logic that guards food-safety interlocks

The updated federal advisory AA26-097A (22 July) confirms Iran-affiliated actors reaching internet-exposed PLCs — Rockwell, Siemens and Schneider — extracting project files and modifying or deleting control logic, and in some cases disabling shutdown and alarm functions so equipment can enter unsafe states without alerting operators. In food and beverage processing, that same class of controller enforces cook/pasteurization temperatures, CIP cycles and refrigeration interlocks. Defeated alarm logic is not just downtime — it is a food-safety exposure, which is why controller integrity belongs on the HACCP-adjacent risk list.

Food and Ag-ISAC: ransomware pressure on the sector keeps rising, led by Qilin, Akira and CL0P

Food and Ag-ISAC reporting documents a marked rise in ransomware against the sector, with Qilin, Akira and CL0P among the most active groups and CL0P notably over-indexing on food and agriculture relative to its overall activity. Most attacks are opportunistic — scanning for exposed systems, buying access from brokers, and phishing into just-in-time supply chains — rather than sector-specific targeting. The Fairlife halt fits the pattern: the sector’s perishable, time-critical output is what makes it a reliable pressure point.

Critical Active Exploitation — KEV

FortiSandbox RCE flaws added to CISA KEV — exploited appliances on the plant IT/OT boundary

CISA added two critical Fortinet flaws, CVE-2026-39808 and CVE-2026-25089 (both CVSS 9.1), to the KEV catalog on 16 July after in-the-wild exploitation. They are OS command-injection bugs in FortiSandbox / Cloud / PaaS allowing unauthenticated command execution via crafted HTTP requests. In food and beverage plants, Fortinet appliances often guard the line between enterprise IT and processing OT; a compromised inspection appliance is a foothold and a blind spot. CISA set a 19 July federal remediation deadline.

Critical Critical Advisory — Patch Tuesday

July ICS Patch Tuesday: a CVSS-10 Siemens flaw among criticals in gear used across F&B lines

July’s ICS Patch Tuesday brought fixes from Siemens, Schneider Electric and Rockwell — Siemens alone issued nine advisories including a CVSS-10 authentication-bypass in Opencenter X and criticals in SIMATIC S7-1500, Mendix and Desigo CC. SIMATIC controllers and building/refrigeration controls like Desigo CC are common across food and beverage processing and cold storage, so affected plants should triage these against their installed base. No public exploitation is reported yet.

USDA’s National Farm Security Action Plan puts ag cybersecurity and foreign-threat risk front and center

On 8 July the USDA released its National Farm Security Action Plan, tying agricultural cybersecurity to national-security concerns about foreign infiltration and potential sabotage of the food system. Alongside land-ownership and espionage measures, it signals rising federal expectations for cyber resilience across farming and food production. It is a strategy rather than a rule, but it points to where scrutiny — and eventually requirements — are heading for the sector.

Info Policy — Legislation

Farm and food cybersecurity legislation advances with risk assessments and crisis drills

The Farm and Food Cybersecurity Act would direct USDA, with CISA, to assess sector cyber threats every two years and run annual crisis-simulation exercises for food-related emergencies, while the broader Farm, Food, and National Security Act of 2026 folds cybersecurity protections for agricultural facilities into farm-bill priorities. For operators the direction of travel is clear: periodic risk assessment and exercised incident response are becoming baseline expectations.
