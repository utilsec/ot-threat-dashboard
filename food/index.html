Top Story

Ransomware shuts two Mackay Sugar mills at the start of crushing season — harvest disrupted for 1,300 growers

A ransomware attack on Mackay Sugar, Australia's second-largest sugar producer, halted milling and cane haulage at its Farleigh and Racecourse mills after striking on 10 June — at the very start of the crushing season. More than 1,300 family-owned cane farms had harvesting disrupted, and because the mills' cogeneration plant also feeds the regional grid, the outage rippled beyond the fence line. The Gentlemen ransomware group claimed the intrusion on 16 June and threatened to leak stolen data, a double-extortion pattern the crew has used against Asia-Pacific critical infrastructure since mid-2025. For food and agriculture the lesson is seasonal timing: attackers hit when a processor can least afford downtime, turning IT ransomware into lost harvest, spoiled throughput and idled growers.

No patch — segmentation, backups, seasonal IR planning Action: Segment processing OT from enterprise IT so ransomware cannot stop the line, keep offline tested backups, build seasonal incident-response and manual-operation plans around peak-throughput windows, and pre-arrange recovery support before the busy season.

High Incident

FortiBleed credential theft now feeds ransomware — the remote path into processing plants

SOCRadar reported on 2 July that FortiBleed — a sniffer that harvested 110M+ credentials from roughly 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls since mid-June — is now driving INC Ransom and Lynx intrusions. Food and beverage processors, cold-chain operators and distributors lean on Fortinet for remote access to plant networks, refrigeration and packaging controls, and vendor links. Because credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, no patch helps: an attacker signs in as a trusted engineer. Assume any exposed FortiGate touching processing or cold-chain OT is compromised and rotate now.

Food and Ag-ISAC: 72 active threat actors are working the farm-to-table supply chain

The Food and Ag-ISAC identified 72 active threat actors — from a pool of more than 330 monitored — consistently targeting food supply chains, spanning both nation-state groups and financially motivated criminals. Russia accounts for the largest share of observed activity, followed by China, with Lazarus Group, Moonstone Sleet and APT41 among the top-scoring adversaries. The most common tradecraft is living-off-the-land using legitimate tools, plus modified malware, long persistence and stealthy exfiltration. The message for the sector: this is deliberate, sophisticated targeting, not opportunistic noise, and defenses should assume patient, stealthy adversaries.

High Advisory

CISA flags CVSS-10 flaws in Gardyn IoT Hub — a warning shot for precision and indoor agriculture

On 2 July CISA published advisory ICSA-26-183-03 for Gardyn IoT Hub, with flaws scoring up to CVSS 10 that let unauthenticated attackers take full control of Gardyn-managed devices. An exposed privileged iothubowner key (CVE-2026-13768) lets an attacker enumerate the entire fleet, run arbitrary commands on connected devices and pivot into the owner's home network, while device logs sat in a publicly listable cloud container. Gardyn is consumer indoor-growing hardware, but CISA scoped it to the food-and-agriculture sector for a reason: as precision-ag and controlled-environment platforms proliferate, cloud-managed IoT with exposed admin keys becomes a real agricultural attack surface.

No confirmed in-the-wild exploitation as of this edition Not in CISA KEV

A June CISA advisory cycle covered vulnerabilities across Delta, Schneider Electric, Mitsubishi and other automation vendors whose controllers, drives and HMIs run food and beverage processing, packaging and bottling lines.
