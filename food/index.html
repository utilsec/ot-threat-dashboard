Top Story

Ransomware reaches fairlife’s production systems — Coca-Cola halts US dairy manufacturing

The Coca-Cola Company disclosed on 16 July that a third party gained unauthorised access to part of its fairlife dairy subsidiary’s environment, including its production-related systems, in a ransomware event. US production is temporarily suspended; Canadian operations are unaffected, and the company says product quality and safety are not affected. No group has claimed the attack, Coca-Cola has not said whether data was taken, and it states the full scope and impacts are not yet known. This is the food and beverage sector’s clearest recent reminder that a plant does not need its PLCs touched to stop: ransomware rarely speaks ICS protocols — it lands on the ERP, MES, virtualisation and identity infrastructure that scheduling, batching, pasteurisation records and quality release depend on, and a perishable-product line halts anyway.

Confirmed — company-acknowledged ransomware with production impact; no group attribution yet No CVE identified publicly — initial access vector not disclosed
Incident response ongoing — no patch applicable Action: Treat this as a tabletop you did not have to pay for. Confirm which IT systems your production genuinely cannot run without (ERP, MES, historian, batch scheduling, cold-chain monitoring, label/QA release), verify those dependencies are segmented and cannot be reached with a single set of compromised domain credentials, test that operators can run the process manually for a defined period, and check that OT backups are offline and restorable — not just replicated.

High Incident

Cyberattack on Japan’s cold-chain giant Nichirei ripples through KFC and major retailers

Frozen-food and cold-storage leader Nichirei disconnected its systems on 13 July after detecting unauthorised server access, halting refrigerated-warehouse operations and Nichirei Foods shipping. The fallout spread fast across the supply chain: KFC Japan’s 1,300-plus restaurants faced Original Recipe ingredient-delivery disruption, and Aeon supermarkets, TableMark and Kura Sushi were among others affected. Some impacted servers held personal data; Nichirei filed an initial breach report and began phased restoration on 17 July. It has not confirmed whether a known ransomware or extortion group was involved. For food and beverage operators the lesson is about concentration risk: cold-chain and third-party logistics are single points of failure whose outage cascades into every downstream brand that depends on them — often faster than any of those brands can react.

Critical Active Exploitation

SonicWall SMA 1000 zero-days exploited — the remote access into processing plants and cold chain

SonicWall confirmed active exploitation of two Secure Mobile Access 1000 flaws after investigating multiple cases. CVE-2026-15409 is a CVSS 10.0 unauthenticated server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface; CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2) lets an authenticated admin inject OS commands through the Appliance Management Console. CISA added both to the KEV catalog on 14 July with a 17 July federal deadline. For food and beverage the exposure follows the sector’s shape: processing and packaging sites are geographically dispersed, run lean overnight, and depend on remote support from OEMs for filling, packaging and refrigeration equipment. Cold-chain monitoring in particular is often reached remotely by a third party. When the appliance carrying that access is what gets exploited, the intrusion arrives as a legitimate maintenance session.

Active — SonicWall confirms exploitation in multiple cases In CISA KEV 14 Jul — FCEB due 17 Jul
Patch available — 12.4.3-03453 / 12.5.0-02835 hotfix Action: Patch SMA 1000 to the fixed hotfix now. Then check SonicWall’s indicators: /__api__/login or /__api__/logout requests returning HTTP 200 in extraweb_access.log, /wsproxy requests with suspicious host parameters (HTTP 101), path-traversal hotfix rollbacks in ctrl-service.log, and rogue routes in /var/lib/unit/conf.json. On any hit, re-image and rotate all credentials and TOTP tokens. Separately, list which OEMs hold standing access to filling, packaging and refrigeration systems.
Suggested SLA: Patch & IoC sweep — 24h (confirmed exploitation on the OEM access path into plants) Owner:

High Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converter flaw exploited in the wild — the boxes bridging older process gear

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, one of the flaws disclosed in April via the BRIDGE:BREAK research project. In food and beverage plants these devices are how legacy equipment reached the network without being replaced: weigh stations, older fillers and cappers, batch scales, refrigeration and pasteuriser controllers, and CIP skids. They were usually installed by an integrator during a retrofit, never entered in an asset register, and never patched, because the machine behind them still runs. That is what makes the category attractive — unmanaged, unpatched, inside the trusted network, with a direct path to a process asset. Exploitation is confirmed and follows an explicit OT threat warning; for most processors the honest first step is finding them.

Active — exploitation confirmed in the wild Lantronix serial-to-IP converters (BRIDGE:BREAK)
Vendor firmware available — see Lantronix advisory Action: Scan plant networks for serial-to-IP converters rather than trusting documentation — look for the management ports across production, packaging and refrigeration areas. Remove internet exposure first, then segment, update firmware at the next sanitation or changeover window, and replace default credentials. Assign each an owner so it joins the patch cycle.
Suggested SLA: Discovery & exposure remediation — 72h; firmware — next window Owner:

High Threat Intel — Context

Context: Food and Ag-ISAC’s 2025 data still frames the sector’s ransomware exposure

Foundational context for the incidents above rather than fresh news: the Food and Ag-ISAC’s ransomware report, published 12 February 2026, recorded 265 attacks against food and agriculture during 2025 — about 4.2% of the 6,377 incidents it tracked across all sectors, itself an 82% rise on the 3,508 tracked in 2024. Five groups — Qilin, Akira, CL0P, Play and Lynx — accounted for close to half of the sector’s recorded incidents. A follow-on sector threat report in March identified 72 active threat actors targeting food supply chains, with observed adversary activity attributed predominantly to Russia (about 59%) and China (about 25%). The numbers are a year old and should be read as baseline, not as this week’s picture.

Baseline reporting — 2025 data, published Feb/Mar 2026 Food and Ag-ISAC (Qilin, Akira, CL0P, Play, Lynx)
No patch — sector threat context Action: Use the named groups to sanity-check your assumptions rather than as a target list: Qilin, Akira and CL0P favour internet-facing access, IT service infrastructure and mass-exploited file-transfer platforms respectively. Confirm you have no unmanaged file-transfer appliances, that your edge is inventoried, and that a ransomware event in IT would not silently take production with it.

Critical Vendor Advisories

July ICS Patch Tuesday: critical Rockwell Logix DoS flaws and a Siemens CVSS 10 auth bypass

The July 2026 cycle carries real weight for food and beverage plants. Rockwell Automation published 12 advisories including three critical denial-of-service flaws in CompactLogix, ControlLogix, Compact GuardLogix and GuardLogix controllers that can force a major non-recoverable fault — a controller that stops and does not restart on its own. Allen-Bradley Logix hardware is widespread across filling, packaging and process lines, so this is a throughput and product-loss question, not a data one; on a line running perishable product, an unplanned controller stop has a cost that starts accruing immediately. Siemens published nine advisories, six critical, including a CVSS 10 authentication bypass in Opencenter X and fixes for Simatic S7-1500. Schneider fixed a high-severity code-execution flaw in its IGSS SCADA product. VDE CERT added five more covering Codesys, Wago, Murrelektronik and Mettler Toledo — the last of which is common in weighing and inspection.

No known exploitation reported for these advisories Siemens / Schneider / Rockwell / VDE CERT

Schedule against sanitation or changeover windows rather than deferring. Check Mettler Toledo equipment against the VDE CERT advisories.

High Vulnerability

BeyondTrust patches critical auth-bypass flaws in Remote Support and Privileged Remote Access

BeyondTrust disclosed two authentication-bypass vulnerabilities on 7 July affecting Remote Support (RS) and Privileged Remote Access (PRA). CVE-2026-40138 (CVSS 9.2) stems from improper validation of authentication data and could let a network-positioned attacker bypass access controls to reach the appliance, including elevated accounts; CVE-2026-40139 involves improper processing of RS authentication requests. Exploitation of both requires a specific authentication configuration to be enabled, narrowing the exposed population, and neither is under active exploitation — no public proof-of-concept exists. Cloud customers were patched on 21 April; self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier needs action. Alongside the SonicWall item, the theme is consistent: the tooling that brokers third-party access into plants is under sustained research pressure.

No known exploitation; no public PoC as of this edition Not in CISA KEV
Patched — RS/PRA 25.3.3+ or April rollup (cloud patched 21 Apr) Action: Determine whether you run self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier with the affected configuration enabled, and patch. Then audit which equipment vendors, refrigeration contractors and integrators hold standing access into plant networks, and whether those sessions are time-bounded, approved per use and recorded.

Regulation & Policy
Info Regulation

Australia’s CISC unveils Enhanced CIRMP Rules covering AI, legacy systems, supply chain and insider risk

Australia’s Cyber and Infrastructure Security Centre published Enhanced CIRMP Rules addressing AI, legacy systems, supply-chain and insider risks across critical infrastructure. Food and grocery sit within Australia’s SOCI critical-infrastructure regime, so processors and retailers with Australian operations have direct exposure here. The broader signal matters to everyone else: legacy systems and supply-chain risk are moving from acknowledged facts of life into written obligations. For a sector where a decades-old retort, filler or blast freezer is often described as unpatchable and left there, an explicit regulatory expectation changes what “accepted risk” has to look like on paper — and those expectations usually reach customer questionnaires long before they reach other jurisdictions’ statute books.

Regulatory development — no threat activity CISC Enhanced CIRMP Rules (Australia / SOCI — incl. food & grocery)
No patch — compliance obligation Action: If you operate in Australia, map the Enhanced CIRMP Rules against your risk-management programme, focusing on legacy process equipment and insider risk. If you do not, treat it as a leading indicator: inventory the plant assets that cannot be patched and write down the compensating controls now — that evidence is what customers and auditors increasingly ask to see.

Info Guidance

NIST publishes an OT backup quick-start guide aimed at cutting recovery time

NIST released SP 1339, an OT Backup Quick Start Guide intended to improve industrial cyber resilience and accelerate incident recovery. It lands usefully next to this month’s Rockwell advisories describing faults a controller will not recover from unaided (see Critical Advisories). In a processing plant, what determines how fast you restart is PLC logic, HMI projects, recipe and setpoint data, batch records, drive parameters and the engineering-workstation image needed to push them back — not the file server. Those artefacts are frequently stale, undocumented, or sitting on the same domain as the systems they are supposed to restore. In food specifically there is a second clock running: extended downtime is not just lost output, it is product at risk and, potentially, a traceability gap.

Guidance publication — no threat activity NIST SP 1339
No patch — process and resilience guidance Action: Use SP 1339 as a checklist against a concrete scenario: if a controller on your highest-throughput line faulted tomorrow, how long to restore logic and recipes and get back to rate — and what happens to product in process meanwhile? Confirm PLC logic, HMI projects and recipe data are backed up, isolated from the production domain, and restored in a test at least once. Record the measured restore time.
