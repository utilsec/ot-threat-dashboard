Top Story

Fairlife halts all US production after ransomware hits Coca-Cola’s dairy subsidiary

Coca-Cola’s roughly $4 billion dairy brand Fairlife suspended all US production after a ransomware attack. An SEC filing and a Coca-Cola statement (16 July) confirmed a third party gained unauthorised access to internal systems “including those related to production”; networks were isolated and operations partly shut down, with no restart date given. Coca-Cola says product quality and safety were not impacted, and Canadian production is unaffected. Whether OT was directly touched remains unconfirmed — the shutdown may reflect isolation at the IT/scheduling layer. For food and beverage this is the sector’s nightmare in miniature: perishable dairy, cold-chain and packaging lines idled nationally, with the public-pressure dynamic that makes food producers prime ransom targets — a smaller echo of the JBS meatpacking shutdown in 2021.

Confirmed — SEC filing / Coca-Cola statement acknowledge ransomware with production impact; no group attribution yet No CVE identified publicly — initial access vector not disclosed

High Incident

Cyberattack on Japan’s cold-chain giant Nichirei ripples through KFC and major retailers

Frozen-food and cold-storage leader Nichirei disconnected its systems on 13 July after detecting unauthorised server access, halting refrigerated-warehouse operations and Nichirei Foods shipping. The fallout spread fast across the supply chain: KFC Japan’s 1,300-plus restaurants faced Original Recipe ingredient-delivery disruption, and Aeon supermarkets, TableMark and Kura Sushi were among others affected. Some impacted servers held personal data; Nichirei filed an initial breach report and began phased restoration on 17 July. It has not confirmed whether a known ransomware or extortion group was involved. For food and beverage operators the lesson is about concentration risk: cold-chain and third-party logistics are single points of failure whose outage cascades into every downstream brand that depends on them — often faster than any of those brands can react.

Confirmed — systems taken offline; cold-chain & shipping disrupted Company disclosure — Nichirei, 13 Jul 2026
No patch — incident response & supply-chain concentration Action: Map your dependence on cold-chain and 3PL partners the way you would a single-site risk: identify which of your products cannot ship or stay in spec if one logistics provider goes dark, and for how long. Confirm you have manual fallback for temperature monitoring and dispatch, that partner data exchanges cannot pivot into your OT, and that contracts include incident-notification and recovery-time commitments.

High Nation-State — Advisory

FSB Center 16 is harvesting router configs over SNMP — the plant edge nobody audits

A joint advisory (AA26-194A, 13 July) from NSA, CISA, the FBI and partner agencies across roughly a dozen countries details a long-running Russian FSB Center 16 campaign that scans the internet for routers with weak SNMP community strings and copies their configurations to attacker infrastructure, in some cases also abusing the long-known Cisco Smart Install weakness (CVE-2018-0171). No zero-day is needed — a default community string is enough. Reframed for food and agriculture: the exposed gear is the processing-plant edge router and the remote-access boxes reaching refrigeration, dosing and packaging controls. A harvested config is a map of the plant network — VLANs, ACLs, management IPs and the paths into OT — handed to an adversary who has not yet had to break anything.

Confirmed campaign — multi-agency attribution to FSB Center 16 Cisco Smart Install weakness — CVE-2018-0171
Config hardening — disable Smart Install; SNMPv3 authPriv Action: Treat plant and site edge routers as OT assets. Disable Cisco Smart Install where it is not needed, move off SNMPv1/v2c to SNMPv3 authPriv with strong credentials, block UDP 69 (TFTP) and TCP 4786 at the perimeter, and alert on Cisco Config-Copy MIB OIDs. Confirm no management interface on a refrigeration, dosing or packaging network is reachable from the internet.
Suggested SLA: Edge-router hygiene review & SNMP hardening — 14 days Owner:

Critical Vendor Advisories

July ICS Patch Tuesday: critical Siemens fixes, incl. a CVSS 9.8 SINEC OS flaw on RUGGEDCOM switches

The July 2026 cycle carries real weight for food and beverage plants. Siemens led with critical fixes including a CVSS 9.8 flaw in SINEC OS on RUGGEDCOM industrial switches, plus updates for SIMATIC S7-1500 controllers. Both are workhorse components in F&B: SIMATIC controllers run filling, packaging and process lines, and RUGGEDCOM switches carry the plant network those lines sit on — so a switch-OS flaw is not a peripheral IT issue but a path into the fabric connecting control gear. Schneider and Rockwell also shipped advisories in the same cycle. None of these are reported as exploited, which keeps them firmly in patch-and-monitor territory rather than fire-drill — but on a line running perishable product, the calendar matters: schedule fixes against sanitation or changeover windows before an attacker makes the choice for you.

No known exploitation reported for these advisories Siemens / Schneider / Rockwell
Patches & mitigations available Action: Match the Siemens advisories to your inventory: flag every RUGGEDCOM switch running SINEC OS and every SIMATIC S7-1500 on a production or packaging line, and rank by product risk — temperature-critical or perishable lines set the priority. Confirm no switch management interface is reachable from IT or the internet, then schedule firmware against the next sanitation or changeover window rather than deferring indefinitely.

High ICS Advisory

CISA advisory for the Gardyn IoT Hub puts a spotlight on ag-IoT and indoor-farming exposure

CISA published an ICS advisory (2 July) for the Gardyn IoT Hub, the controller behind Gardyn’s connected indoor-farming systems. On its own it is a niche consumer/commercial grow platform, not a plant-floor controller — but it is a clean illustration of where agriculture is heading: precision-ag and indoor-farming rely on cloud-managed IoT hubs, cameras, sensors and dosing controls that ship with the connectivity of any smart device and, too often, the patch discipline of none. The same class of exposure sits under connected tractors and implements, environmental controllers and automated greenhouses. For food and ag operators the value here is directional — treat ag-IoT as part of the attack surface, inventory what is connected, and do not assume “farm equipment” is out of scope for cyber risk.

No known exploitation reported Gardyn IoT Hub — CISA ICS advisory
Vendor update / mitigations — see CISA advisory Action: If you run Gardyn or comparable connected grow systems, apply the vendor update and remove any internet exposure. More broadly, inventory your ag-IoT and precision-ag estate — environmental controllers, cameras, dosing/irrigation controls, connected implements — and confirm each has an owner, is segmented from business systems, and is in a patch cycle rather than forgotten after install.

Regulation & Policy
Info Policy — Forward-Looking

Food-and-ag cyber policy gathers momentum: two bills plus USDA’s Farm Security Action Plan

Legislative and policy attention to food and agriculture cyber risk is building, and high-profile food-sector ransomware keeps renewing it. The Farm and Food Cybersecurity Act — reintroduced to protect the food supply chain — would mandate regular USDA cyber risk assessments of the food and agriculture sector, while the Cybersecurity in Agriculture Act would authorise USDA Regional Agriculture Cybersecurity Centers to build capacity closer to producers. Both sit alongside USDA’s National Farm Security Action Plan. None of this is a dated, in-force rule yet — treat it as forward-looking direction rather than a compliance deadline. But the trajectory is clear: food and agriculture is being treated as critical infrastructure worth legislating for, and the expectations these efforts describe tend to surface in customer and insurer questionnaires well before they become statute.
