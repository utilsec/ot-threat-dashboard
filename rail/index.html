Top Story

A radio flaw in the brake link of US freight trains still has no fielded fix

CISA advisory ICSA-25-191-10 (first published 10 July 2025, last revised as “Update C” on 18 September 2025) documents CVE-2025-1727, a weak-authentication flaw (CWE-1390) in the End-of-Train / Head-of-Train remote linking protocol that has carried US freight braking telemetry for decades. The protocol authenticates packets with only a BCH checksum and no cryptography, so an attacker with a cheap software-defined radio — researchers put the cost under $500 — can forge the RF packets that pass brake commands between the head-of-train unit and the end-of-train device, potentially triggering an unintended brake application and a sudden stop. CVSS v3.1 is 8.1 and CVSS v4 is 7.2. Affected gear spans Wabtec TrainLink NG/NG3/NG4/NG5, Siemens Trainguard EOT/HOT and DPS Electronics ETD/HTD units. The AAR is moving the fleet to a new IEEE 802.16t-based secure protocol, but replacement is not expected to be fielded before 2027 — so today the mitigation is RF/network isolation and monitoring, not a patch. No public exploitation has been reported.

No confirmed exploitation reported — but a documented, low-cost RF attack on a safety function CVE-2025-1727 — not in CISA KEV; CISA ICSA-25-191-10 (Update C)
No fix — replacement protocol not fielded before 2027 Action: Treat the EoT/HoT RF link as an untrusted channel. Engage the AAR/vendor program on the 802.16t migration timeline for your fleet, and in the interim prioritise RF-spectrum monitoring for anomalous EoT/HoT traffic, operational detection of unexplained brake events, and isolating EoT/HoT provisioning and management interfaces from IT and remote access.

High Incident — Nation-State (context)

Ukraine’s state railway kept trains moving through a major cyberattack — but its ticketing went dark

On 24 March 2025 Ukrzaliznytsia, Ukraine’s state railway, was hit by what it called a large-scale, targeted cyberattack that knocked out online ticket sales, the mobile app and other customer-facing IT, forcing long queues at station windows. Crucially, the operator stressed that train movement, dispatching and signalling were unaffected — services ran on manual and backup processes throughout. Ukrainian officials pointed to Russia and called it an act of terrorism; no group publicly claimed it. It is a clean illustration of the pattern rail keeps seeing: attackers reach the enterprise and booking layer, disrupt the passenger experience and generate headlines, while the safety-critical OT that actually moves trains stays walled off — provided that segmentation actually holds.

Medium Incident — DDoS

Deutsche Bahn’s booking systems knocked offline by a DDoS — trains ran on

On 17–18 February 2026 a distributed denial-of-service attack disrupted Deutsche Bahn’s customer-facing systems, taking bahn.de and the DB Navigator app offline and degrading passenger information and online booking. Train control and signalling were not affected, and systems were largely stable again by that evening. No group claimed responsibility, though pro-Russian hacktivist crews such as NoName057(16) and Killnet have repeatedly hit European transport portals with the same low-sophistication technique. DDoS against a rail portal is disruptive and reputational rather than safety-impacting — but it ties up responders, erodes public trust, and is cheap enough to be used as harassment or as cover for something else.

Confirmed DDoS — customer IT (bahn.de, DB Navigator); no OT/signalling impact No CVE — volumetric denial-of-service
Mitigated same-day — no patch applicable Action: Confirm your public web, journey-planner and ticketing endpoints sit behind DDoS protection / CDN scrubbing, and that failover to station-based and offline processes is rehearsed. High Threat Intel — Research

The train-brake radio flaw took 20 years and a DEF CON talk to be taken seriously

The EoT/HoT weakness did not arrive with CISA’s 2025 advisory — it arrived with the flaw finally being acted on. Independent researchers flagged the unauthenticated brake-link protocol as far back as 2012, and Eric Reuter demonstrated the software-defined-radio attack publicly at DEF CON in 2018; for years the response was that the affected devices were end-of-life and the risk theoretical. SecurityWeek frames the episode as a case study in how OT-safety disclosures stall when the fix means re-equipping an entire national fleet. For rail security teams the lesson is procedural as much as technical: a documented, low-cost RF attack on a safety function sat unmitigated for a decade because ownership of the fix was diffuse.

Landscape Threat Intel — Landscape

Rail’s core problem is still knowing what OT it has — and TS 50701 makes that explicit

Vendor and standards analysis this year keeps returning to the same foundation: rail operators often cannot fully inventory the signalling, interlocking, on-board and trackside OT they run, much of it decades old and bolted onto IP over time. CENELEC’s CLC/TS 50701 railway-cybersecurity specification — and the incoming IEC 63452 international standard built on the same lines — put asset identification and a risk-based zones-and-conduits model (aligned with IEC 62443) at the centre of rail cyber programmes, precisely because you cannot protect or segment what you cannot see. It is landscape rather than incident, but it is the groundwork the EoT saga, the segmentation lessons from Ukrzaliznytsia, and the TSA/EU regulatory push all depend on.

Landscape / standards — not an incident No CVE — asset-visibility & standards theme
No patch — programme maturity Action: Prioritise an OT asset inventory across signalling, interlocking, rolling-stock and trackside networks, then map zones and conduits per TS 50701 / IEC 62443. Use it to find the internet- and IT-reachable OT and the legacy serial/RF links first.

Critical Advisory — Active Exploitation

Serial-to-IP converter flaw under active attack — the exact legacy bridge rail runs everywhere

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, disclosed in April via the BRIDGE:BREAK research into serial device servers and now confirmed in in-the-wild attacks. These converters — and comparable Digi and Moxa terminal servers — are the fixture that bolts legacy serial field equipment onto IP networks, and rail is full of them: interlocking, level-crossing, SCADA-telemetry and trackside links that were never designed for IP exposure. They typically sit at the OT edge with little monitoring, so an exploited converter is a quiet foothold ahead of any signalling controller. Not a rail-specific advisory, but a precise fit for rail’s legacy-serial estate.

Active — confirmed in-the-wild exploitation CVE-2025-67038 — BRIDGE:BREAK disclosure; verify KEV status against CISA
Vendor guidance / firmware available Action: Inventory Lantronix, Digi and Moxa serial-to-IP converters and terminal servers across signalling, telemetry and trackside sites; apply vendor fixes and pull them off any internet-reachable path. Where they must stay reachable, front them with an access-controlled jump host and add logging — these boxes rarely have either.

Info Regulation — TSA

US rail cyber still runs on TSA security directives — with a permanent rule proposed but not final

US freight and higher-risk passenger/transit railroads remain bound by TSA’s security directives (the 1580/1582 series), which require reporting cyber incidents to CISA, naming a cybersecurity coordinator, performing an annual vulnerability assessment, and implementing a TSA-approved cybersecurity plan built on IT/OT network segmentation, access control, continuous monitoring and timely patching. These directives are renewed and updated roughly annually. In November 2024 TSA published a proposed rule, “Enhancing Surface Cyber Risk Management,” to make cyber risk-management programmes permanent and mandatory for higher-risk pipeline, freight rail, passenger rail and bus operators; as of this edition that rule is still at the proposed stage, so the directives remain the binding requirement.

Regulatory — directives in force; permanent rule proposed No CVE — TSA surface-transportation cyber policy
Compliance item — no patch applicable Action: Confirm your TSA directive obligations are current — incident reporting to CISA, a named cybersecurity coordinator, the annual assessment and the approved implementation plan — and watch the ‘Enhancing Surface Cyber Risk Management’ rulemaking, which would raise the bar to a formal cyber risk-management programme. Non-US operators can use the directive requirements as a practical baseline.

Info Regulation — Preparedness

Cyber Europe 2026 war-gamed a cross-border rail-and-port cyber crisis

On 10–11 June 2026, ENISA ran Cyber Europe 2026, its large-scale exercise, around a scenario in which coordinated attacks hit both rail and maritime infrastructure — compromising port logistics and navigation while interference to railway networks stopped cross-border train services and delayed thousands of passenger and freight movements, with ransomware against transport authorities and ticketing and stolen data weaponised for disinformation. More than 5,000 participants across national agencies, EU institutions and industry coordinated the response. It is an exercise, not an incident, but the scenario is a fair reading of where rail risk is heading — cross-border, multi-modal, and blending OT disruption with information operations — and a prompt to test your own cross-operator and cross-border coordination.
