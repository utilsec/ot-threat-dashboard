Manufacturing is the ransomware bullseye — about 62% of industrial victims in Q1 2026, and an OT halt can hit within hours

Manufacturing has become the ransomware bullseye. Industrial-ransomware tracking put manufacturing at roughly 62% of all industrial victims in the first quarter of 2026 — hundreds of incidents in a single quarter — and Dragos' 2026 report likewise found manufacturing absorbing about two-thirds of OT-relevant ransomware, with dwell times measured in weeks. What makes the sector so exposed is that a crew only has to reach the seam between IT and the plant: once ransomware crosses into OT, production can halt within hours, and a stopped line bleeds revenue immediately. The groups are numerous and rotating, and increasingly reach in through stolen credentials and engineering workstations rather than exotic exploits. The defensible posture is boringly effective — segment IT from OT, lock down remote access, and keep tested, isolated backups you can actually restore.

Doppel: manufacturing attacks are shifting toward identity — leaked credentials and vishing lead the way in

Security firm Doppel warned in mid-June that cyber threats against manufacturers are increasingly identity-driven, with leaked credentials and voice-phishing (vishing) campaigns emerging as primary entry vectors. The pattern matters because it matches how ransomware crews actually operate: rather than burning exploits, they authenticate with valid credentials and then work toward the plant floor, where an engineering workstation or remote-access account can bridge into OT. For manufacturers the implication is that identity hygiene — MFA everywhere, aggressive credential rotation, help-desk verification against vishing, and monitoring for anomalous logins — is now front-line OT defense, not just IT housekeeping.

Dragos 2026: manufacturing takes about two-thirds of OT ransomware, and new groups target engineering workstations

Dragos' 2026 report frames manufacturing as the most-targeted OT sector, absorbing roughly two-thirds of ransomware impacting industrial organizations, against a backdrop of a growing number of ransomware groups and dwell times measured in weeks. It also details newly tracked adversaries — including AZURITE, focused on OT engineering workstations to exfiltrate operational data and maintain long-term access — alongside a broader shift from data theft toward operational disruption. For plant defenders the takeaways are concrete: treat the engineering workstation as a crown-jewel asset, watch the IT/OT boundary for lateral movement, and assume adversaries may already be resident when planning detection and recovery.

FortiBleed: ~430,000 Fortinet firewalls swept for 110M+ credentials, now ransomware-linked — the remote-access path into engineering networks

CISA is urging Fortinet operators to act after researchers assessed the FortiBleed campaign swept roughly 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways for more than 110 million credentials, now directly linked to active INC Ransom and Lynx ransomware. For manufacturers the danger is the remote-access layer: these appliances often front vendor and engineer access into plant and engineering networks, and because credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, there is no patch to apply. Valid stolen credentials are exactly how ransomware crews get in, so treat any exposed device as compromised and rotate now.
