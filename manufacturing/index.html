Top Story

Ransomware halt pushes a German auto and aerospace parts maker into insolvency

A German precision-components supplier to the automotive and aerospace sectors has filed for insolvency after a ransomware attack encrypted both its administrative systems and the industrial software coordinating its production robots, silencing a three-shift plant within hours. Recovery ran to six weeks because backups had never been tested against the malware strain and network segments had to be rebuilt from scratch — wiping out roughly 40% of projected annual revenue. The firm declined to pay, but the downtime proved fatal: banks pulled credit and contracts moved to competitors. For plant managers the lesson is blunt — an IT-origin ransomware event that reaches OT can end a business even without ICS-tailored malware. Tested offline controller backups, real network segmentation and validated restore drills are existential, not optional. It lands amid separate confirmed intrusions at Nidec and Indra.

Review who — staff or third parties — can reach production remotely, and whether that access is brokered, logged and time-bounded.

First in-the-wild exploitation of a PTC Windchill flaw — the PLM system holding your product data

CISA added CVE-2026-12569, a remote code execution flaw in PTC Windchill, to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog after the first observed exploitation in the wild. Windchill is PLM software: it holds CAD models, bills of materials, engineering change orders, supplier drawings and the release state of designs. That makes it an unusually rich target and an awkward one to classify — it is not an OT system, but it sits at the junction where engineering intent becomes manufacturing instruction, and it typically integrates with MES and ERP on one side and engineering workstations on the other. The realistic risk for most manufacturers is intellectual-property theft and supply-chain reconnaissance rather than direct process manipulation, but a foothold in PLM is a foothold with excellent visibility into how the plant works.

Critical Active Exploitation

SonicWall SMA 1000 zero-days exploited — the appliances your OEMs and integrators dial through

SonicWall confirmed active exploitation of two Secure Mobile Access 1000 flaws after investigating multiple cases. CVE-2026-15409 is a CVSS 10.0 unauthenticated server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface; CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2) allows an authenticated administrator to inject OS commands via the Appliance Management Console. CISA added both to the KEV catalog on 14 July with a 17 July federal deadline. Manufacturers should read this as a supply-chain access problem rather than a firewall problem. Machine builders, OEMs, robotics vendors and system integrators typically hold standing remote access into cells and lines for diagnostics and warranty support, and that access is frequently brokered through exactly this class of appliance. A compromise there does not trip anomaly detection — it looks like the vendor logging in to check a fault, which is what the vendor does.

Active — SonicWall confirms exploitation in multiple cases In CISA KEV 14 Jul — FCEB due 17 Jul
Patch available — 12.4.3-03453 / 12.5.0-02835 hotfix Action: Patch to the fixed hotfix build, then run SonicWall’s forensic checks: /__api__/login or /__api__/logout requests returning HTTP 200 in extraweb_access.log, /wsproxy requests with suspicious host parameters (HTTP 101), path-traversal hotfix rollbacks in ctrl-service.log, and rogue routes in /var/lib/unit/conf.json. On any hit, re-image and rotate all credentials and TOTP tokens. Separately, enumerate which OEMs hold standing access into which cells.

High Threat Intel

Manufacturing attacks shift toward identity: credential leaks and vishing surge, Doppel reports

Doppel reports that cyber threats against manufacturing are moving toward identity-driven attacks, with credential leaks and voice phishing both rising. The shift is worth taking seriously because it routes around most of what manufacturers have invested in. Segmentation, firewalls and OT monitoring are built to stop an intruder crossing a boundary; none of them fire when someone signs in with working credentials obtained from a leak or talks a service desk into a password reset. Manufacturing is a soft target for the vishing variant specifically — multi-site operations, shift-based staffing, heavy contractor and agency presence, and a service desk conditioned to restore access fast because downtime is measured in units per hour. Treat identity as a control surface with the same seriousness as the network boundary.

Active trend — credential leaks and vishing against manufacturing Doppel research
No patch — identity controls & service-desk process Action: Test your service desk against a vishing scenario involving an urgent production outage — that pretext works because it is usually true. Enforce phishing-resistant MFA on anything reaching engineering workstations or OT, monitor for leaked credentials tied to your domains and supplier domains, and define a verification standard for password resets that does not bend under production pressure.

High Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converter flaw exploited in the wild — the retrofit boxes on legacy machines

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, disclosed in April through the BRIDGE:BREAK research project. On a plant floor these devices are how a 20-year-old press, CNC, test rig or weigh station got a network connection without being replaced — installed during a retrofit, commissioned by an integrator, and then left alone precisely because the machine behind it still works. That combination (unmanaged, unpatched, inside the trusted network, with a clear path to a production asset) is what makes the category attractive. Exploitation is confirmed and follows an explicit OT threat warning. For most manufacturers the first task is discovery: these converters rarely appear in the asset register because nobody procured them as IT equipment.

Active — exploitation confirmed in the wild Lantronix serial-to-IP converters (BRIDGE:BREAK)

Remove any internet exposure immediately, segment the rest, update firmware at the next changeover, and replace default credentials. Give each device an owner so it enters the patch cycle instead of disappearing again.

July’s ICS Patch Tuesday is a bad one for plant floors. Rockwell Automation published 12 advisories, including three critical denial-of-service holes in CompactLogix, ControlLogix, Compact GuardLogix and GuardLogix controllers that can be exploited to cause a major non-recoverable fault — a controller that stops and stays stopped until someone intervenes. For discrete and process manufacturers the exposure is unusually direct: these are not adjacent systems, they are the controllers running the line.

CISA’s 14 July batch: Rockwell 1715-AENTR debug port and two ABB advisories

CISA issued four ICS advisories on 14 July (ICSA-26-195-01 through -04), released alongside the vendors’ own Patch Tuesday advisories — three from ABB and one from Rockwell. The Rockwell entry covers the 1715-AENTR EtherNet/IP adapter, where a network-accessible debug port fails to enforce proper privilege controls, allowing unauthenticated remote access to intrusive command-line functions. The ABB advisories address Advant Master Online Builder (an incorrect Online Builder version included on installation media) and Ability Edgenius, where a Linux kernel privilege-escalation issue could let a locally authenticated user or a compromised container workload obtain root. Check each advisory for exact affected versions.

BeyondTrust patches critical auth-bypass flaws in Remote Support and Privileged Remote Access

BeyondTrust disclosed two authentication-bypass vulnerabilities on 7 July in Remote Support (RS) and Privileged Remote Access (PRA). CVE-2026-40138 (CVSS 9.2) stems from improper validation of authentication data and could allow a network-positioned attacker to bypass access controls and reach the appliance, including elevated accounts; CVE-2026-40139 involves improper processing of RS authentication requests. Both depend on a specific authentication configuration being enabled, which limits the exposed population, and neither has been observed under active exploitation — no public proof-of-concept exists. Cloud customers were patched on 21 April; self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier requires action. Read it next to the SonicWall item: privileged remote access is how machine builders and integrators reach your lines, and it is under sustained research attention.

Info Guidance

NIST publishes an OT backup quick-start guide focused on cutting recovery time

NIST released SP 1339, an OT Backup Quick Start Guide aimed at improving industrial cyber resilience and accelerating incident recovery. For manufacturers the framing lands at a useful moment, given this month’s Rockwell advisories describing faults a controller does not recover from on its own (see top story). The artefacts that actually determine how fast a line restarts are PLC logic, HMI projects, robot programs, recipe and setpoint data, drive parameters and the engineering-workstation image needed to push any of it back — not the file server. Those are the things most often stale, undocumented, or stored on the same domain as the systems they would restore. The guide is short-format and pitched at establishing a workable baseline rather than a programme.

Guidance publication — no threat activity NIST SP 1339
No patch — process and resilience guidance Action: Use SP 1339 as a checklist against a real scenario: if a ControlLogix controller on your highest-value line took a non-recoverable fault tomorrow, how long to restore logic and get back to rate? Confirm PLC logic, HMI projects, robot programs and recipes are backed up, held offline or otherwise isolated from the production domain, and restored in a test at least once. Record the measured time.

Info Regulation

Australia’s CISC unveils Enhanced CIRMP Rules covering AI, legacy systems, supply chain and insider risk

Australia’s Cyber and Infrastructure Security Centre published Enhanced CIRMP Rules addressing AI, legacy systems, supply-chain and insider risks across critical infrastructure. Manufacturers with Australian operations or customers should read the detail, but the wider relevance is the direction: legacy systems and supply-chain risk are moving from acknowledged realities into explicit, written obligations. That is a meaningful shift for a sector whose standard answer on a 20-year-old machine has been that it still runs and cannot be touched. Where regulators name legacy assets and insider risk directly, “we accepted it” starts requiring an artefact rather than a conversation — and the same expectations tend to migrate into customer security questionnaires well before they migrate into other jurisdictions’ law.

Regulatory development — no threat activity CISC Enhanced CIRMP Rules (Australia / SOCI)
No patch — compliance obligation Action: If you operate in Australia, map the Enhanced CIRMP Rules against your existing risk-management programme with particular attention to legacy assets and insider risk. If you do not, use it as a forward indicator: inventory legacy systems that cannot be patched and document the compensating controls now, because that evidence is increasingly what customers and regulators ask for.
