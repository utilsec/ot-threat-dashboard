PTC Windchill/FlexPLM RCE (CVSS 9.3) added to CISA's KEV after web-shell attacks — a strike at the plant's engineering brain

CISA added CVE-2026-12569, a critical unauthenticated remote-code-execution flaw in PTC's Windchill and FlexPLM software, to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog after confirming attackers are dropping JSP web shells on unpatched instances — the first PTC product ever to make the KEV list. For manufacturers this strikes the heart of the enterprise: Windchill is the product-lifecycle and product-data backbone across automotive, aerospace, defense and heavy industry, holding CAD, BOMs, process plans and supplier data and feeding MES and ERP. A web shell there means silent command execution and theft of the crown-jewel engineering data that defines what the shop floor builds. PTC has patched and CISA set a 28 June federal deadline; hunt logs for POSTs to /Windchill/login/ ending in sixteen hex characters.

High Incident

FortiBleed credential theft now feeds ransomware — the remote path into plant networks

SOCRadar reported on 2 July that FortiBleed — a sniffer that harvested 110M+ credentials from some 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls since mid-June — is now driving INC Ransom and Lynx intrusions, with at least a dozen ransomware deployments traced to the stolen access. Manufacturers routinely front plant remote access, vendor connections and site-to-site links with Fortinet. Because valid credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, there is no patch: an attacker logs in as a trusted engineer. Treat any exposed FortiGate touching OT as compromised and rotate credentials now.

CISA advisory: Mitsubishi Electric CNC Series can be knocked offline by crafted packets

CISA advisory ICSA-26-078-05 covers an improper-validation flaw in Mitsubishi Electric CNC Series controllers that lets a remote attacker trigger an out-of-bounds read and a denial-of-service by sending specially crafted packets to TCP port 683. On a shop floor a CNC dropping offline means stopped machining and lost throughput. Review the advisory for affected models, apply Mitsubishi's guidance, and restrict access to the control port so only trusted engineering hosts can reach these machines.

High Advisory

Mitsubishi MELSEC iQ-F Ethernet modules vulnerable to DoS — one has no fix planned

CISA advisory ICSA-26-169-06 (18 June) flags an expected-behavior-violation flaw in the Mitsubishi Electric MELSEC iQ-F Series FX5-ENET/IP Ethernet module: a flood of communication packets can stop the module's communication and anomaly-detection functions, a denial-of-service against widely deployed factory-automation PLCs. Mitsubishi does not plan a fixed version for this module and instead recommends IP-filtering to block untrusted hosts. That makes network controls, not patching, the remediation — and a reason to keep these controllers off any broadly reachable network.

Guidance Policy

Engineering-led incident response is becoming the standard as living-off-the-land defeats signatures

Practitioners increasingly argue that OT incident response must be engineering-led, because attackers using legitimate tools and valid accounts leave few signature-based traces — and because the people who understand a process can spot when it behaves wrongly. For manufacturers the practical implications are organizational as much as technical: give process and controls engineers a defined role in detection and response, pair them with OT-aware monitoring, and ensure safety systems can enforce a safe state independent of the control network.

Guidance / practice — not a vulnerability OT incident-response practice
Program / organizational action Action: Define an engineering-led OT incident-response process, give controls engineers a role in anomaly detection, deploy OT-aware monitoring with process baselines, and verify independent safety systems can force a safe state.
