“FortiBleed” credential theft now feeds active ransomware — and those FortiGate firewalls front the shore-side networks that reach terminal and vessel OT

CISA warns the FortiBleed campaign has harvested working administrator and VPN credentials from tens of thousands of internet-facing FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways — tied to roughly 74,000 exposed Fortinet devices inside an operation that collected over 110 million credentials since mid-June. On 2 July researchers linked the stolen access directly to the INC Ransom and Lynx ransomware crews, with at least a dozen deployments traced to it. For ports, terminals and vessel operators the exposure is direct: these appliances front the shore-side and remote-access links that reach terminal operating systems, cargo-handling OT and ship-management networks, and ransomware crews increasingly target logistics chokepoints. Because valid credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, no patch closes the gap and an intruder logs in looking legitimate — treat any exposed FortiGate fronting maritime OT as already compromised.

Active — confirmed exploitation; now feeding INC/Lynx ransomware (12+ deployments) CISA alert 18 Jun; DHS IoCs 2 Jul 2026

Critical Active Exploitation

“CitrixBleed” returns: NetScaler flaw exploited within 24 hours, leaking secrets from gateways that front port and fleet remote access

Citrix patched CVE-2026-8451 on 30 June — an unauthenticated memory-overread in NetScaler ADC and Gateway appliances configured as a SAML identity provider — and researchers saw in-the-wild exploitation within 24 hours, aided by a same-day public proof-of-concept. Like the original CitrixBleed, it lets a remote attacker read appliance memory and harvest live session tokens, credentials and TLS keys. Ports, terminal operators and shipping lines commonly use NetScaler to publish remote access to terminal operating systems, ERP and fleet-management platforms, so leaked secrets can give an attacker authenticated entry toward operational systems. Assume any internet-facing, unpatched NetScaler had its memory read after 1 July.

Active — exploited within 24h of disclosure; public PoC Citrix NetScaler ADC / Gateway (SAML IdP)
Patch available (Citrix, 30 Jun) Action: Patch NetScaler to the fixed build now, terminate all active sessions, and — assuming memory disclosure — rotate every session token, credential and TLS private key on the appliance; remove management interfaces from the internet and hunt for anomalous session reuse.

Critical Active Exploitation

Actively-exploited Lantronix EDS5000 flaw hands attackers root on the serial-to-IP bridges used on vessels and at terminals

CISA says CVE-2025-67038, a command-injection flaw in Lantronix EDS5000 serial-to-IP device servers, is being exploited in the wild and added it to the KEV catalog on 23 June (advisory ICSA-26-069-02). An unauthenticated attacker can inject OS commands via the username parameter and run them as root, taking full control of a device whose job is bridging legacy serial equipment onto IP networks. Serial-to-IP converters are common aboard ships and at terminals, linking engine, navigation, cargo and monitoring systems to shipboard and shoreside networks — and Forescout showed such bugs can even falsify sensor readings, a serious safety concern in a maritime OT context.

Active — honeypot exploitation observed (Forescout); targeted fingerprinting In CISA KEV — deadline 26 Jun 2026 (ICSA-26-069-02)
Vendor patch available (Lantronix) Action: Inventory Lantronix EDS5000 and similar serial-to-IP servers aboard vessels and at terminals, apply the vendor fix, remove internet exposure, restrict management to segmented networks, and hunt for command injection via the username parameter and unexpected root processes.

High GNSS / Navigation

Persistent GPS/GNSS jamming and spoofing keeps disrupting vessels in the Gulf and chokepoints as MARAD warns of Iranian threats to shipping

Navigation-signal interference remains a live operational hazard for mariners: mass GPS/GNSS jamming and spoofing continues across the Strait of Hormuz, Persian Gulf, Black Sea and Baltic, producing false position jumps, degraded AIS and elevated collision and grounding risk — effects that have been tied to real incidents. MARAD advisory 2026-004 warns of heightened Iranian threats to commercial vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz and Gulf of Oman, and geopolitical tension has coincided with spikes in electronic interference. This is deliberate electronic warfare and denial rather than a vessel-network compromise, but it degrades the same PNT data bridge, ECDIS and DP systems rely on — so crews should treat GNSS as untrusted in affected waters and fall back to independent means.

Ongoing GNSS interference — heightened threat in Hormuz/Gulf transit MARAD advisory 2026-004 / USCG

Siemens urges immediate patching of RUGGEDCOM switches as a critical SINEC OS flaw (CVSS 9.8) draws a public exploit

CISA advisory ICSA-26-188-05 (published 7 July) flags a critical CVSS 9.8 vulnerability in Siemens SINEC OS before V4.0, the operating system on RUGGEDCOM industrial networking gear. A memory-corruption bug in a bundled third-party component lets a remote attacker compromise unpatched devices, and a public exploit has already been disclosed. RUGGEDCOM switches and routers are hardened networking widely used in port infrastructure, rail-served terminals and marine facilities, sitting in the path that segments and moves control-system traffic. No confirmed in-the-wild ICS attacks are reported yet, but a public exploit plus a 9.8 score on this kind of backbone gear warrants emergency patch prioritization.

USCG maritime cyber rule advances as IACS UR E26/E27 and a new Maritime Cybersecurity Act raise the bar

Maritime cyber compliance keeps hardening. Under the USCG’s MTS cyber Final Rule, US-flag and MTSA-regulated operators must report cyber incidents promptly, and the Cybersecurity Officer designation and Cybersecurity Plan submission deadline lands in 2027 — while the training obligation already passed. In parallel, IACS UR E26 (ship) and E27 (systems/equipment) now apply to newbuilds contracted since mid-2024 and are entering the delivery phase, and US lawmakers introduced a Maritime Cybersecurity Act to mandate periodic vulnerability assessments at port facilities. Operators should treat these as one converging program, not separate checklists.
