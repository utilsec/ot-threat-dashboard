FortiBleed: ~430,000 Fortinet firewalls swept for 110M+ credentials — and a July maritime bulletin ties the stolen logins to active ransomware

CISA is pressing Fortinet operators to act after researchers assessed the FortiBleed campaign swept roughly 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways — about half of those exposed worldwide — for more than 110 million credentials, and a 3 July maritime cybersecurity bulletin ties that stolen access to active INC Ransom and Lynx ransomware operations. For shipping companies and terminals the exposure is the remote-access layer: the same appliances connect shoreside offices, vessel networks and port systems, and because credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, there is no patch to close the gap. An attacker signing in with valid credentials looks like a legitimate mariner or IT admin, so treat any exposed device as compromised and rotate now — before it becomes the doorway to a terminal-halting ransomware event.

High Incident

Anubis ransomware on Italy's Adriatic Port Authority shows how a single port compromise ripples across a region

The Adriatic Port Authority, which oversees the Port of Ancona, was compromised in December 2025 in an attack later attributed to the Anubis ransomware gang, disrupting maritime trade across the region and forcing shipment delays and vessel rerouting. Researchers who detailed the case pointed to aging port infrastructure and thin cybersecurity practices as the enabling conditions — a profile that fits many regional ports. For terminal and port operators it is a concrete reminder that ransomware against back-office and terminal-operating systems translates directly into halted cargo movement, so segmentation, offline backups and tested recovery of the terminal operating system matter as much as perimeter defense.

High Threat Intel

GNSS spoofing and jamming keep degrading navigation and AIS — and it is no longer only a war-zone problem

Positioning interference continues to disrupt maritime navigation at scale, with more than a thousand vessels a day affected in some regions and vessels grounded after spoofing placed them far from their true position. The threat has also reached U.S. approaches: seven vessels near the Port of Long Beach reported GPS outages on 29 January. Because bridge navigation, AIS and increasingly automated systems lean on GNSS, spoofing and jamming are effectively an OT-integrity problem for ships and ports. Crews need trained manual and celestial fallback, and operators should treat position and timing as data that can be attacked, not trusted by default.

USCG Cybersecurity in the MTS rule: training deadline passed, Cyber Officer and plans due July 2027

The U.S. Coast Guard's Cybersecurity in the Marine Transportation System rule has been in effect since 16 July 2025, and its first phased obligation — cybersecurity training for personnel with IT/OT access — carried a 12 January 2026 deadline that has now passed. Next up, regulated U.S.-flagged vessels and facilities must designate a Cybersecurity Officer and submit a USCG-approved Cybersecurity Plan by 16 July 2027, backed by a cybersecurity assessment. Operators should be building toward those deliverables now, and reviewing the USCG's updated FAQs for how requirements apply to their vessels and facilities.

Policy Standard

IACS UR E26/E27 push cyber requirements into newbuild vessel and equipment design

The classification societies' unified requirements IACS UR E26 (ship cyber resilience) and E27 (system and equipment cyber resilience) apply to newbuild vessels and set expectations for how cyber resilience is engineered into hulls and onboard systems from the keel up. For owners and yards the effect is that cybersecurity is now a design-and-class matter, not just an operational one — equipment must arrive with defined security capabilities and documentation. Confirm newbuild and major-conversion projects are specifying E26/E27 conformance and that suppliers can evidence it.

Standard / class requirement — not a vulnerability IACS unified requirements
Design / procurement action Action: Specify IACS UR E26/E27 conformance in newbuild and conversion contracts, require suppliers to evidence system cyber-resilience capabilities, and fold class requirements into vessel design reviews.
