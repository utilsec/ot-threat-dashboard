Top Story

Federal alert sharpens: Iran-affiliated hackers are manipulating the PLCs that also run port and terminal OT

Federal agencies sharpened their warning that Iran-affiliated hackers are actively manipulating the programmable logic controllers behind US critical infrastructure — the same controllers that run cranes, fuel, power and water systems inside MTSA-regulated ports and terminals. Advisory AA26-097A, updated 22 July, confirms actors reached internet-exposed Rockwell, Schneider and Siemens gear, stole project files, and rewrote or deleted control logic, in some cases disabling shutdown and alarm functions without alerting operators. Port facilities are exactly the kind of mixed IT/OT environment the campaign favours: diverse controllers, remote maintenance access, and edge devices often reachable from the internet. The activity has already caused operational disruption and financial loss across critical-infrastructure sectors. It is opportunistic rather than sophisticated — the way in is weak credentials and controllers left exposed, not a novel exploit.

Active — confirmed hands-on-OT logic manipulation across critical infrastructure FBI/CISA/NSA/EPA — AA26-097A
No single patch — remove PLCs from internet, fix credentials Action: Inventory PLCs and edge devices across terminal, fuel, crane and facility systems reachable from the internet and pull them behind a firewall/VPN; replace default/shared credentials and require MFA on remote access; back up and hash controller logic to detect tampering; review logs against the July 2026 IOCs.
Suggested SLA: Internet-exposure & credential review — 72h; logic-integrity review — 7 days Owner:

King Ocean Services discloses data breach at Caribbean/Latin America ocean carrier

Miami-based ocean carrier and logistics firm King Ocean Services disclosed on 11 July a data-security incident that exposed personal information, including government ID numbers, of affected individuals. King Ocean provides ocean transportation, trucking and warehousing across the Caribbean, Central and South America. The confirmed impact is IT/data-side rather than vessel or terminal OT, but it underlines that maritime logistics operators remain squarely in attackers' sights.

Maritime cyber incidents surged in 2025 as OT attacks and ransomware climbed

Sector threat reporting (Resecurity and others) documents a sharp rise in maritime cyber activity: 828 recorded incidents in 2025, roughly double the prior year, with ransomware cases more than doubling and attacks specifically on maritime operational technology up around 150%. The trajectory reflects a sector with expanding connectivity — ship-to-shore links, port automation, third-party managers — and uneven security maturity. The trend, not any single event, is the story: OT is increasingly in scope for maritime attackers.

Coast Guard leadership and international partners continue to flag GPS spoofing and jamming as a persistent maritime safety threat, with disruptions reported on the order of a thousand a day affecting tens of thousands of vessels, and multiple European states warning of indiscriminate spoofing tied to electronic-warfare activity. This is interference rather than a network intrusion, but it degrades navigation, timing and AIS integrity that bridge and port systems depend on. FortiSandbox RCE flaws added to CISA KEV — the exploited edge on ports' remote-access path

CISA added two critical Fortinet flaws, CVE-2026-39808 and CVE-2026-25089 (both CVSS 9.1), to the KEV catalog on 16 July after evidence of in-the-wild exploitation. CISA set a 19 July federal remediation deadline.

Info Regulation — USCG

USCG Marine Transportation System cyber rule: reporting and Cyber Security Officer duties in force

The US Coast Guard's Marine Transportation System cybersecurity rule continues to phase in for US ports and MTSA-regulated facilities. Reportable cyber incidents must be reported to the National Response Center (within 12 hours for incidents affecting seaworthiness or security compliance), and facilities must designate a Cyber Security Officer alongside the traditional Facility Security Officer. For operators the near-term work is governance: named accountability, incident-reporting playbooks, and a cyber plan that maps to the rule.

Regulation — no exploitation USCG
Compliance — governance & reporting Action: Confirm a designated Cyber Security Officer, stand up a 12-hour incident-reporting procedure to the NRC, and align the facility cyber plan with the MTS rule's requirements.

Info Regulation — IACS

IACS UR E26/E27 keep raising the cyber bar for newbuild vessels

The IACS Unified Requirements UR E26 (whole-ship cyber resilience) and UR E27 (equipment-level security) apply to vessels with construction contracts signed on or after 1 July 2024, covering identification, protection, detection, response and recovery across integrated IT/OT and third-party equipment. The requirements shift cyber resilience into ship design and supplier obligations, so owners, yards and integrators building 500 GT-plus tonnage need cyber baked into specifications, not bolted on later.

Regulation — no exploitation IACS
Compliance — newbuild design & supplier terms Action: For newbuild and major-conversion programs, hold yards and equipment suppliers to UR E26/E27 in specifications and factory acceptance, and ensure delivered vessels have the required detection and recovery capabilities.
