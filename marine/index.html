Top Story

GPS spoofing and AIS jamming scramble more than 1,100 ships around the Strait of Hormuz

Maritime-intelligence firms including Windward documented GNSS interference affecting over 1,100 vessels across the Middle East Gulf in a single 24-hour window as hostilities among Iran, the U.S. and Israel escalated. Ships’ reported positions were displaced onto airports, inland sites and even a nuclear plant; Windward flagged at least 21 new AIS jamming clusters across UAE, Qatari, Omani and Iranian waters, and Hormuz transits fell roughly a third below normal. For vessel and terminal operators the lesson is concrete: in contested waters, satellite position, timing and AIS tracks cannot be treated as authoritative. Spoofed positions raise collision risk in congested lanes and can trigger false compliance and sanctions-screening alerts, while switching AIS off to avoid mislocation sacrifices collision-avoidance visibility — pushing reliance back onto radar, inertial aids and disciplined visual watchkeeping.

Confirm sanctions- and compliance-screening systems will not auto-flag a spoofed track as a port call or territorial incursion, and review war-risk cover and routing with the vessel operator before transit.

King Ocean Services discloses a data breach at a Florida-based container shipping line

King Ocean Services, a Florida-headquartered ocean-freight and container carrier running services across the Western Hemisphere, began notifying individuals on 11 July of a data-security incident that exposed personal information, including government identification numbers. Confirmed via the company’s own breach notice; operational details remain limited.

High Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converter flaw exploited in the wild — the bridges between shipboard serial gear and the network

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, one of the flaws disclosed in April through the BRIDGE:BREAK research project. This category deserves particular attention at sea and on the quayside. Maritime OT is still overwhelmingly serial at the sensor and actuator layer — NMEA feeds, engine and auxiliary monitoring, alarm and monitoring panels, cargo and ballast systems, crane and gate equipment — and serial-to-IP converters are what connected all of it to shipboard or terminal Ethernet during retrofits and digitalisation projects. They are typically installed by an integrator, absent from any drawing, and never patched because the equipment behind them still works. Exploitation is confirmed and follows an explicit OT threat warning; for most operators the first task is establishing what is actually installed.

Active — exploitation confirmed in the wild Lantronix serial-to-IP converters (BRIDGE:BREAK)
Vendor firmware available — see Lantronix advisory Action: Sweep vessel and terminal networks for serial-to-IP converters by scanning for management ports rather than trusting as-built documentation — on ships in particular, the drawings and the installation diverged years ago. Remove internet exposure first, segment the rest, and schedule firmware updates around drydock or planned maintenance. Feed the findings into the asset inventory your cybersecurity assessment will need.

Info Threat Report

USCG Cyber Command’s fifth CTIME report: reported maritime cyber incidents up 17%, phishing dominant

Coast Guard Cyber Command (CGCYBER) released its fifth annual Cyber Trends and Insights in the Marine Environment (CTIME) report, drawing on a record 42 Cyber Protection Team missions plus industry telemetry from 2025. Reported maritime cyber incidents rose 17% year-over-year. Phishing was the initial-access vector in 43% of incidents — an 18-point jump — with syndicates such as Scattered Spider pairing phishing with voice impersonation (vishing) to defeat help desks and weak MFA; ransomware featured in 19% of attack paths, and OT was in scope for 62% of the command’s missions. CGCYBER stresses that the blurring IT/OT boundary aboard ships, machinery and port facilities raises the odds of a cyber event producing physical consequences. Notably, Cyber Protection Teams deployed alongside boarding teams in Dark Fleet interdiction operations for the first time.

Threat report — trend data, not a single incident USCG CGCYBER — CTIME 2026 (5th edition)
No patch — programmatic guidance Action: Use the report to prioritise. Phishing/vishing awareness and help-desk identity verification come first, because that is the dominant vector; then MFA hardening and IT/OT segmentation so an office-side compromise cannot reach navigation, cargo or engine controls. Map the findings directly onto the cybersecurity assessment you owe under the USCG rule — the report is effectively a threat model handed to you by the regulator.

Critical Active Exploitation

SonicWall SMA 1000 zero-days under active attack; both added to CISA KEV

SonicWall confirmed active exploitation of two Secure Mobile Access 1000 flaws after investigating multiple cases. CVE-2026-15409 is a CVSS 10.0 unauthenticated server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface; CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2) lets an authenticated administrator inject OS commands through the Appliance Management Console. CISA added both to the KEV catalog on 14 July with a 17 July federal deadline. For ports, terminals and vessel operators the placement is what matters: this class of appliance brokers the shoreside and vendor access that reaches terminal operating systems, crane and gate control, and satcom-backhauled vessel networks. A confirmed compromise is also a reportable-incident question under the USCG rule, where notification to the National Response Center is due immediately on discovery — a notification decision, not just a patch.

Active — SonicWall confirms exploitation in multiple cases In CISA KEV 14 Jul 2026 — FCEB due 17 Jul
Patched — 12.4.3-03453 / 12.5.0-02835 hotfix Action: Patch SMA 1000 to the fixed build immediately, then run SonicWall’s forensic checks — /__api__/login or /__api__/logout requests returning HTTP 200, /wsproxy requests (HTTP 101) with suspicious host parameters, path-traversal hotfix rollbacks, and rogue login/logout routes in conf.json. On any indicator, re-image, rotate all user and admin passwords, reset TOTP tokens — and involve your Cybersecurity Officer on the NRC reporting decision early rather than after the forensics.

High Vulnerability

BeyondTrust patches critical auth-bypass flaws in the privileged remote access tooling vendors use

BeyondTrust disclosed two authentication-bypass vulnerabilities on 7 July in Remote Support (RS) and Privileged Remote Access (PRA). CVE-2026-40138 (CVSS 9.2) stems from improper validation of authentication data and could let a network-positioned attacker bypass access controls and reach the appliance, including elevated accounts; CVE-2026-40139 involves improper processing of RS authentication requests. Both require a specific authentication configuration to be enabled, which limits exposure, and neither is under active exploitation — no public proof-of-concept exists. Cloud customers were patched on 21 April; self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier needs action. Alongside the SonicWall top story, the pattern is clear enough: vendor remote maintenance — the standing route OEMs use into vessel and terminal systems — is under sustained research attention.

No known exploitation; no public PoC as of this edition Not in CISA KEV
Patched — RS/PRA 25.3.3+ or April rollup (cloud patched 21 Apr) Action: Check whether you run self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier with the affected authentication configuration enabled, and patch. Independently, enumerate which equipment makers and service providers hold standing remote access into vessel or terminal OT, and whether those sessions are time-bounded, individually approved and recorded — this is exactly the ground a cybersecurity assessment will cover.

CISA issued four ICS advisories on 14 July (ICSA-26-195-01 through -04), released alongside vendor Patch Tuesday advisories: three from ABB and one from Rockwell. The ABB entries cover Advant Master Online Builder, where an incorrect Online Builder version was included on installation media, and Ability Edgenius, where a Linux kernel privilege-escalation issue could let a locally authenticated user or a compromised container workload gain root. Info Regulation

The USCG cyber rule’s big deadline is 16 July 2027: Cybersecurity Officer, assessment and plan

A forward-looking compliance marker rather than news. Under the Coast Guard’s Cybersecurity in the Marine Transportation System final rule, U.S.-flagged vessels, OCS facilities and waterfront facilities must, by 16 July 2027, designate a Cybersecurity Officer, complete an initial cybersecurity assessment (annually thereafter) and submit a Cybersecurity Plan to the USCG for approval. Two earlier milestones are already live and worth confirming you meet: reportable cyber incidents have had to go to the National Response Center immediately on discovery since 16 July 2025, and the personnel cybersecurity training requirement took effect 16 January 2026. The Coast Guard has also signalled that Port State Control scrutiny will extend to indicators of poor cybersecurity practice on foreign-flagged vessels, with deficiencies, detention or denial of entry available as outcomes.

Regulatory milestone — no threat activity USCG MTS cyber rule (33 CFR) — CySO/assessment/plan due 16 Jul 2027
No patch — compliance obligation Action: With twelve months to run, the binding constraint is usually the assessment, not the paperwork: it depends on an asset inventory most operators do not yet have (see the serial-server items above). Start the inventory now, name the Cybersecurity Officer early enough that they can influence the plan rather than inherit it, and confirm your crews and shoreside staff have actually completed the training that became mandatory in January.
