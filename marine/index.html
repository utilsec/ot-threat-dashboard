CISA flags critical API flaws in iDirect iQ-Series satellite terminals — the VSAT box nearly every vessel depends on

On 2 July CISA warned (ICSA-26-183-01) that two high-severity API flaws in ST Engineering iDirect iQ-Series satellite terminals let unauthenticated attackers on the network pull sensitive device data and remotely reboot the unit. For maritime operators that is a direct hit on the box most vessels, rigs and remote terminals depend on for connectivity: iDirect provides the VSAT backhaul linking ship to shore. CVE-2026-38059 exposes serial numbers, device and key identifiers and firmware over unauthenticated REST endpoints; CVE-2026-38057 lets a malicious web page an authenticated admin visits trigger a reboot, and repeated abuse sustains a denial of the satellite link — cutting navigation data, reporting and safety comms at sea. iDirect fixed it in software 4.5.2.2; terminals on 4.5.2.1 or earlier need updating.

No confirmed in-the-wild exploitation as of this edition Not in CISA KEV
Fixed in 4.5.2.2 — update from ≤4.5.2.1 Action: Inventory iDirect iQ-Series terminals across the fleet and shore sites, update to 4.5.2.2 or newer, restrict management interfaces to trusted networks, and monitor for unexpected reboots or loss of the satellite link.

GPS/GNSS interference keeps spoofing vessels off course — roughly 1,000 disruptions a day

GNSS interference has become a routine hazard rather than a wartime exception, with reporting citing on the order of 1,000 spoofing or jamming events daily affecting tens of thousands of vessels. Incidents range from ships shown hundreds of miles from their true position to AIS tracks jumping erratically at impossible speeds — and even a scheduled U.S. Navy test off Long Beach in January knocked out GPS for seven inbound vessels within an hour. The lesson for bridge teams is to treat GNSS as spoofable: cross-check position with radar, terrestrial aids and dead reckoning, and train crews to recognize and report anomalies.

Active — routine, ongoing interference worldwide GNSS spoofing / jamming
No patch — procedures, cross-checks, crew training Action: Brief bridge teams to treat GNSS as untrusted, cross-check position against radar and terrestrial aids, maintain dead-reckoning discipline, log and report interference to authorities, and consider resilient PNT or anomaly-detection tooling on key routes.

Counterfeit malware-laced USB drives breached classified military networks — a warning for vessel OT

Reporting describes counterfeit USB drives carrying China-linked malware that bypassed procurement channels and infected more than 50 computers, including closed classified networks, during 2024 disaster-relief operations — with similar tainted devices still sold through mainstream online retailers. For maritime OT the parallel is direct: engine, cargo and navigation systems are frequently updated by USB in air-gapped or lightly connected environments, making removable media one of the few reliable ways onto a ship's control network. Controlling and scanning USB media is a low-cost, high-value control.

FortiBleed credential theft now feeds ransomware — ports and shipping lines run on the same firewalls

SOCRadar reported on 2 July that FortiBleed — a sniffer that harvested 110M+ credentials from roughly 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls since mid-June — is now driving INC Ransom and Lynx intrusions. Ports, terminals and shipping lines lean heavily on Fortinet for remote access to terminal operating systems, crane and gate controls and vessel-to-shore links. Because credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, no patch helps: an attacker signs in as a legitimate administrator. Assume any exposed FortiGate touching port or vessel OT is compromised and rotate credentials now.

Active exploitation — credentials feeding ransomware (INC / Lynx) Credential-exposure campaign (no single CVE)
No patch — credential rotation & access hardening Action: Rotate all credentials and certificates on internet-facing FortiGate/SSL-VPN devices at ports, terminals and shore offices, terminate sessions, enforce MFA, restrict management access, and hunt for unauthorized logins.

FCC votes to toughen submarine-cable security rules — the arteries under global shipping lanes

On 25 June the FCC voted unanimously to modernize the rules governing submarine cables that carry the vast majority of transoceanic internet, financial and data traffic, adding mandatory cybersecurity and physical-security risk-management plans for submarine line terminal equipment operators and excluding entities tied to designated foreign adversaries. The same order tightened cyber-hygiene requirements for emergency-alert participants. For the maritime sector it signals rising regulatory attention to the undersea and coastal communications infrastructure that ports, vessels and offshore operations quietly depend on.

Regulatory action — not a vulnerability FCC rulemaking
Policy / compliance action Action: If you operate or contract submarine line terminal equipment or coastal comms, review the new FCC cybersecurity and physical-risk-management plan requirements and supply-chain restrictions, and confirm certification paths for your equipment and vendors.

USCG Marine Transportation System cyber rule is in force — assessments and reporting are now table stakes

The U.S. Coast Guard's Cybersecurity in the Marine Transportation System rule, effective since mid-2025, requires U.S.-flagged vessels and regulated waterfront and offshore facilities to designate a Cybersecurity Officer, run annual assessments, report incidents to the National Response Center, and complete crew cybersecurity training — a deadline that passed in January. Operators should treat this as an operating baseline, not a project: keep assessments current, exercise the reporting path, and align vessel and facility security plans with the rule as enforcement matures.

Compliance program — not a vulnerability USCG MTS cyber rule
Program / compliance action Action: Confirm a designated Cybersecurity Officer, keep annual cyber assessments and crew training current, rehearse incident reporting to the National Response Center, and align vessel and facility security plans with the MTS rule.
