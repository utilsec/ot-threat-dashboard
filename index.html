Thirteen governments warn Russia’s FSB Center 16 spent a decade mapping critical-infrastructure networks through exposed routers

The FBI, CISA, NSA and partners from thirteen nations issued a joint advisory (AA26-194A) warning that Russia’s FSB Center 16 — the espionage unit tracked as Berserk Bear, Energetic Bear, Dragonfly and Static Tundra — has spent more than a decade quietly compromising the networking devices that front critical infrastructure. The actors go after internet-exposed routers and switches still running legacy, unencrypted protocols — SNMP v1/v2 and Cisco’s Smart Install (SMI) — abusing weak or default credentials and a seven-year-old Cisco IOS flaw (CVE-2018-0171) to pull configurations and map victim networks across energy, water, telecommunications and government. For OT defenders the message is blunt: the edge network gear bridging IT and the plant is itself the objective, and a mapped, quietly-owned router is a pre-positioned foothold toward the control system behind it.

Attackers keep exploiting a critical PTC Windchill flaw in the wild — a manufacturing PLM foothold toward the plant floor

Threat actors are still actively exploiting a critical remote-code-execution flaw in PTC Windchill, the product-lifecycle-management platform that anchors engineering and manufacturing data across automotive, aerospace, defense and heavy-industry suppliers. CISA added CVE-2026-12569 to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 25 June — the first PTC product ever listed — after PTC confirmed attackers were chaining the unauthenticated flaw to plant persistent JSP webshells for remote command execution and data theft. Because Windchill holds the design files, BOMs and process documentation that feed OT and production, a compromised server is both a supply-chain intelligence goldmine and a foothold toward the plant floor. Patches and mitigations shipped from 17 June; internet-exposed instances still need triage.

Critical Incident / Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converters under active attack — unauthenticated root command injection on OT connectivity gear

CISA confirmed in-the-wild exploitation of CVE-2025-67038, an unauthenticated OS-command-injection flaw in Lantronix EDS5000 serial-to-IP device servers that yields root-level code execution. The bug — one of the 20 “BRIDGE:BREAK” serial-to-IP issues Forescout disclosed in April — was added to CISA’s KEV catalog on 23 June. Forescout later reported a honeypot caught exploitation as early as 5 April, shortly after the patch shipped, suggesting attackers reverse-engineered the fix; the activity was targeted rather than generic botnet scanning. Serial-to-IP converters bridge legacy serial OT and IoT devices onto networks, so a compromised unit can serve as a lateral-movement foothold or be used to falsify sensor readings and mask dangerous process conditions. Thousands of Lantronix systems sit internet-exposed, most in the U.S.

Medium Incident / Nation-State

Mandiant confirms Iran-linked Handala breach of California Water Service stopped short of OT — despite the crew’s disruption boast

California Water Service (Cal Water), one of the largest U.S. investor-owned water utilities, said Mandiant’s investigation into the breach claimed by Iran-linked group Handala found no evidence of activity in its IT or OT environments. Handala — widely assessed as a front for Iranian operations — had leaked ~5GB of data and asserted it could have disrupted the water supply, implying deep ICS access. Mandiant instead traced the activity to unauthorized access of a small number of user accounts across two third-party service-provider platforms, one active customer’s online account via stolen credentials, and an external GPS-correction site; no billing or payment systems and no control systems were reached. The case is a clean example of separating a threat actor’s psychological claims from verified impact — while underscoring that third-party and customer-facing accounts remain a soft entry point around OT.

High Threat Research / Nation-State

Iran, Russia and China are hitting water systems differently — and defenses should follow their intent

Research summarized by DomainTools separates how the three main state threats approach water and wastewater OT. Iran-aligned crews and hacktivists (CyberAv3ngers and similar IRGC-linked groups) go after weakly authenticated, internet-exposed PLCs and HMIs, posing the highest risk to smaller utilities. Russia-aligned actors are more willing to actually manipulate controls — researchers point to the 2024 Muleshoe, Texas, incident where a remote interface was used to overflow a water tank. China’s Volt Typhoon activity looks less like immediate disruption and more like durable pre-positioning for a future conflict. The practical guidance for utilities: reduce internet exposure and enforce strong authentication first, then tune detection and segmentation to the actor most likely to target you.

Active nation-state targeting — documented water-OT intrusions Iran / Russia / China — water & wastewater

High Threat Research / Nation-State

Claroty Team82: Iran-linked crews are hijacking emergency warning systems — a cyber-psychological attack on OT audio gear

Claroty’s Team82 detailed how Iran-linked actors — CyberAv3ngers, tied to the IRGC and Iran’s intelligence ministry — targeted the emergency-alert and public-address systems Israelis rely on during missile attacks. The researchers reconstructed an operation abusing internet-exposed Barix audio-over-IP broadcast devices via a high-severity firmware flaw (CVE-2024-41700), and drew technical parallels to a January 2025 supply-chain intrusion by the MOIS-linked group Handala. Team82’s core assessment is that the real technical impact of silencing sirens may be smaller than its intended psychological effect: eroding public trust in official warnings. For OT defenders the lesson is concrete — safety-adjacent audio, siren and PA controllers are attack surface too, a patch exists but must be applied manually so many devices stay exposed, and these systems belong in your OT asset inventory and off the public internet.

CISA flags critical flaws in Hydro-Québec’s Le Circuit Électrique EV-charging backend

CISA advisory ICSA-26-188-01 covers three vulnerabilities in the cloud backend behind Hydro-Québec’s Le Circuit Électrique EV-charging network, which manages more than 3,500 public stations. The most serious, CVE-2026-20744 (CVSS 9.8), is an unauthenticated websocket endpoint that could allow privilege escalation; two others enable denial-of-service by hammering authentication (CVE-2026-42952) or by opening multiple sessions under one charging-station ID to overwhelm the backend (CVE-2026-44383). EV-charging is an increasingly OT-adjacent slice of the energy sector, where a compromised management backend can disrupt availability across a whole fleet. Hydro-Québec has mitigated most stations by disabling OCPP and adding authentication where the protocol is still in use; no in-the-wild exploitation has been reported.

High Advisory

CISA flags multiple vulnerabilities in OpenPLC v3, used across water and energy

CISA advisory ICSA-26-190-01, published 9 July, flags an authenticated arbitrary-file-write flaw in the legacy web-UI program-upload workflow of OpenPLC v3, the widely used open-source PLC runtime that CISA lists as deployed in water & wastewater and energy. Crucially, the OpenPLC project says v3 is end-of-life and will receive no further patches — the recommended fix is to migrate to OpenPLC v4. Because OpenPLC is free, cross-platform and common in labs, pilots, small utilities and training setups, these flaws can drift into production control paths that lack vendor patch-management discipline. Operators running OpenPLC v3 — especially anything reachable from business networks or the internet — should plan the v4 upgrade and lock down access to the runtime and its web interface in the meantime.

High Advisory

CISA flags unauthenticated API flaws in ST Engineering iDirect satellite terminals used in energy and remote sites

CISA advisory ICSA-26-183-01, published 2 July, warns that two high-severity flaws in ST Engineering iDirect iQ-Series satellite terminals let an unauthenticated remote attacker take full control of an affected device. The iQ200 exposes REST API endpoints (including /api/identity) without authentication, and state-changing endpoints such as /api/reboot lack CSRF protection and rely only on a session cookie missing the SameSite attribute. The bugs carry CVSS scores around 8.6 and 8.1 and affect firmware 4.5.2.1 and earlier. Satellite terminals frequently backhaul connectivity for remote energy, pipeline and utility sites, so a hijacked terminal can be used to intercept traffic or pivot into the OT network behind it. ST Engineering released fixed firmware 4.5.2.2 on 28 June, ahead of the public advisory.

Policy Regulation

NERC CIP-012-2 takes effect 1 July, tightening protection of control-center comms

NERC’s revised reliability standard CIP-012-2 became enforceable on 1 July 2026, sharpening requirements to protect real-time assessment and monitoring data exchanged between bulk-electric-system control centers — and closing gaps around the security of that data while it is being transmitted. It applies to Balancing Authorities, Reliability Coordinators, Transmission Operators and Owners, and Generator Operators and Owners. For North American electric entities the effective date is a hard compliance milestone: confidentiality, integrity and availability protections for inter-control-center links must be in place and evidenced, not merely planned. The change also reinforces a broader OT-security principle worth applying beyond NERC’s footprint — that the communication paths between control centers are themselves critical assets deserving encryption, monitoring and formal documentation.

Effective date — enforceable 1 Jul 2026 NERC CIP-012-2 — bulk electric system
Compliance milestone Action: Electric entities should confirm CIP-012-2 protections for control-center-to-control-center comms are implemented and evidenced (covering data in transit), update the required plan and responsible-entity documentation, and validate encryption/monitoring on those links ahead of audit.

Policy Guidance

NIST publishes SP-1339 OT Backup Quick Start Guide to speed industrial recovery

NIST released SP-1339, an OT Backup Quick Start Guide aimed at helping industrial operators build backup and recovery practices tuned to control-system realities — where restoration can involve physical processes, safety interlocks and firmware provenance, not just re-imaging a server. It is practical, actionable guidance rather than a mandate, and it dovetails with the recurring incident-response lesson that OT recovery fails when backups aren’t OT-aware or offline-tested. Asset owners can use it as a template to validate that controller configurations, logic and engineering-workstation images are captured, protected and recoverable — and to rehearse recovery before an incident forces the issue.

Guidance — voluntary best-practice resource NIST SP-1339 — OT backup & recovery
Resilience / recovery readiness Action: Use SP-1339 to review OT backup coverage — confirm controller logic/config and engineering-workstation images are backed up, stored offline, integrity-checked and periodically test-restored — and fold OT recovery into incident-response tabletop exercises.
