“FortiBleed” credential-theft campaign hits ~86,600 FortiGate devices — the edge firewalls and VPNs guarding OT networks

CISA is warning that a months-long campaign dubbed FortiBleed has compromised working administrator and VPN credentials on tens of thousands of internet-facing FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways — roughly 86,600 devices across 194 countries, with active exploitation confirmed and IoCs shared via DHS on 2 July. Why OT defenders should care: these appliances are the front door to industrial networks, terminating the remote access and IT/OT segmentation that most plants depend on, so stolen admin credentials can hand an attacker a straight path toward control systems without touching a single PLC. Researchers attribute the campaign to Russian-speaking actors reusing credentials from earlier Fortinet flaws and brute-forcing devices lacking MFA. CISA urges immediate session termination and password resets on all internet-facing Fortinet gear.

High Threat Campaign

New “Armored Likho” APT hits electric-power and government targets with AI-generated malware

Kaspersky disclosed a previously undocumented espionage cluster it calls Armored Likho, targeting government agencies and electric-power sector organizations across Russia, Kazakhstan and Brazil. The intrusions start with spear-phishing that drops a RAR archive and staged loaders pulling a new Python infostealer, BusySnake, from GitHub — it harvests browser credentials, cookies, OTP codes and Telegram sessions and opens reverse-SSH tunnels for remote access. Notably, researchers assess the first-stage loaders were likely AI-generated, citing redundant comments and boilerplate code blocks inconsistent with hand-written malware — an early real-world signal of LLM-accelerated tooling reaching critical-infrastructure targeting. This is IT-side credential and espionage activity, not confirmed OT manipulation, but power-sector engineering staff are squarely in scope, and stolen credentials are the usual bridge from business networks into control systems.

Threat actors are exploiting CVE-2026-12569 in PTC Windchill and FlexPLM in the wild — the first confirmed real-world abuse of the product lifecycle management platform, and the first PTC flaw added to CISA's KEV catalog (added 25 June, federal deadline 28 June). Critical Active Exploitation

Actively-exploited Lantronix EDS5000 flaw hands attackers root on a serial-to-IP bridge between PLCs and the enterprise network

CISA says CVE-2025-67038, a command-injection flaw in Lantronix EDS5000 serial-to-IP device servers, is being exploited in the wild and added it to the KEV catalog on 23 June (federal deadline 26 June, advisory ICSA-26-069-02). An unauthenticated attacker can inject OS commands via the username parameter and execute them as root, fully controlling a device whose entire job is bridging legacy serial gear — PLCs, RTUs, meters and sensors — onto IP networks. The flaw is one of 20 “BRIDGE:BREAK” issues Forescout disclosed in April; researchers showed such bugs can manipulate sensor readings to hide dangerous conditions. Forescout observed exploitation in an EDS5000 honeypot on 5 April — targeted fingerprinting, not commodity botnet traffic — suggesting attackers reverse-engineered the patch.

Siemens urges immediate patching of RUGGEDCOM switches as a critical SINEC OS flaw (CVSS 9.8) draws a public exploit

CISA advisory ICSA-26-188-05 (published 7 July) flags a critical CVSS 9.8 vulnerability in Siemens SINEC OS versions before V4.0, the operating system running on RUGGEDCOM industrial networking gear. The root issue is a memory-corruption bug in a bundled third-party component (GNU elfutils) that a remote attacker can trigger to compromise unpatched devices; a public exploit has already been disclosed. RUGGEDCOM switches and routers are hardened OT networking backbone deployed in substations, rail, energy and heavy-industry environments, so a compromise sits directly in the path that segments and moves control-system traffic. Siemens has released a fixed version for the RUGGEDCOM RST2428P and urges customers to update immediately. No confirmed in-the-wild ICS attacks are reported yet, but with a public exploit and a 9.8 score on widely deployed OT infrastructure this warrants emergency patch prioritization.

NERC CIP 2026 milestones hit: control-center data protection now effective, internal network monitoring phasing in

A cluster of NERC CIP milestones is now live for electric utilities. CIP-012-2 became effective on 1 July 2026, strengthening protection of real-time operational data exchanged between control centers. Earlier in 2026, CIP-003-9 expanded governance for low-impact BES Cyber Systems around vendor electronic remote access and supply-chain risk, and CIP-015-1 introduced mandatory Internal Network Security Monitoring (INSM) with phased compliance running through 2030. Looking ahead, NERC must file a CIP-015-2 modification by 2 September 2026 to extend INSM scope to Electronic Access Control or Monitoring Systems (EACMS) and Physical Access Control Systems (PACS) outside the electronic security perimeter. Registered entities should confirm CIP-012-2 control-center protections are operating, stand up an INSM roadmap, and begin scoping EACMS/PACS assets ahead of the expansion.

The deadline for organizations to complete their first NIS2 compliance audit moved to 30 June 2026, and the EU has continued to standardize implementation — the NIS2 Cooperation Group adopted common incident-reporting templates and the Commission proposed simplifications to ease administrative burden while keeping notification timelines strict. CISA launches ANCHOR-CI, a new public-private framework for critical-infrastructure security collaboration

On 1 July, CISA announced ANCHOR-CI (Alliance of National Councils for Homeland Operational Resilience – Critical Infrastructure), an advisory-body framework meant to broaden information sharing and government-industry partnership on critical-infrastructure security. It absorbs lessons from the retired CIPAC model and can spin up four council types — sector, cross-sector, industry, and regional coordinating councils — drawing federal, state, local, tribal and territorial partners. It will run for an initial two years. For OT asset owners the practical value is a more structured channel for real-time threat sharing and for shaping sector guidance; the near-term move is to identify which council(s) map to your sector and region and to plug your ISAC/sector engagement into the new structure so you are inside the loop as it stands up.
