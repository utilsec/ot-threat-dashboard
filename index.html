CISA/FBI: Iranian-affiliated actors keep hitting internet-exposed Allen-Bradley PLCs — with confirmed operational disruption

U.S. agencies continue to warn that Iranian-affiliated actors are exploiting internet-facing Rockwell Automation/Allen-Bradley programmable logic controllers across water and wastewater, energy and government-facilities sectors, a campaign running since at least March and re-emphasized amid heightened Middle East tensions. Unlike the many inflated hacktivist claims, this activity is agency-confirmed and some victims have experienced real operational disruption and financial loss. The actors are reaching PLCs left exposed on the public internet, often protected only by default or weak credentials, rather than burning novel exploits — which makes the fix largely one of hygiene and exposure reduction. Get every PLC and OT device off the public internet or behind a VPN with MFA, change all default/shared credentials, and hunt for unexpected configuration or logic changes on internet-reachable controllers.

Anubis ransomware at the Adriatic Port Authority — a fresh case study of how an IT breach halts maritime logistics and leaks port security plans

Threat-intelligence firm Resecurity published a detailed case study on the Anubis ransomware attack against Italy's Central Adriatic Sea Port Authority (which runs the port of Ancona), and the reporting drew a maritime-sector warning across the trade press. The intrusion — assessed to have started from a spear-phishing email at the port-management company before lateral movement into core systems — encrypted the platforms behind cargo tracking, shipping schedules and customs processing and exfiltrated contracts, employee records and, most concerning, port safety plans and security-operations details. Attackers reportedly demanded a multi-million-dollar bitcoin ransom under a seven-day publish threat. The episode is a clean illustration of the pattern that dominates OT incident data: adversaries land in enterprise IT and cause operational and safety impact without ever touching a PLC, and the stolen physical-security documentation is exactly what smuggling or insider-recruitment operations prize. For port and vessel operators, treat phishing resistance, IT/OT segmentation and protection of physical-security documents as OT-safety controls, not just IT hygiene.

High Incident

The Gentlemen ransomware halts Australia's Mackay Sugar mid-harvest — a food/agri-industrial disruption with no controller ever touched

Mackay Sugar, Australia's second-largest raw sugar producer, disclosed a ransomware attack on 10 June that forced suspension of cane haulage and milling at its Farleigh and Racecourse mills in North Queensland during the opening weeks of the annual crushing season, stranding cane from roughly 1,300 grower families and the surrounding regional supply chain. The Gentlemen ransomware group (tracked by Microsoft as Storm-2697, and assessed by researchers as one of 2026's most active operations) claimed the attack and listed the company on its leak site around 15 June, threatening to publish exfiltrated data. The company stood up a limited manual crushing operation to clear already-harvested cane and later reported steam trials and a staged restart of milling. As with most OT incidents, the damage cascaded from an enterprise-IT ransomware event into physical operations and seasonal supply-chain loss without adversaries touching a PLC. The lesson for food & beverage and agri-industrial operators is timing: an IT outage during a harvest or processing campaign is an operational and safety event, not just a data problem — so phishing resistance, IT/OT segmentation and rehearsed manual-fallback procedures should be sized against peak-season windows.

On 25 June CISA added a critical improper-input-validation flaw (CVE-2026-12569, CVSS 9.3) enabling unauthenticated remote code execution in PTC Windchill PDMLink/FlexPLM to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog. Reporting indicates attackers continued dropping web shells onto unpatched, internet-reachable instances. Max-severity Ubiquiti UniFi OS chain exploited in the wild — full root takeover of network gear that underpins many OT and building sites

CISA confirmed a three-flaw chain in Ubiquiti UniFi OS is being exploited in the wild and added it to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 23 June. The chain — CVE-2026-34908 (improper access control), CVE-2026-34909 (path traversal) and CVE-2026-34910 (improper input validation) — lets a network-adjacent attacker with no credentials reach arbitrary command execution, and researchers showed the three can be combined into full remote code execution with root privileges. UniFi gateways, switches and controllers are widely deployed at industrial plants, utilities, remote sites and commercial facilities, frequently sitting astride the IT/OT boundary and providing the routing, segmentation and remote access that OT depends on — so a root-level compromise here is a direct path to pivot toward control networks. Update UniFi OS now, pull management interfaces off the public internet, rotate credentials and sessions, and hunt connected OT segments for follow-on activity.

Critical Advisory

Delta Electronics DVP12SE PLC exposes unauthenticated Modbus — anyone on the network can rewrite control logic, and no patch yet

CISA advisory ICSA-26-181-07 (30 June) warns that the Delta Electronics DVP12SE programmable logic controller exposes its Modbus TCP service on port 502 with no authentication or access control, so any reachable network source can read and write coils, holding registers, relay states and process-control functions without credentials or operator approval (CVE-2026-12818, CVE-2026-12819). A companion resource-exhaustion issue lets an attacker flood the Modbus port to degrade the device. In practice this means an attacker with network line-of-sight can remotely issue commands, change operational values and interfere with control logic — the classic insecure-by-design exposure that makes flat OT networks and internet-reachable PLCs so dangerous. All versions are affected and Delta is still working on a fix, so mitigation is compensating controls: enable the PLC's built-in IP Filter, segment and firewall the controller off any untrusted network, and never expose Modbus/502 to the internet.

Critical auth-bypass in Frangoteam FUXA web SCADA/HMI — 1,200+ instances exposed, many at water and solar sites

CISA advisory ICSA-26-181-02 (30 June) covers CVE-2026-13207, an authentication-bypass flaw (CVSS v4 8.6) in the open-source FUXA web SCADA/HMI platform (versions 1.3.1 and earlier) caused by dot-segment path normalization in its REST API. An unauthenticated attacker can reach user accounts and role assignments, exposing the OT privilege structure to reconnaissance. Researchers at GreyNoise reported finding more than 1,200 internet-facing FUXA instances shortly after disclosure — most still unpatched and a number of them at water-treatment and solar operations, exactly the small, distributed sites that put dashboards online for remote monitoring. Frangoteam shipped a fix in version 1.3.2 and CISA released Snort/Suricata signatures. Upgrade to 1.3.2, pull FUXA off the public internet behind a VPN, rotate credentials, and load the detection signatures into your IDS/IPS.
