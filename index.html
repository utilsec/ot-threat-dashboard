Canada's CSE reveals a pro-Russia group claimed control-level access to a Quebec water plant — pumps, chlorine dosing and pressure allegedly within reach

In its 2025–2026 annual report released last week, Canada's Communications Security Establishment (CSE) and its Canadian Centre for Cyber Security disclosed that pro-Russia hacktivist group NoName057(16) claimed it had breached an unnamed Quebec municipality's water treatment plant and gained the ability to covertly control pumps, chlorine dosing, pressure settings and the monitoring and alert systems operators depend on. CSE said it learned of the activity in October only through the group's own public claim of responsibility — the utility did not detect it independently, which is the most telling detail. But a national cyber authority treating the claim as credible enough to feature is itself the signal. Treat it as a template threat: assume internet-exposed or weakly-authenticated HMI/SCADA at small utilities is being hunted, verify no unauthorized changes to chemical-dosing or pressure setpoints, pull operator interfaces off any public path, and enforce MFA on all remote access.

CISA/FBI: Iranian-affiliated actors keep hitting internet-exposed Allen-Bradley PLCs — with confirmed operational disruption

U.S. agencies continue to warn that Iranian-affiliated actors are exploiting internet-facing Rockwell Automation/Allen-Bradley programmable logic controllers across water and wastewater, energy and government-facilities sectors, a campaign running since at least March and re-emphasized amid heightened Middle East tensions. Unlike the many inflated hacktivist claims, this activity is agency-confirmed and some victims have experienced real operational disruption and financial loss. The actors are reaching PLCs left exposed on the public internet, often protected only by default or weak credentials, rather than burning novel exploits — which makes the fix largely one of hygiene and exposure reduction. Get every PLC and OT device off the public internet or behind a VPN with MFA, change all default/shared credentials, and hunt for unexpected configuration or logic changes on internet-reachable controllers.

Iran-linked Handala claims breach of California Water Service — Cal Water confirms an IT/billing compromise but says no OT activity was found

The Iran-linked group Handala claimed a breach of California Water Service (Cal Water), one of the largest investor-owned U.S. water utilities, publishing a roughly 5GB data set that analysts assess contains customer billing PII and administrative credentials for an internal GPS-correction (RTKBase/NTRIP) network spanning several service districts. Cal Water investigated and reported it found no evidence of activity in its operational-technology environment, framing the incident as an IT and billing-system compromise rather than a control-system intrusion — while Handala publicly boasted it could have disrupted the water supply. Handala is assessed as an Iranian MOIS-aligned front (also tracked as Void Manticore / Storm-0842) whose toolkit includes destructive wipers, so the episode is a reminder that an enterprise or billing foothold at a utility is a staging point toward OT. Verify IT/OT segmentation and monitoring between business systems and SCADA, rotate any exposed credentials, and hunt for wiper staging and lateral movement toward control networks.

High Incident

CISA, NSA and partners: attackers are actively modifying internet-exposed automatic tank gauge (ATG) systems

A joint CISA/NSA/FBI/EPA fact sheet warns that threat actors are compromising internet-exposed automatic tank gauge systems — the controllers that remotely monitor fuel and liquid levels, temperature and leaks — and altering them through command execution. The agencies describe access gained via authentication bypasses, hardcoded credentials, SQL injection and privilege escalation, after which an attacker can change tank settings, disable alarms, and falsify level readings, creating operational disruption or safety hazards. ATGs sit across the Energy, Chemical, Food & Agriculture and Transportation sectors, and are a classic example of small field devices casually put on the public internet. The remedy is blunt: get ATGs off the internet, put them behind a VPN or firewall, and replace default and hardcoded credentials with strong unique passwords.

Claroty's Team82 disclosed a set of severe, highly exploitable vulnerabilities in equipment that keeps data centers running — power-supply devices and HVAC controllers — centered on a chain in the widely deployed Trane Tracer SC+ automation controller that could allow unauthenticated remote code execution and full external takeover of a building-management system. All findings were responsibly disclosed to Trane and Vertiv, which shipped fixes before publication, so the immediate action is to patch and to stop treating building-automation and facility-power controllers as harmless IT peripherals. Inventory Tracer SC+ and comparable BMS/power controllers, apply vendor updates, and pull their management interfaces off any untrusted network.

CISA adds actively-exploited DELMIA Apriso MES flaws to KEV — the manufacturing-execution layer between ERP and the shop floor

On 25 June CISA added two Dassault Systèmes DELMIA Apriso vulnerabilities to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog after confirming active exploitation: CVE-2025-6205, a missing-authorization flaw (CVSS 9.1) that can hand an attacker privileged access, and CVE-2025-6204, a code-injection flaw (CVSS 8.0). Apriso is a manufacturing execution system that orchestrates production, quality and warehouse operations and sits squarely between enterprise ERP and plant-floor OT, so a compromise threatens both production data and a credible pivot toward controllers and HMIs. Releases 2020 through 2025 are affected — the same product line CISA previously flagged via CVE-2025-5086. Patch to a fixed release immediately, pull any internet-facing Apriso instances behind the firewall, hunt for web shells and unauthorized privileged access, and review what OT and shop-floor systems the MES tier can reach.

Max-severity Ubiquiti UniFi OS chain exploited in the wild — full root takeover of network gear that underpins many OT and building sites

CISA confirmed a three-flaw chain in Ubiquiti UniFi OS is being exploited in the wild and added it to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 23 June. The chain — CVE-2026-34908 (improper access control), CVE-2026-34909 (path traversal) and CVE-2026-34910 (improper input validation), each rated CVSS 10.0 — lets a network-adjacent attacker with no credentials reach arbitrary command execution, and researchers showed the three can be combined into full remote code execution with root privileges. UniFi gateways, switches and controllers are widely deployed at industrial plants, utilities, remote sites and commercial facilities, frequently sitting astride the IT/OT boundary and providing the routing, segmentation and remote access that OT depends on — so a root-level compromise here is a direct path to pivot toward control networks. Update UniFi OS now, pull management interfaces off the public internet, rotate credentials and sessions, and hunt connected OT segments for follow-on activity.

Schneider Electric PowerLogic P7 — unauthorized command execution and DoS on a power-grid protection & control relay

CISA advisory ICSA-26-176-07 covers three flaws (CVE-2026-9716, -9717, -9718) in Schneider Electric's PowerLogic P7, a protection-and-control platform for advanced electrical-network applications. The issues can allow unauthorized privileged command execution and a denial-of-service condition — including a NULL-pointer dereference that renders the device's HMI and configuration functions unavailable when malformed requests hit exposed network interfaces. Because P7 relays sit in substations and power-distribution schemes, loss of control or protection functionality carries direct safety and reliability implications for the energy sector. Schneider has released fixed firmware (V02.004.001); upgrade, and in the interim restrict network access to the P7 service endpoints (ports 8080 and 3702) and monitor for anomalous SOAP requests to the device.
