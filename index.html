SonicWall SMA1000 zero-days exploited — a CVSS 10 hole in the remote-access box many OT sites depend on

SonicWall is warning that attackers exploited two previously unknown flaws in its SMA1000 secure-access appliances before patches existed. CVE-2026-15409 is a CVSS 10.0 server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface that an unauthenticated remote attacker can use to force the appliance to make requests to unintended destinations; it is being chained with CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2), a post-authentication command-injection flaw in the Appliance Management Console. CISA added both to the KEV catalog on 14 July and set a 17 July deadline for federal agencies under BOD 26-04 — patch or pull the product. Affected models include the 6210, 7210 and 8200v. SMA appliances frequently broker vendor and engineer access into plant networks, which makes an unauthenticated foothold here an IT/OT boundary problem, not just an IT one.

Lantronix EDS5000 serial servers exploited in the wild — root on the box that sits between operators and the process

CISA added CVE-2025-67038, a critical command-injection flaw in Lantronix EDS5000 serial-to-IP device servers, to the KEV catalog on 23 June with a three-day federal remediation deadline. An unauthenticated attacker can inject operating-system commands through the username parameter and execute them as root. Forescout, which disclosed the flaw as part of its BRIDGE:BREAK research, observed a threat actor tagged Chaya_006 hitting honeypots with it as early as 5 April — after Lantronix had patched but before public disclosure. These converters bridge legacy serial equipment onto Ethernet in utility, industrial, transportation and healthcare networks, so root on one means an attacker can tamper with the data operators use to make decisions, or pivot deeper. Firmware 2.2.0.0R1 or later has been available since 20 February.

High Threat Research / Nation-State

AA26-194A: twenty nations detail how FSB Center 16 quietly owns routers — and how to shut it down

The joint advisory published alongside the UK/EU sanctions is the operational half of the story. NSA, CISA, the FBI and DC3 — with the UK NCSC, France’s ANSSI, Canada’s Cyber Centre, Australia’s ASD, New Zealand’s NCSC and other allied agencies — describe FSB Center 16 actors scanning broad IP ranges for internet-facing devices running SNMPv1 or SNMPv2 that still accept default or common community strings, then using that access to persist on routers across communications, defense industrial base, energy, financial services, government (especially state and local) and healthcare networks. High Threat Research / APT

Armored Likho targets electric power operators — with a loader that looks like an LLM wrote it

Kaspersky has detailed a newly named APT it tracks as Armored Likho, running cyber-espionage against government and electric power organizations in Russia, Brazil and Kazakhstan alongside financially motivated attacks on individuals. Initial access is spear-phishing: archives carrying executables or LNK files that show a decoy document while a loader pulls a Python interpreter and payload archive from GitHub repositories in the background. The payload is BusySnake Stealer, a Python infostealer that decrypts its own bytecode only at call time, harvests browser passwords, cookies, OTP keys and Telegram sessions, and stands up a reverse SSH tunnel for persistent interactive access. Kaspersky assesses the group’s activity overlaps with Eagle Werewolf. Researchers flagged loader samples containing verbose inline comments and redundant code blocks — stylistic tells consistent with LLM-generated code. For OT programmes the relevance is not the malware but the harvest: stolen engineer credentials and live SSH tunnels into a power operator’s IT estate are exactly how an adversary reaches the OT boundary without ever writing an exploit.

Block LNK execution from mail and archive extraction where possible, and treat any credential harvested from an engineer’s browser as an OT credential — rotate, enforce MFA, and stop password reuse between corporate and control-system accounts.

NERC CIP-012-2 took effect 1 July, tightening protection of control-center communications

NERC’s revised reliability standard CIP-012-2 became enforceable on 1 July 2026, sharpening requirements to protect real-time assessment and monitoring data exchanged between bulk-electric-system control centers. It applies to Balancing Authorities, Reliability Coordinators, Transmission Operators and Owners, and Generator Operators and Owners. Requirement R1 obliges responsible entities to document plans mitigating unauthorized disclosure and modification of that data in transit, loss of the ability to communicate between control centers, and the means to initiate recovery of those links — adding explicit confidentiality and availability protections over its predecessor. For North American electric entities the effective date is a hard milestone: those protections must be implemented and evidenced, not merely planned. The underlying principle travels well beyond NERC’s footprint — the links between control centers are critical assets in their own right.

Effective date — enforceable since 1 Jul 2026 NERC CIP-012-2 — bulk electric system
Compliance milestone Action: Confirm CIP-012-2 protections for control-center-to-control-center communications are implemented and evidenced for data in transit, update the required plan and responsible-entity documentation, and validate encryption, monitoring and recovery procedures on those links ahead of audit.

Policy Exercise / Resilience

EPA’s National Cyber Drill pulled the internet out from under water utilities — and 66 of 67 never proved they could go manual

The EPA’s 2026 National Cybersecurity Drill dropped participating drinking-water and wastewater utilities into a scenario built on Salt Typhoon reporting: a cyberattack degrades a tier-one telecommunications provider, and utilities must run for three days with no SCADA remote connectivity to outstations, no cloud services, no email and no VoIP. More than 200 utilities and 390 participants joined. The EPA also offered a live-action module in which utilities would actually attempt the transition to manual operations rather than talk through it; 67 opted in, and just one confirmed it had carried the transition out. That single data point is the finding. Most utilities have a documented manual-operations procedure and have never executed it against a clock, which means the assumption underwriting every “we can always run it by hand” risk acceptance is, for practical purposes, untested. It also lines up with CISA’s CI Fortify push for operators able to run safely while isolated for weeks to months.

Policy Regulation

Australia’s enhanced CIRMP rules are in force, adding AI, legacy-system, supply-chain and insider duties

Australia’s enhanced Critical Infrastructure Risk Management Program rules commenced on 10 June 2026, moving the regime from broadly principles-based risk management toward more prescriptive expectations about how specific risk categories must be identified, assessed and managed. The rules introduce a two-tier structure separating baseline CIRMP requirements from new enhanced requirements, and apply across critical electricity, gas, liquid fuel, water, energy-market-operator, freight infrastructure and services, broadcasting and domain-name assets. Grace periods matter for planning: additional material risks, certain cyber risks and personnel access management must be met by 10 June 2027, with remaining obligations due 10 June 2028. The themes — AI, legacy systems, supply chain and insider risk — are a reasonable preview of where other jurisdictions are heading, which makes the rules worth reading even outside Australia.

In force since 10 Jun 2026 — first compliance deadline 10 Jun 2027 Australia SOCI Act — critical electricity, gas, water, fuel, freight and more
Compliance milestone (forward-looking) Action: Australian responsible entities should gap-assess their CIRMP against the enhanced requirements now, with particular attention to AI, legacy-system, supply-chain and personnel-access duties due 10 June 2027. Operators elsewhere can use the rules as a preview of prescriptive expectations likely to appear in their own jurisdictions.
