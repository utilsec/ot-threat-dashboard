Ransomware halts all US production at Coca-Cola’s Fairlife dairy

Coca-Cola disclosed in a 16 July SEC 8-K that its Fairlife dairy subsidiary suffered a ransomware event involving unauthorized access to a portion of its systems, including production-related systems, and has temporarily suspended production at all of its US facilities. As of 17 July no group had claimed the attack and Coca-Cola had not confirmed data theft.

High Nation-State / Active Exploitation

NSA, CISA, FBI and 12 allied nations warn Russia’s FSB Center 16 is harvesting configs from critical-infrastructure routers

A joint advisory (AA26-194A, “Improve Router Hygiene”) attributes a long-running campaign to Russia’s FSB Center 16 — the actor also tracked as Berserk Bear / Dragonfly / Energetic Bear. The actors scan for internet-exposed networking devices running SNMP with default or weak community strings, then issue SNMP writes abusing the Cisco config-copy MIB to exfiltrate device configurations over TFTP; they also opportunistically exploit Cisco Smart Install and known device CVEs. Stolen configs expose network topology, credentials and VPN details — ideal reconnaissance for deeper access. Named sectors most at risk include Energy, Communications, Defense Industrial Base, Financial Services, Government (especially state/local) and Healthcare. For OT teams, the perimeter routers and firewalls fronting plant networks are exactly the under-audited devices in scope.

Active — agency-confirmed, opportunistic exploitation of exposed devices No single CVE — config/protocol abuse (SNMP, Smart Install) plus known CVEs
Mitigations available — disable Smart Install, move to SNMPv3 authPriv Action: Inventory routers/switches/firewalls fronting OT and remote sites. Disable Cisco Smart Install where unused, replace SNMPv1/v2c and default community strings with SNMPv3 (authPriv), and block SNMP/TFTP from untrusted networks. Pull device configs off internet reachability, review them for exposed credentials, and rotate any that were reachable.
Suggested SLA: Audit & harden edge networking devices — 14 days Owner:

Critical Active Exploitation

SonicWall SMA 1000 zero-days exploited in the wild — a favorite OT remote-access on-ramp under attack

SonicWall confirmed attackers are exploiting two zero-days in its SMA 1000 secure-access appliances: CVE-2026-15409, a critical (CVSS 10) server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface reachable by an unauthenticated remote attacker, and CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2), a post-authentication OS command-injection flaw in the management console. Rapid7’s MDR team observed the bugs used for stealthy initial access on internet-facing appliances — harvesting credentials, active session databases and TOTP MFA seeds. CISA added both to its KEV catalog on 14 July with a 17 July federal remediation deadline. SMA-class VPN/remote-access gateways are a common bridge into OT networks, and stolen sessions and MFA seeds are exactly what enables the credential-driven, living-off-the-land pivots defenders keep seeing.

Active — confirmed in-the-wild exploitation (Rapid7 MDR) CVE-2026-15409 & -15410 in CISA KEV (added 14 Jul 2026)
Patch available from SonicWall Action: Patch SMA 1000 appliances immediately (federal KEV deadline was 17 July) and treat any exposed appliance as potentially compromised — rotate all credentials, invalidate active sessions and reset TOTP/MFA seeds. Hunt for anomalous access and confirm the appliance cannot reach OT segments directly.

PTC Windchill RCE exploited in the wild — web shells landing on a manufacturing PLM backbone

CISA added CVE-2026-12569, a critical (CVSS 9.3) remote-code-execution flaw in PTC Windchill and FlexPLM, to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog after the first confirmed in-the-wild exploitation. The bug stems from deserialization of untrusted data, and attackers are chaining it to deploy JSP web shells on exposed servers; PTC has warned of continued heightened threat activity even though patches shipped days earlier. Windchill is product-lifecycle-management software that sits deep in manufacturing engineering environments — holding CAD, BOM and process data and often bridging enterprise and plant networks — so a web shell here is a foothold with a clear line toward OT-adjacent assets.

Siemens SICAM 8 grid-automation devices — DoS flaws and insecure OPC UA defaults

CISA advisory ICSA-26-197-05 covers multiple vulnerabilities across Siemens SICAM 8 products, including SICAM A8000 and EGS device firmware used in substation automation and RTU roles. An HTTP-accessible debugging interface can let an authenticated attacker crash the web process for a denial-of-service condition (CVE-2026-54798 through -54801), and the affected application ships with a default configuration that disables all OPC UA security mechanisms — leaving that channel open to unauthorized access and control unless hardened. The advisory flags impact to the Energy and Critical Manufacturing sectors; Siemens directs customers to firmware V26.20 / V26.20.0 or later.

No confirmed exploitation reported as of this edition Not in CISA KEV — coordinated advisory (16 Jul 2026)
Firmware V26.20 / V26.20.0 or later Action: Identify SICAM 8 (A8000 / EGS) devices in your substation and RTU fleet and schedule the firmware update. Until then, restrict the HTTP/debug interface to management networks only and explicitly enable OPC UA security — do not rely on the shipped defaults. Confirm none of these devices are reachable from IT or the internet.

Regulation & Policy
Info Regulation / NERC CIP

NERC CIP-012-2 now in force — real-time data between control centers gets tighter protection

CIP-012-2 became effective 1 July 2026, strengthening requirements to protect real-time operational data exchanged between control centers against unauthorized disclosure, modification and loss of availability — closing gaps left by the earlier revision. It lands alongside CIP-003-9, whose enforcement began in April, which extends security-governance and vendor electronic-remote-access requirements into low-impact BES environments that historically saw minimal oversight. For registered entities the practical questions now are scope and evidence: which control-center links and low-impact assets are in play, and what documentation demonstrates compliance. Non-NERC operators can still borrow the intent — protect control-to-control communications and govern low-criticality remote access — as good practice.

Regulatory milestone — effective/enforcement dates, not a threat No CVE — NERC CIP standards update
Compliance item — no patch applicable Action: If you are a NERC-registered entity, confirm your CIP-012-2 control-center data protections are implemented and evidenced now that the standard is enforceable, and verify CIP-003-9 low-impact and vendor remote-access controls are in place. If you are not NERC-regulated, treat both as a free maturity benchmark for protecting inter-site control traffic and governing low-criticality remote access.
Suggested SLA: Confirm CIP-012-2 / CIP-003-9 compliance evidence — 30 days Owner:
