Max-severity SimpleHelp auth-bypass stays actively exploited as its federal KEV deadline lapses — the remote-support tool integrators use to reach OT is being used to drop malware

CISA added CVE-2026-48558, a CVSS 10.0 authentication-bypass flaw in the SimpleHelp remote monitoring and management (RMM) platform, to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 29 June with a federal remediation deadline of 2 July — a deadline that has now passed while exploitation continues. The bug lives in SimpleHelp's OpenID Connect flow: on servers configured for OIDC, the software fails to verify the cryptographic signature of identity tokens, so an unauthenticated attacker can submit a forged token with arbitrary identity claims and be granted a fully authenticated “Technician” session — effectively administrative control of the remote-access server, and in some configurations even bypassing MFA. Researchers report the flaw is already being used to drop two newly identified malware families, a Node.js loader tracked as TaskWeaver and a cross-platform infostealer dubbed Djinn. Internet scans put roughly 14,000 SimpleHelp servers online with an estimated 1,000 directly vulnerable. This matters for OT because RMM tools like SimpleHelp are exactly how many system integrators, OEMs and managed-service providers reach into plant, utility and building-automation networks for support — a compromised RMM server is a pre-authenticated path across the IT/OT boundary, and earlier SimpleHelp campaigns already landed in critical-infrastructure environments. Treat any SimpleHelp server as presumed-targeted: patch to the fixed release immediately, pull the console off the public internet, rotate technician credentials and OIDC secrets, and hunt SimpleHelp-connected OT endpoints for TaskWeaver/Djinn activity and unexpected sessions.

Cal Water finds no OT breach after Iran-linked Handala claimed it could cut the water supply — a textbook “claimed vs confirmed” case

California Water Service (Cal Water), one of the largest U.S. investor-owned water utilities, investigated a breach claimed by the Iran-linked group Handala and reported finding no evidence of activity in its operational-technology environment. Handala — which presents as hacktivist but is widely assessed to be a front for Iranian state operations — asserted it could have disrupted the water supply and leaked roughly 5 GB of data. Analysts who reviewed the dump found the group had actually reached a customer billing database (containing personal data) and an internal RTKBase NTRIP GPS caster used by field crews; neither system treats or distributes water, and independent assessments say nothing in the evidence supports the shutdown claim. The episode is a clean reminder that hacktivist “we could turn off your water” claims frequently outrun the actual access — but also that Handala's toolkit reportedly includes wipers and MBR-overwriting malware, so an IT-side foothold at a utility still warrants urgent containment and a hard look at IT-to-OT pathways. Verify OT segmentation and monitoring, confirm no bridge from breached IT systems to control networks, and reset exposed credentials.

Max-severity Ubiquiti UniFi OS chain exploited in the wild — full root takeover of network gear that underpins many OT and building sites

CISA confirmed a three-flaw chain in Ubiquiti UniFi OS is being exploited in the wild and added it to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 23 June. The chain — CVE-2026-34908 (improper access control), CVE-2026-34909 (path traversal) and CVE-2026-34910 (improper input validation) — lets a network-adjacent attacker with no credentials reach arbitrary command execution, and researchers showed the three can be combined into full remote code execution with root privileges. UniFi gateways, switches and controllers are widely deployed at industrial plants, utilities, remote sites and commercial facilities, frequently sitting astride the IT/OT boundary and providing the routing, segmentation and remote access that OT depends on — so a root-level compromise here is a direct path to pivot toward control networks. Update UniFi OS now, pull management interfaces off the public internet, rotate credentials and sessions, and hunt connected OT segments for follow-on activity.

Progress Kemp LoadMaster pre-auth RCE now seeing exploitation attempts — the load balancer that fronts many enterprise and OT-facing services

eSentire's Threat Response Unit reported that CVE-2026-8037 (CVSS 9.6), a pre-authentication OS command-injection flaw in Progress Kemp LoadMaster, began drawing active exploitation attempts on 29 June — days after a public proof-of-concept and detailed write-up landed. The bug sits in the appliance's API: unsanitized input to the “/accessv2” endpoint (an out-of-bounds heap read in a string-escaping routine) lets an unauthenticated attacker inject commands and run code as root. The same defect affects other Progress ADC products (ECS Connection Manager, ObjectScale Connection Manager and the MOVEit WAF). eSentire noted the attempts it saw failed and produced no post-compromise activity, but with a working PoC circulating, successful exploitation is expected to follow. LoadMaster and ADC gear commonly sit at the network edge in front of web, VPN, remote-access and sometimes OT-facing services, so a pre-auth root takeover is a strong pivot point toward internal and control networks. Apply Progress's fixed release now, restrict management/API access, and pull the appliance off any untrusted network.

Delta Electronics DVP12SE PLC exposes unauthenticated Modbus — anyone on the network can rewrite control logic, and no patch yet

CISA advisory ICSA-26-181-07 (30 June) warns that the Delta Electronics DVP12SE programmable logic controller exposes its Modbus TCP service on port 502 with no authentication or access control, so any reachable network source can read and write coils, holding registers, relay states and process-control functions without credentials or operator approval (CVE-2026-12818, CVE-2026-12819). A companion resource-exhaustion issue lets an attacker flood the Modbus port to degrade the device. In practice this means an attacker with network line-of-sight can remotely issue commands, change operational values and interfere with control logic — the classic insecure-by-design exposure that makes flat OT networks and internet-reachable PLCs so dangerous. All versions are affected and Delta is still working on a fix, so mitigation is compensating controls: enable the PLC's built-in IP Filter, segment and firewall the controller off any untrusted network, and never expose Modbus/502 to the internet.

New 2 July CISA ICS batch: unauthenticated takeover of Gardyn IoT Hub exposes a Food & Agriculture connected-grow platform

CISA's newest ICS advisory batch (ICSA-26-183-01/-02/-03, published 2 July) leads with ICSA-26-183-03 covering the Gardyn IoT Hub, tagged to the Food & Agriculture sector (CVE-2026-13768, CVE-2026-55726, CVE-2026-54477). The core weakness is a privileged “iothubowner” key exposed on the device: an unauthenticated attacker who obtains it can call the IoT Hub registry-manager function and pull connection details for every Gardyn Home Kit and Studio unit, enabling access to and control of the managed devices across the fleet. Gardyn says it has already updated its cloud-side IoT Hub infrastructure to remediate and is pushing firmware to devices that stay internet-connected; CISA reports no known public exploitation so far. The broader lesson for critical-infrastructure operators is the recurring one from cloud-managed OT/IoT: a single over-privileged provisioning credential can expose an entire connected fleet, so confirm any internet-facing IoT/OT hub auto-updated, and review how registry/provisioning keys are scoped. Confirm Gardyn devices took the update and verify no unexpected device enrollments.

NERC CIP-012-2 now in effect (1 July 2026) — tighter protection for control-center data

The revised NERC CIP-012-2 standard became enforceable on 1 July, strengthening requirements to protect the confidentiality and integrity of real-time assessment and monitoring data exchanged between control centers and closing gaps the earlier version left around how that inter-control-center traffic is secured. For bulk-electric-system operators it is an immediate compliance milestone; for everyone else it is a useful reminder that control-center-to-control-center links are a prime target and deserve encryption, integrity protection and monitoring regardless of regulatory scope.
