Top Story

Ransomware reaches fairlife’s production systems — Coca-Cola stops US dairy manufacturing

Coca-Cola confirmed on 16 July that a third party gained unauthorised access to part of its fairlife dairy subsidiary’s environment, including production-related systems, in a ransomware event. US production is suspended; Canadian operations are running, and the company says product quality and safety are unaffected. No group has claimed the attack and Coca-Cola has not said whether data was taken. The reason this matters to OT defenders is the pattern, not the victim: a well-resourced food and beverage manufacturer still stopped lines. Ransomware rarely speaks ICS protocols — it lands on the ERP, MES, virtualisation and identity infrastructure that scheduling, batching and quality release depend on, and the plant halts anyway. How long the outage lasts is decided long beforehand by segmentation, credential separation and whether operators can still run manually.

Confirmed — company-acknowledged ransomware with production impact; no group attribution yet No CVE identified publicly — initial access vector not disclosed
Incident response ongoing — no patch applicable Action: Treat this as a tabletop you did not have to pay for. Confirm which IT systems your production genuinely cannot run without (ERP, MES, historian, batch scheduling, label/QA release), verify those dependencies are segmented and cannot be reached with a single set of compromised domain credentials, test that operators can run the process manually for a defined period, and check that OT backups are offline and restorable — not just replicated.
Suggested SLA: IT-to-OT dependency review & manual-run validation — 30 days Owner:

Critical Incident / Active Exploitation

SonicWall SMA1000 zero-days exploited — a CVSS 10 hole in the remote-access box many OT sites depend on

SonicWall has confirmed that attackers exploited two previously unknown flaws in its SMA1000 secure-access appliances before fixes existed. CVE-2026-15409 is a CVSS 10.0 server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface that an unauthenticated remote attacker can use to make the appliance issue requests to unintended destinations; it is being chained with CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2), a post-authentication command-injection flaw in the Appliance Management Console. CISA added both to the KEV catalog on 14 July and set a 17 July deadline for federal agencies under BOD 26-04 — patch or stop using the product. Affected models include the 6210, 7210 and 8200v. SMA appliances routinely broker vendor and engineer access into plant networks, which makes an unauthenticated foothold here an IT/OT boundary problem rather than an IT-only one.

Active — confirmed zero-day exploitation before patch availability CVE-2026-15409 & CVE-2026-15410 in CISA KEV (added 14 Jul 2026)
Hotfixes available (12.4.3-03453 / 12.5.0-02835) Action: Locate every SMA1000 (6210 / 7210 / 8200v), apply SonicWall’s hotfix now or take the appliance out of service, then hunt for compromise — unexpected outbound requests from the appliance, new admin accounts, anomalous Work Place sessions. Rotate credentials, re-issue MFA seeds, and re-verify which OT segments the appliance can actually reach.

High Threat Intelligence / Sysdig

JADEPUFFER: Sysdig documents an intrusion an AI agent appears to have run end to end

Sysdig’s threat research team has published what it assesses to be the first ransomware operation driven from start to finish by an LLM agent. Initial access came through CVE-2025-3248, an unauthenticated remote code execution flaw in Langflow, an open-source framework for building LLM applications. From there the agent pivoted to a production MySQL host running Alibaba Nacos, escalated, moved laterally, established persistence and encrypted 1,342 Nacos service configuration entries before deleting the originals. The detail worth noting is behavioural rather than technical: the agent recovered from its own failures without a human — one sequence went from a failed login to a working fix in 31 seconds. Nothing here manipulates a physical process, but the economics of attacking exposed, unloved infrastructure just changed.

Confirmed in-the-wild intrusion analysed by Sysdig; agentic attribution is the researchers’ assessment CVE-2025-3248 (Langflow RCE) — previously KEV-listed and widely exploited
Patched upstream — Langflow and Nacos fixes available Action: Find the AI/analytics tooling that has quietly appeared next to your OT data — Langflow, Nacos, notebooks, MLOps and dashboard services on plant-adjacent or DMZ hosts are commonly stood up outside change control. Inventory them, get them off the internet, patch CVE-2025-3248, and make sure the service accounts they hold cannot reach the historian or control network. Speed of response is now the differentiator, not attacker patience.

High Threat Intelligence / Rapid7

Rapid7: SMA1000 intruders are taking session databases and MFA seeds, not just shells

Rapid7’s MDR team discovered the two SonicWall SMA1000 zero-days in live incidents and has published what the attackers did once inside. The pair are used together for quiet initial access; the operators then harvest high-value credentials, active session databases and TOTP multi-factor seed configurations to guarantee they can come back — including after the appliance is patched. For OT this is the part teams miss: patching the box closes the hole but does not evict an adversary holding valid sessions and the seeds needed to mint MFA codes for the accounts your integrators and engineers use to reach the plant. Assume identity compromise, not just appliance compromise.

Active — TTPs observed in live incident response by Rapid7 MDR Same CVEs as the SonicWall advisory — KEV-listed 14 Jul 2026
Patch available — but insufficient on its own Action: After patching, invalidate every active session on the appliance, force a password reset for all accounts that authenticated through it, and re-enrol TOTP seeds — a patched appliance with stolen seeds is still an open door. Prioritise vendor, integrator and engineering accounts with paths into OT. Then hunt backwards through appliance logs for the credential and session-database access described in Rapid7’s write-up.
Suggested SLA: Session invalidation & MFA re-enrolment — immediate, alongside patching Owner:

Critical Advisory / Siemens ProductCERT

Siemens July Patch Tuesday: a CVSS 10 authentication bypass, plus criticals in Simatic S7-1500 and Desigo CC

Siemens published nine advisories on 14 July, six of them covering critical-rated issues. The headline is a token invalidation flaw in Opencenter X scored CVSS 10.0, which lets an attacker bypass authentication and take full control of the application. Critical vulnerabilities were also patched or mitigated in Mendix, Sidis Secured SmartPlug, Simatic S7-1500, Cadra and Desigo CC — many rooted in third-party components, and exploitable for denial of service, code execution, data disclosure and privilege escalation. The S7-1500 and Desigo CC entries are the ones to triage first: controllers and building management supervision, not engineering desktops.

For Simatic S7-1500, confirm firmware levels, restrict access to the controller network, and schedule the fix into the next maintenance window with a rollback plan; do not force a controller update outside change control on the strength of a score alone.

Critical Advisory / Rockwell Automation

Rockwell patches 12 advisories — unauthenticated CLI access on 1715 Redundant IO and three critical Logix DoS flaws

Rockwell Automation released 12 advisories on 14 July, two covering critical issues. In the 1715 Redundant I/O product, an unauthenticated attacker can reach intrusive CLI commands — enough to read or delete files, stop tasks, change I/O states and modify memory. That is direct process consequence, not information disclosure. Separately, three critical denial-of-service flaws in CompactLogix, ControlLogix, Compact GuardLogix and GuardLogix controllers can be driven to a major non-recoverable fault, published by CISA as ICSA-26-197-06. High-severity fixes cover Flex 5000 Adapter, FactoryTalk DataMosaix, FactoryTalk Services Platform, Arena, ThinManager, Studio 5000 Logix Designer, 1756-EN, 1734 POINT I/O and 1719-AENTR. Redundant I/O and safety controllers exist precisely to avoid unplanned trips.

No confirmed exploitation reported at time of publication Not in CISA KEV

For the Logix DoS set, a major non-recoverable fault means a manual trip and recovery, so route it through your safety and change process, not the IT patch cycle. In the meantime, enforce CIP Security / access restrictions and confirm controller networks are not routable from the enterprise.

High Advisory / Schneider Electric

Schneider Electric: code execution in IGSS SCADA and an authentication bypass in EcoStruxure Cybersecurity Admin Expert

Schneider Electric issued two advisories on 14 July. The first is a high-severity flaw in IGSS — its Interactive Graphical SCADA System — where a specially crafted file can be used to execute arbitrary code, the classic engineering-workstation path in which a project or configuration file becomes the payload. The second is a high-severity authentication bypass in EcoStruxure Cybersecurity Admin Expert that a local attacker can use to compromise the devices it manages. The second one deserves a moment: CSAE is the tool used to administer security on Schneider devices, so a bypass there is a compromise of the control plane you rely on to enforce everything else.

No confirmed exploitation reported at time of publication Not in CISA KEV

High Policy / Attribution & sanctions

EU and UK sanction Russia’s cyber ecosystem together for the first time, naming FSB Centre 16

On 13 July the EU adopted what it calls its largest cyber sanctions package, listing nine individuals and four entities — intelligence officers, criminals, hacktivists and the companies enabling them — and did so in parallel with the UK, which sanctioned 24. The EU publicly identified the 16th Centre of Russia’s FSB as the controller of multiple threat clusters including TURLA, and cited infiltration of government networks and sabotage of critical infrastructure affecting France, Germany, Poland, Cyprus, the Netherlands, Austria, Slovakia, Romania and Finland, alongside Ukraine. Sanctions do not patch anything. What they do is confirm, at state level, that the campaigns your intelligence feeds have been describing against energy, water and telecom infrastructure are institutional and sustained.

Medium Regulation / US defence industrial base

Pentagon suspends CMMC Phase 2 and opens a 60-day review of the whole programme

In a 13 July memo signed by DoD CIO Kirsten Davies, the department suspended Phase 2 of the Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification programme — the stage that would have required third-party assessments on all contracts involving sensitive but unclassified information from 10 November 2026 — and stood up a CMMC Reform Task Force to report back within 60 days. The arithmetic drove it: roughly 100,000 defence industrial base companies still needed assessments against only about 100 available assessors. Phase 1 self-assessment requirements, in force since last November, remain. For defence-adjacent manufacturers this buys time on certification, but the underlying NIST SP 800-171 obligations in existing contracts have not moved.

Regulatory change — no threat activity associated Phase 1 self-assessment requirements remain in force
Compliance programme change Action: Do not stand down the work. If you were preparing for a C3PAO assessment, keep the SSP, POA&M and evidence current — the contractual 800-171 requirements are unchanged and the review may reinstate assessments on a different timeline. Redirect budget that was earmarked for assessment scheduling into the control gaps that assessment would have exposed, particularly on the OT side, where CUI on shop-floor systems is routinely out of scope on paper and in scope in reality.
Suggested SLA: Programme re-plan — 30 days; re-check at task force report (~mid-Sep 2026) Owner:

Info Regulation / CIRCIA

CIRCIA final rule slips to September 2026 — but the 72-hour clock is statutory and will not move

CISA now expects to publish the final Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act rule in September 2026, having previously targeted May and missed a statutory October 2025 deadline set by Congress. The agency ran a series of town halls earlier this summer, drawing more than 1,200 critical infrastructure stakeholders, largely on the criticism that the 2024 draft conflicts with reporting obligations operators already carry. When it lands, covered entities across all 16 sectors — electric, water, chemical, manufacturing among them — must report covered incidents within 72 hours and ransomware payments within 24. Those clocks are written into the statute itself; no amount of rulemaking softens them.

Forward-looking — final rule expected Sep 2026 Applies across all 16 critical infrastructure sectors once effective
Regulatory timeline — no technical action Action: Use the slip. Decide now who is authorised to declare a reportable incident at 3am on a Sunday, and whether your OT incident process can produce the facts CIRCIA wants inside 72 hours — most OT programmes cannot, because impact assessment depends on engineers who are not in the IR call. Map CIRCIA against the reporting you already owe NERC, TSA or your state regulator so you file once, not four times, under conflicting definitions.
Suggested SLA: Reporting-readiness gap review — before final rule publication (Sep 2026) Owner:

Info Policy / Vulnerability coordination

White House launches ‘Gold Eagle’, an AI-driven clearinghouse for prioritising vulnerabilities

The White House announced Gold Eagle on 14 July: a clearinghouse that consolidates software vulnerability findings from government and industry, prioritises the most consequential, and coordinates remediation across critical infrastructure. It was established under June’s executive order on advanced AI innovation and security, and is run with Treasury, DHS/CISA and the Department of War alongside industry partners. The stated driver is volume — AI models are finding flaws faster than the community can triage them. It is already taking intake and coordinating scanning verification. Nothing here creates an obligation for asset owners. The open question for OT is whether cross-sector prioritisation surfaces industrial flaws or buries them beneath higher-volume IT findings.

Programme launch — no new mandate or reporting obligation on asset owners Cross-sector initiative established under EO 14409 (2 Jun 2026)
Policy / coordination initiative Action: No action required this week. Track whether Gold Eagle output becomes an additional feed alongside ICS-CERT and vendor PSIRTs rather than a replacement, and keep your PSIRT subscriptions intact — OT advisories have historically been under-represented in cross-sector prioritisation schemes, where a CVSS 10 in a niche PLC loses to a mid-severity flaw in ubiquitous IT software every time.
