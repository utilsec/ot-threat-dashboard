Nidec confirms ransomware at its Taiwan subsidiary as Blackfield demands $2M and claims 2TB of stolen data

Japanese industrial motor and components maker Nidec has confirmed a ransomware attack on its Taiwanese subsidiary, Nidec Chaun Choung Technology (Nidec CCI), with the Blackfield crew demanding roughly $2 million and claiming it exfiltrated more than two terabytes of corporate data spanning employee, financial, procurement, manufacturing and IT records. Medium Incident / Nation-State

Mandiant confirms Iran-linked Handala breach of California Water Service stopped short of OT — despite the crew’s disruption boast

California Water Service (Cal Water), one of the largest U.S. investor-owned water utilities, said Mandiant’s investigation into the breach claimed by Iran-linked group Handala found no evidence of activity in its IT or OT environments. Handala — widely assessed as a front for Iranian operations — had leaked ~5GB of data and asserted it could have disrupted the water supply, implying deep ICS access. Mandiant instead traced the activity to unauthorized access of a small number of user accounts across two third-party service-provider platforms, one active customer’s online account via stolen credentials, and an external GPS-correction site; no billing or payment systems and no control systems were reached. The case is a clean example of separating a threat actor’s psychological claims from verified impact — while underscoring that third-party and customer-facing accounts remain a soft entry point around OT.

Internet-facing ICS plateaus near 170,000 — but Bitsight says the real attack surface is still widening

Bitsight’s 2026 Global State of ICS/OT Exposure finds the count of internet-facing industrial control systems has flattened at roughly 170,000 devices seen monthly — a number defenders might read as progress. The report’s warning is that the risk profile is expanding anyway: modern controllers increasingly speak general-purpose protocols such as SSH, HTTP and MQTT alongside legacy fieldbus protocols, so each exposed device offers more reachable services and a broader attack surface than the raw count implies. For asset owners the takeaway is that exposure management can’t stop at “is this device on the internet?” — it has to account for which services and protocols each controller exposes and whether any of them are reachable from untrusted networks.

No patch — exposure & attack-surface management Action: Run an external exposure sweep of your own IP ranges for ICS protocols and management services (including SSH/HTTP/MQTT on controllers), remove or gateway anything internet-reachable, and track exposed-service counts — not just device counts — as a program metric.

Critical Advisory

Progress Kemp LoadMaster: pre-auth RCE (CVSS 9.6) now under active exploitation at the network edge

A critical OS command-injection flaw, CVE-2026-8037 (CVSS 9.6), lets an unauthenticated remote attacker run arbitrary commands on Progress Kemp LoadMaster load balancers by reaching the appliance’s API. Because LoadMaster typically sits at the outer edge of enterprise and IT/OT boundary networks, a successful hit hands attackers a direct, credential-free foothold into the environment. Progress disclosed the bug on 4 June and shipped a fix; functional proof-of-concept code appeared on 29 June, and security firms and H-ISAC reported exploitation attempts beginning the same day. This is exactly the kind of edge-appliance flaw OT teams should treat as an IT/OT-boundary emergency, since a compromised balancer can become a pivot toward control networks and remote-access paths.

Patch available (Progress) Action: Apply Progress’ fixed LoadMaster build immediately; restrict the management API to trusted networks; put the appliance behind access controls and monitoring; and hunt for signs of exploitation and any lateral movement toward OT or remote-access segments.

High Advisory

Daktronics controller flaws could let attackers hijack highway signs and billboards

CISA advisory ICSA-26-176-04 covers three vulnerabilities in Daktronics controller firmware (VFC-DMP-5000, DMP-5000 and DMP-8000) that, chained, could give a remote attacker root-level control over what the signs display — via path traversal, an unrestricted file upload and a weak default administrative account. While tampering with roadway signs and billboards is more disruption-and-disinformation than process safety, spoofed traffic messages carry real public-safety and public-trust consequences, and root on an internet-exposed controller is a serious foothold. Daktronics released patched firmware in March; the risk is concentrated in units left exposed with default credentials. Transportation and outdoor-display operators should update firmware and replace default passwords.

No confirmed exploitation reported — unauthenticated root if exposed Daktronics DMP-5000 / DMP-8000 — transportation

Australia’s enhanced CIRMP rules expand critical-infrastructure obligations to AI, legacy and supply-chain risk

Australia’s Cyber and Infrastructure Security Centre unveiled enhanced Critical Infrastructure Risk Management Program (CIRMP) rules that broaden what responsible entities must address — explicitly folding in AI-related risk, aging legacy systems, supply-chain dependencies and insider threats. For operators inside or trading with Australia’s SOCI regime, it is a prompt to revisit risk-management programs so these categories are documented and managed, not assumed. More broadly it reflects a global regulatory drift toward naming specific systemic risks — legacy OT, third parties, AI — rather than relying on generic “reasonable steps” language, and gives asset owners elsewhere a useful checklist for their own OT risk programs.

Policy Guidance

NIST publishes SP-1339 OT Backup Quick Start Guide to speed industrial recovery

NIST released SP-1339, an OT Backup Quick Start Guide aimed at helping industrial operators build backup and recovery practices tuned to control-system realities — where restoration can involve physical processes, safety interlocks and firmware provenance, not just re-imaging a server. It is practical, actionable guidance rather than a mandate, and it dovetails with the recurring incident-response lesson that OT recovery fails when backups aren’t OT-aware or offline-tested. Asset owners can use it as a template to validate that controller configurations, logic and engineering-workstation images are captured, protected and recoverable — and to rehearse recovery before an incident forces the issue.
