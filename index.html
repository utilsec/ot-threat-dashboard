Max-severity Ubiquiti UniFi OS chain is under active exploitation — full root takeover of the network gear that underpins many OT and building sites

The most urgent item this week is a three-flaw chain in Ubiquiti UniFi OS that CISA confirmed is being exploited in the wild and added to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 23 June, with a 26 June federal remediation deadline that has now passed. The chain — CVE-2026-34908 (improper access control), CVE-2026-34909 (path traversal) and CVE-2026-34910 (improper input validation / OS command injection), each rated at the maximum CVSS 9.8–10.0 — lets a network-adjacent attacker with no credentials reach arbitrary command execution, and Bishop Fox researchers showed the three can be chained into full remote code execution with root privileges. UniFi gateways, switches and controllers are widely deployed at industrial plants, utilities, remote sites and commercial facilities, frequently sitting astride the IT/OT boundary and providing the routing, segmentation and remote access that OT depends on — so a root-level compromise of this layer is a direct path to pivot toward control networks. Treat any internet-reachable UniFi device as presumed-targeted: update UniFi OS now, pull management interfaces off the public internet, rotate credentials and admin sessions, and hunt the connected OT segments for follow-on activity.

Medium Incident

Forescout honeypot catches pro-Russia TwoNet manufacturing a fake water-utility “victory”

Forescout's Vedere Labs documented how the pro-Russia hacktivist persona TwoNet breached one of its OT honeypots — a decoy water-treatment HMI — using default credentials it had deliberately left accepting, then posted screenshots to Telegram framing the access as a real attack on a U.S. water utility before abandoning the channel. Inside, the actors ran Metasploit reconnaissance, attempted setpoint changes via Modbus function codes, and probed S7Comm/S7Comm-Plus on adjacent decoy PLCs, showing basic but real multi-protocol OT fluency. Forescout says it's the first time an actor publicly claimed an attack that actually landed on one of its honeypots. The lesson cuts two ways: exposed HMIs with default credentials remain trivially compromised, and hacktivist “we hit a water utility” claims are routinely inflated — defenders should verify before reacting and get internet-facing HMIs off the public net.

Medium Incident

Cal Water: Mandiant finds no IT or OT breach after Iran-linked Handala's water-disruption claim

California Water Service disclosed that the Iran-aligned hacktivist group Handala gained unauthorized access to a small set of accounts on third-party service-provider platforms and leaked data tied to utilities serving Bakersfield, Visalia and Chico. Google's Mandiant investigated and concluded the activity was limited to a GPS-correction server and a billing database and did not reach Cal Water's own IT or OT environments or affect service, despite Handala's public claim it could disrupt the water supply. The episode is a clean reminder that hacktivist “we breached a water utility” claims routinely outrun the facts — and that fast, evidence-based forensics and messaging matter as much as containment when nation-state-aligned actors target the water sector through supply-chain access.

Research Grid / DER

“Harmonic swarm” research weaponizes distributed energy resources against the grid

Emerging research highlighted around the S4 conference describes a “harmonic swarm” attack that abuses centralized control over software-defined smart inverters in distributed energy resources. By retuning inverter parameters to inject coordinated high-frequency signals — reportedly above the roughly 3 kHz range that conventional protection relays monitor — an attacker could drive grid-wide oscillations and electrical stress on substation transformers while staying beneath the threshold that trips standard protection. The broader point for asset owners: as generation shifts from mechanical machines to fleets of internet-connected, software-controlled inverters, the grid becomes programmable and the aggregate cyber control of many small DER assets becomes a systemic risk that traditional relay protection wasn't designed to catch. It's a forward-looking threat model, not a confirmed in-the-wild incident, but it argues for treating DER aggregation platforms and inverter management as critical OT.

Research Protocol Risk

Claroty Team82: 1990s LonTalk protocol now exposed over IP is a quiet attack surface in building management systems

Claroty's Team82 published research on LonTalk, a 1990s building-automation protocol still embedded in legacy BMS across commercial real estate, retail, hospitality and data centers. The concern is the move to carry LonTalk over IP via CEA-852: gateways and servers designed for isolated serial networks are now reachable from enterprise networks and, in some cases, the internet. Team82 says it identified vulnerabilities and design weaknesses in the IP implementation that create remote attack paths into exposed BMS gateways, and an internet scan turned up a meaningful number of exposed controllers. For owners of large facilities, the practical step is to inventory LonTalk/CEA-852 devices, get them off any internet-reachable path, and segment building-automation networks away from both IT and core OT.

PTC Windchill RCE under active exploitation — the PLM backbone that holds engineering drawings and pivots toward the shop floor

On 25 June CISA added a critical (CVSS ~9.3) improper-input-validation flaw enabling unauthenticated remote code execution in PTC Windchill PDMLink/FlexPLM to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog, with a 28 June federal remediation deadline. Reporting indicates attackers continued dropping web shells onto unpatched, internet-reachable instances after the deadline passed. Windchill is the product-lifecycle-management hub for automotive, aerospace, defense and other discrete manufacturers, so a compromise exposes engineering drawings, bills of material and supplier data — and gives an attacker a credible pivot toward connected OT and shop-floor systems. Treat any internet-facing Windchill/FlexPLM instance as presumed-targeted: patch immediately, hunt for unexpected JSP files and web shells, remove internet exposure, rotate credentials, and review what OT and supplier data the PLM tier can reach.

Critical Advisory

Rockwell FLEX I/O EtherNet/IP adapters — unauthenticated web-password change (CVSS 9.4) plus a CIP denial-of-service

CISA advisory ICSA-26-167-05 (and Rockwell's own SD1775) covers two flaws in the widely deployed 1794-AENTR / 1794-AENTRXT FLEX I/O dual-port EtherNet/IP adapters. The more serious, CVE-2026-0647 (CVSS v3.1 9.4), lets an unauthenticated attacker change the device's web-interface password with a single crafted HTTP GET request — an account-takeover path to the embedded web server. The second, CVE-2026-0646, is a CIP-protocol memory-handling bug that can fault the adapter and drop its connection to attached I/O, requiring a manual reset. Rockwell found the issues internally and reports no known exploitation, but these adapters sit at Purdue Level 1/2 in critical manufacturing where an I/O outage means lost control. Update affected adapters from v2.012 to v2.013 and restrict EtherNet/IP and web-management access in the meantime.
