Max-severity SimpleHelp auth-bypass stays actively exploited past its federal KEV deadline — the remote-support tool your integrators and OEMs use to reach plant and substation networks

CISA added CVE-2026-48558, a CVSS 10.0 authentication-bypass flaw in the SimpleHelp remote monitoring and management (RMM) platform, to its KEV catalog on 29 June; the 2 July federal deadline has now lapsed while exploitation continues. On OIDC-configured servers SimpleHelp fails to verify identity-token signatures, so an unauthenticated attacker can forge a fully authenticated “Technician” session — administrative control of the remote-access server, in some configurations bypassing MFA — and attackers are already dropping the TaskWeaver loader and Djinn infostealer through it. Roughly 14,000 servers are online, an estimated 1,000 directly vulnerable. For electric utilities this is the vendor pathway: turbine OEMs, relay technicians and integrators use RMM to reach plants, substations and renewable-site SCADA, so a compromised server bypasses much of an electronic security perimeter's intent. Patch immediately, remove internet exposure, rotate technician credentials and OIDC secrets, ask your OEMs what RMM reaches your sites, and hunt for TaskWeaver/Djinn.

CISA/FBI/EPA/DOE: Iranian-affiliated actors keep hitting internet-exposed Allen-Bradley PLCs across the energy sector — with confirmed operational disruption

U.S. agencies continue to warn that Iranian-affiliated actors are exploiting internet-facing Rockwell Automation/Allen-Bradley programmable logic controllers across the energy, water and government-facilities sectors, a campaign running since at least March and re-emphasized amid heightened Middle East tensions. Unlike the many inflated hacktivist claims, this activity is agency-confirmed: reporting describes malicious interaction with project files, manipulation of data shown on HMI/SCADA displays, and in some cases real operational disruption and financial loss. The actors are reaching PLCs left exposed on the public internet, often protected only by default or weak credentials, rather than burning novel exploits. For power operators the exposure pattern is most common at the grid edge — small generation sites, balance-of-plant systems, pump storage and distributed-energy installations where controllers were put online for remote monitoring. Get every PLC and OT device off the public internet or behind a VPN with MFA, change all default/shared credentials, and hunt for unexpected configuration or logic changes on internet-reachable controllers.

Medium Incident

London Hydro discloses customer-data breach — a distribution utility's account portal exploited, meter information among the exposed data

London Hydro, the distribution utility serving more than 160,000 customers in London, Ontario, disclosed a data-security incident after detecting suspicious activity on a customer account on 18 June: the account was used to exploit a system vulnerability that allowed access to information about other customers. Potentially exposed data includes names, addresses, email addresses, phone numbers, account and billing numbers, service addresses, pricing plans, contract start dates and meter information; the utility says banking details, payment cards, dates of birth and government IDs were not involved, service delivery was unaffected, and the technical flaw was corrected the same day, with law enforcement notified, external forensics engaged and credit monitoring offered. This is an IT-side breach with no reported OT impact — but for utility defenders it is a useful reminder that customer-facing portals are attack surface too, that account, service-address and meter data is exactly what social-engineering and targeting campaigns feed on, and that billing and AMI-adjacent systems deserve a hard look for pathways toward the meter-data-management and head-end tiers. Verify your customer portal's authorization model can't leak across accounts, and review what a compromised portal identity could reach.

High Advisory

CISA flags auth-bypass and CSRF flaws in ST Engineering iDirect satellite terminals — the VSAT backhaul reaching remote substations and renewable sites

CISA published advisory ICSA-26-183-01 on 2 July covering ST Engineering iDirect Evolution iQ-Series, 3315-Series and 9-Series satellite terminals running software 4.5.2.1 and earlier — equipment deployed worldwide across the energy, communications and transportation sectors. The advisory describes two issues, reported with CVSS v3.1 scores of 8.6 and 8.1: REST API endpoints (including /api/identity) that respond without authentication, letting anyone with network access pull the terminal's serial number, Device ID, Terminal Private Key identifier, MAC address and exact firmware version — identifiers used for satellite-network authentication, raising terminal-impersonation and reconnaissance risk — and a cross-site request forgery weakness in which state-changing endpoints such as /api/reboot accept requests authenticated only by a session cookie, so a session-riding request can reboot the terminal and drop the link. No public exploitation has been reported to CISA. For utilities, VSAT terminals like these are the telemetry backhaul of last resort — remote substations, DER and solar sites, and metering points beyond fiber or cellular reach — so a rebootable, impersonatable terminal is a quiet way to blind SCADA visibility into exactly the sites operators check least. Inventory where satellite backhaul carries OT telemetry, update terminals to 4.5.2.2 or newer, and keep terminal management APIs unreachable from untrusted networks.

Schneider Electric EasyLogic T150 and Saitel DP RTUs expose stored credentials — the remote terminal units running substation and distribution automation

CISA advisory ICSA-26-181-04 (30 June) covers two insufficiently-protected-credentials flaws (CVE-2026-9650, CVE-2026-9651) in Schneider Electric's EasyLogic T150 (formerly Saitel DR) and Saitel DP remote terminal units — platforms deployed for substation automation, distribution automation and telecontrol in electric networks. The weaknesses allow an unauthenticated attacker who can access credentials stored within firmware or system files to gain unauthorized access and expose sensitive information — on devices that typically live inside a NERC CIP electronic security perimeter and speak directly to protection and switching equipment. Affected versions include EasyLogic T150 releases up to 11.06.30/11.06.31 and Saitel DP releases up to 11.06.35/11.06.37; Schneider has released fixed firmware (T150 11.06.32 via its Customer Care Center, Saitel DP 11.06.38), and a device reboot is required after updating. Schedule the firmware during a maintenance window, treat RTU credentials as potentially exposed until updated (rotate them), and verify these units are reachable only from your SCADA/telecontrol zones — never from corporate or public networks.

Critical Advisory

Delta Electronics DVP12SE PLC exposes unauthenticated Modbus — anyone on the network can rewrite control logic, and no patch yet

CISA advisory ICSA-26-181-07 (30 June) warns that the Delta Electronics DVP12SE programmable logic controller exposes its Modbus TCP service on port 502 with no authentication or access control, so any reachable network source can read and write coils, holding registers, relay states and process-control functions without credentials or operator approval (CVE-2026-12818, CVE-2026-12819). A companion resource-exhaustion issue lets an attacker flood the Modbus port to degrade the device. Delta PLCs of this class turn up across the power sector's edges — balance-of-plant skids, solar and storage integrations, auxiliary systems and packaged equipment supplied by third parties — often on flat networks where anything can reach port 502. All versions are affected and Delta is still working on a fix, so mitigation is compensating controls: enable the PLC's built-in IP Filter, segment and firewall the controller off any untrusted network, and never expose Modbus/502 to the internet.

High Advisory

New “CitrixBleed-pattern” NetScaler flaw exploited within 24 hours of disclosure — memory leakage from the remote-access gateways utilities sit behind

Citrix shipped fixes this week for six NetScaler ADC / NetScaler Gateway vulnerabilities, and the headline one — CVE-2026-8451 (CVSS 8.8), an insufficient-input-validation bug that causes a memory overread in the appliance's SAML handling — was being exploited in the wild less than 24 hours after public disclosure. Researchers at watchTowr showed that a malformed SAML authentication request padded with whitespace forces the parser to read past its buffer and return adjacent process memory to the client, echoing the CitrixBleed flaws whose leaked session tokens fueled major intrusions in 2023–24; honeypot operators observed live exploitation attempts beginning 30 June. Exposure requires the appliance to be configured as a SAML identity provider, which narrows the affected population but does not diminish the pattern: NetScaler gateways front VPN and remote-access services at control centers, plants and corporate utility networks that bridge toward OT, and leaked memory can include material that lets attackers hijack authenticated sessions. Apply the fixed builds now, confirm whether any NetScaler is configured for SAML, terminate active sessions and rotate credentials after patching, and watch authentication logs for anomalous SAML requests.

Policy Standard

NERC CIP-012-2 now in effect (1 July 2026) — tighter protection for control-center data

The revised NERC CIP-012-2 standard became enforceable on 1 July, strengthening requirements to protect the confidentiality and integrity of real-time assessment and monitoring data exchanged between control centers and closing gaps the earlier version left around how that inter-control-center traffic is secured. For bulk-electric-system operators it is an immediate compliance milestone: confirm your identified control-center-to-control-center links, the security protections applied to them, and the documentation an auditor will ask for. Even entities outside CIP scope should take the hint — control-center data links are a prime target and deserve encryption, integrity protection and monitoring regardless of regulatory obligation.

Policy Standard

FERC Order 919 starts the clock on virtualization-era CIP — eleven revised standards, compliance by 1 April 2028

FERC's Order No. 919, issued 19 March 2026 and effective 26 May 2026, approved eleven revised CIP reliability standards plus new and updated glossary definitions that finally bring virtualization into the CIP framework — letting bulk-electric-system entities secure virtualized EMS/SCADA, hypervisors and software-defined infrastructure under standards written for that architecture instead of shoehorning them into hardware-era definitions. The package sits alongside CIP-003-11 improvements for low-impact BES cyber systems (password protocols for remote users, intrusion detection) and CIP-002-8 changes to how Transmission Operator and Transmission Owner control centers are categorized. Compliance lands 1 April 2028, which sounds distant but is one architecture-refresh cycle away: utilities planning EMS upgrades, control-center virtualization or DMZ redesigns between now and then should sequence those projects against the new standards rather than retrofitting later. This is a forward-looking compliance item — the near-term action is planning, not remediation.
