Top Story

Russia’s FSB Center 16 is harvesting router configs across the energy sector — joint advisory names the grid

On 13 July, NSA, CISA, the FBI and partner agencies from a dozen countries issued joint advisory AA26-194A, attributing a long-running campaign against poorly configured routers to Russia’s FSB Center 16 (also tracked as Static Tundra, Berserk Bear and Dragonfly). The actors scan for routers exposing SNMP with default community strings and abuse Cisco Smart Install to copy device configurations off to attacker infrastructure — handing them network maps, credentials and VPN details. Energy is named among the most-targeted sectors, and CERT Polska has tied the same actor to the attempted December 2025 wiper attack on Poland’s grid. For power utilities the exposure is concrete: the edge routers linking substations, EMS and control centres are exactly the gear being harvested, and a stolen running-config is a blueprint of your OT network.

Active — ongoing state-sponsored exploitation of exposed routers NSA / CISA / FBI + 12 nations (AA26-194A)
Hardening, not a single patch — config & protocol hygiene Action: Disable Cisco Smart Install on every device; move off SNMPv1/v2 to SNMPv3 with authPriv and strong keys, or disable SNMP where unused. Pull internet-facing routers behind the OT boundary, rotate SNMP community strings and device credentials, and treat any exposed running-config as compromised intelligence. Review substation and control-centre routers first — they carry the most sensitive topology.
Suggested SLA: Smart Install / SNMP audit of internet-facing OT routers — 72h Owner:

Router Hygiene The device nobody audits is now the one being harvested

AA26-194A reframes the humble edge router as a strategic asset: default SNMP strings and legacy Cisco Smart Install let an adversary copy your entire running-config remotely. For grid operators that config names every substation, VLAN, VPN peer and credential. The uncomfortable part is that these devices usually sit outside the CIP asset inventory and the patch cycle, so the fix starts with finding them.

Pre-Positioning Reconnaissance is the campaign, not the prelude to it

Between the FSB router advisory and Dragos’ VOLTZITE reporting, the through-line is patient access: mapping engineering workstations, alarm logic and the conditions that trigger a shutdown, then waiting. Detection strategies tuned to look for the ‘attack’ miss the part that matters — the quiet enumeration that makes a future disruption cheap and fast.

Protection Relays SNMP keeps showing up where it shouldn’t

Schneider’s Easergy MiCOM advisory (hard-coded SNMP credentials) and the FSB campaign both point at the same neglected management plane. Protection relays and RTUs often ship SNMP enabled with weak defaults; on a substation LAN that is an information-disclosure path straight to device internals. Inventory which relays answer SNMP and lock the community strings down.

CISA ICSA-26-190-03
Compliance CIP-012-2 lands as the threat it addresses is live

CIP-012-2 took effect 1 July, tightening protection of real-time operational data moving between control centres — precisely the traffic an adversary who has mapped your routers would want. The timing is a useful forcing function: the standard now requires the encryption and integrity controls that the FSB campaign makes concrete rather than theoretical.

Critical Advisory — Critical

Rockwell 1715-AENTR EtherNet/IP adapter exposes an unauthenticated debug port — energy-flagged

CISA advisory ICSA-26-195-04 covers a Rockwell Automation 1715-AENTR EtherNet/IP adapter that exposes a network-accessible debug port without enforcing privilege controls, allowing unauthenticated remote access to intrusive CLI commands. CISA flags the affected sectors as Energy, Water and Wastewater, and Critical Manufacturing. On a substation or generation LAN, unauthenticated CLI to a redundancy adapter is a direct path to reading files, stopping tasks and altering I/O state.

No public exploitation reported yet CISA ICSA-26-195-04 (Rockwell)
Vendor firmware / mitigation available Action: Apply the Rockwell firmware update on 1715 redundant-I/O adapters; where you cannot patch immediately, block the debug/CLI port at the switch and confirm these adapters are not reachable from IT or remote-access segments. Verify no engineering path traverses the affected devices.

Info Regulation — NERC CIP

CIP-012-2 is now in force; CIP-003-9 tightens the low-impact and vendor-access perimeter

NERC CIP-012-2 became enforceable on 1 July 2026, strengthening protection of real-time operational data exchanged between control centres. It follows CIP-003-9 (effective 1 April 2026), which expands governance for low-impact BES Cyber Systems with a focus on vendor electronic remote access and supply-chain risk. Both map directly onto this cycle’s threats: control-centre data protection and third-party remote-access control.
