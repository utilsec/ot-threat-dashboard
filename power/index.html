Top Story

“FortiBleed” credential theft now feeds active ransomware — and those FortiGate firewalls front remote access into EMS/SCADA and substations

CISA warns the FortiBleed campaign has harvested working administrator and VPN credentials from tens of thousands of internet-facing FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways — leaked credentials tie to roughly 74,000 exposed Fortinet devices inside an operation that collected over 110 million credentials since mid-June. On 2 July researchers linked the stolen access directly to the INC Ransom and Lynx ransomware crews, with at least a dozen deployments traced to it. For electric utilities the exposure is immediate: these appliances terminate the remote access and IT/OT segmentation that EMS/SCADA, substations and the NERC CIP electronic security perimeter depend on. Because valid credentials leaked rather than a single bug being exploited, no patch closes the gap and an intruder signing in looks like a legitimate engineer — so treat any exposed FortiGate guarding grid OT as already compromised.

Active — confirmed exploitation; now feeding INC/Lynx ransomware (12+ deployments) CISA alert 18 Jun; DHS IoCs 2 Jul 2026
Mitigations available (Fortinet / CISA) Action: Treat every internet-facing FortiGate/SSL-VPN fronting grid OT as compromised: terminate active sessions, rotate all admin and VPN credentials, reissue certificates, enforce phishing-resistant MFA, remove management interfaces from the internet, and hunt for unauthorized config pulls using the DHS-shared IoCs.

Critical Active Exploitation

“CitrixBleed” returns: NetScaler flaw exploited within 24 hours, leaking secrets from gateways that front utility remote access

Citrix patched CVE-2026-8451 on 30 June — an unauthenticated memory-overread in NetScaler ADC and Gateway appliances configured as a SAML identity provider — and researchers saw in-the-wild exploitation within 24 hours, aided by a same-day public proof-of-concept. Like the original CitrixBleed, it lets a remote attacker read appliance memory and harvest live session tokens, credentials and TLS private keys. At many utilities NetScaler gateways terminate the remote access and IT/OT segmentation that reach control-center and substation networks, so leaked secrets can hand an attacker authenticated entry toward grid systems without touching a relay. Assume any internet-facing, unpatched NetScaler had its memory read after 1 July.

Active — exploited within 24h of disclosure; public PoC Citrix NetScaler ADC / Gateway (SAML IdP)
Patch available (Citrix, 30 Jun) Action: Patch NetScaler to the fixed build now, terminate all active sessions, and — assuming memory disclosure — rotate every session token, credential and TLS private key on the appliance; remove management interfaces from the internet and hunt for anomalous session reuse.

High Threat Campaign

New “Armored Likho” APT hits electric-power and government targets with apparently AI-generated malware

Kaspersky disclosed a previously undocumented espionage cluster it calls Armored Likho, targeting government agencies and electric-power organizations across Russia, Kazakhstan and Brazil. Intrusions begin with spear-phishing that drops a RAR archive and staged loaders pulling a new Python infostealer, BusySnake, which harvests browser credentials, cookies, OTP codes and Telegram sessions and opens reverse-SSH tunnels. Researchers assess the first-stage loaders were likely AI-generated — an early real-world signal of LLM-accelerated tooling reaching critical-infrastructure targeting. This is IT-side credential and espionage activity, not confirmed OT manipulation, but power-sector engineering staff are squarely in scope and stolen credentials are the usual bridge from business networks into control systems.

Critical Advisory

Siemens urges immediate patching of RUGGEDCOM switches as a critical SINEC OS flaw (CVSS 9.8) draws a public exploit

CISA advisory ICSA-26-188-05 (published 7 July) flags a critical CVSS 9.8 vulnerability in Siemens SINEC OS before V4.0, the operating system on RUGGEDCOM industrial networking gear. A memory-corruption bug in a bundled third-party component (GNU elfutils) lets a remote attacker compromise unpatched devices, and a public exploit has already been disclosed. RUGGEDCOM switches and routers are the hardened networking backbone in substations, generation plants and rail, sitting directly in the path that segments and moves control-system traffic. No confirmed in-the-wild ICS attacks are reported yet, but a public exploit plus a 9.8 score on widely deployed grid infrastructure warrants emergency patch prioritization.

Public exploit disclosed — no confirmed in-wild ICS exploitation yet Siemens ProductCERT (ICSA-26-188-05)
Vendor fix available (RUGGEDCOM RST2428P) Action: Inventory RUGGEDCOM devices running SINEC OS below V4.0, apply the Siemens update immediately, remove management interfaces from routable/internet access, restrict them to an isolated management network, and monitor for anomalous access to switch/router management.

High Advisory

Weak session tokens let attackers hijack management sessions across Schneider power relays, meters and RTUs

CISA advisory ICSA-26-169-07 covers CVE-2026-4827 (CVSS v4 8.2), an insufficient-entropy flaw that makes web-management session tokens predictable across a wide swath of Schneider Electric power gear — Easergy P3/P5 and T300 protection relays, EcoStruxure Power Monitoring Expert, PowerLogic ION and PM5000 meters, and Saitel DP/DR RTUs. A remote, unauthenticated attacker who observes a few tokens can predict valid ones and take over an authenticated console session, effectively bypassing login on devices at the heart of substation protection, power monitoring and remote telemetry. No in-wild exploitation is reported yet, but the affected list spans equipment deployed across electric utilities. Schneider has published firmware updates and mitigations.

No known in-wild exploitation as of this edition Schneider Electric CPCERT (ICSA-26-169-07)

High Advisory

Hard-coded credentials in Schneider Easergy MiCOM Px40 protection relays expose device data over SNMP

CISA advisory ICSA-26-190-03 (published 9 July) warns that Schneider Electric’s Easergy MiCOM Px40 series — protection relays deployed for medium-, high- and extra-high-voltage protection across substations and generation plants — contains hard-coded credentials (CWE-798) reachable through the device SNMP service. An unauthenticated attacker able to reach the SNMP port can interrogate a relay and retrieve sensitive device information, aiding reconnaissance and follow-on targeting of substation protection schemes. No in-wild exploitation is reported. Protection relays sit at the heart of grid fault clearing and equipment protection, so any unauthenticated exposure — even information disclosure — warrants tight access control. Schneider has published remediation and mitigations; where firmware can’t be updated immediately, restricting SNMP access is the priority.

No known in-wild exploitation as of this edition Schneider Electric via CISA (ICSA-26-190-03)
Remediation / mitigations available Action: Inventory Easergy MiCOM Px40 relays, apply Schneider’s remediation, disable or tightly restrict SNMP on protection relays, block SNMP at the substation boundary, move relay management to an isolated network, and monitor for unexpected SNMP queries.

Info Regulation

NERC CIP 2026 milestones hit: control-center data protection now effective, internal network monitoring phasing in

Several NERC CIP obligations reach milestones this year: CIP-012-2, protecting the confidentiality and integrity of real-time data exchanged between control centers, is now effective, while CIP-015-1 internal network security monitoring (INSM) for high- and medium-impact environments is phasing in, and FERC has directed expanding scope toward EACMS and PACS. For registered entities the direction is clear — more prescriptive duties around control-center communications, east-west visibility inside the electronic security perimeter, and faster documentation. Utilities should map current CIP posture against these milestones now rather than at the compliance deadline.
