FortiBleed: 430,000 Fortinet firewalls swept for 110M+ credentials, now tied to active ransomware — the remote-access doorway into utility OT

Researchers now tie the FortiBleed credential-theft campaign directly to active ransomware. On 2 July, SOCRadar reported the operation — which planted a custom sniffer on roughly 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls across 150+ countries and harvested more than 110 million credentials since mid-June — is feeding INC Ransom and Lynx intrusions, with at least a dozen ransomware deployments traced to the stolen access. For electric utilities the exposure is direct: these firewalls front remote access into EMS/SCADA, substations and the NERC CIP electronic security perimeter. Because valid credentials leaked rather than a single bug being exploited, no patch closes the gap, and an attacker signing in looks like a legitimate engineer. Treat any exposed FortiGate guarding OT as already compromised.

Active exploitation — leaked credentials now driving ransomware (INC / Lynx) Credential-exposure campaign (no single CVE)
No patch — credential rotation & access hardening Action: Treat every internet-facing FortiGate/SSL-VPN fronting OT as compromised: rotate all local and admin credentials, revoke and reissue certificates, terminate active VPN sessions, restrict the management interface to trusted hosts, enforce MFA, and hunt for unauthorized configuration changes.
Suggested SLA: Credential rotation & session reset on OT-facing devices — 72h Owner:

China-linked CL-STA-1062 deploys TinyRCT backdoor in electricity and water utilities across Southeast Asia

Palo Alto Networks Unit 42 reported in late June that a Chinese-speaking group it tracks as CL-STA-1062 has expanded from Taiwanese web-hosting targets into Southeast Asian critical infrastructure, compromising electricity and water utilities alongside government and military organizations. The group plants a lightweight C# backdoor named TinyRCT for persistence, command execution, file exfiltration and screenshotting, with a self-destruct feature to erase forensic evidence. For grid operators the takeaway is strategic pre-positioning: state actors are quietly establishing footholds in utility IT that can pivot toward OT, so hunt for stealthy persistence rather than waiting for disruption.

High Incident

U.S. agencies keep warning Iran-affiliated actors are logging into internet-exposed PLCs across energy and water

The April joint advisory AA26-097A from CISA, FBI, NSA, EPA, DOE and Cyber Command — that Iranian-affiliated actors are reaching internet-facing programmable logic controllers across energy, water and government facilities — remains an active campaign, re-emphasized amid Middle East tensions. For power operators the lesson maps onto distributed generation and auxiliary systems, where solar sites, storage and small substations increasingly expose controllers reachable from the public internet, often shielded only by default or shared credentials. The activity is agency-confirmed rather than a hacktivist boast and has produced real operational disruption. The remedy is hygiene: get controllers off the public internet, replace default credentials, and hunt for unexpected logic changes.

ELECTRUM's attack on Poland's distributed energy resources is the template for grid-edge risk

Dragos attributes a December 2025 attack on operational-technology systems across multiple Polish grid sites to ELECTRUM, calling it the first major cyberattack focused on distributed energy resources — wind, solar and combined-heat-and-power. The intrusion targeted the RTUs and communications managing dozens of smaller generation sites, precisely the edge that expands as grids add renewables. For North American operators building out DER it is a preview: the distributed edge multiplies remotely reachable controllers faster than most utilities can monitor them, and destructive intent is now demonstrated rather than theoretical.

CISA flags ST Engineering iDirect iQ-Series satellite terminals for the energy sector

Among six ICS advisories CISA released on 2 July (ICSA-26-183-01) was one for ST Engineering iDirect iQ-Series satellite terminals, called out for energy-sector use. Two API flaws let an unauthenticated attacker on the network read sensitive device data (CVE-2026-38059) and force a reboot via cross-site request (CVE-2026-38057), which repeated can sustain a link outage. Satellite backhaul is how many remote substations and DER sites phone home, so a vulnerable terminal is a foothold — and a denial-of-service risk — at the very edge of the grid. iDirect fixed it in software 4.5.2.2.

No confirmed in-the-wild exploitation as of this edition Not in CISA KEV
Fixed in 4.5.2.2 — update from ≤4.5.2.1 Action: Locate iDirect iQ-Series terminals used for remote-site backhaul, update to 4.5.2.2 or newer, restrict management interfaces to trusted networks, and monitor satellite-linked sites for anomalous access or unexpected reboots.

High Advisory

CISA's 7 July advisory cycle concentrates on the grid edge: Hydro-Québec EV-charging backend, Hitachi Energy PROMOD V and e-mesh EMS

On 7 July CISA published a run of ICS advisories heavy on power-sector gear. ICSA-26-188-01 covers the backend of Hydro-Québec's Le Circuit électrique EV-charging network, where an unauthenticated websocket endpoint, no throttling on login attempts and weak session expiry could allow privilege escalation or denial of service; Hydro-Québec has disabled OCPP on most stations and added authentication to mitigate. The same day brought advisories for Hitachi Energy PROMOD V (ICSA-26-188-02), which transmits data over plain HTTP in versions ≤1.0.10, and Hitachi Energy e-mesh EMS (ICSA-26-188-03), the platform that manages distributed energy, storage and microgrids. None are known to be exploited and vendor fixes or mitigations exist — but the widening grid edge of public charging, DER and energy-management platforms keeps generating advisories that need triage by exposure.

Standard Policy

NERC CIP-015-1 internal network security monitoring moves toward enforcement — blind spots inside the ESP close

With FERC's Order 907 approving NERC CIP-015-1, registered entities are building toward Internal Network Security Monitoring for high- and medium-impact BES Cyber Systems inside their Electronic Security Perimeters — a shift from perimeter defense to watching east-west traffic where intrusions like the grid-edge reconnaissance Dragos describes would show up. FERC has also directed NERC to extend the requirement to access-control and monitoring systems outside the ESP. Use the runway to deploy OT-aware monitoring, baseline normal control traffic, and close the visibility gap that leaves most incidents detected only by a hunch.

Compliance program — not a vulnerability NERC CIP (mandatory standard)
Program / compliance action Action: Stand up OT-aware internal network monitoring inside ESPs, baseline normal control-system traffic, define anomaly detection and response, and prepare evidence for CIP-015 implementation timelines.
