Top Story

Fourteen agencies attribute a decade-long router campaign to Russia’s FSB Center 16 — energy named as a target sector

NSA, CISA, the FBI and partners across twelve countries published joint advisory AA26-194A on 13 July, attributing more than a decade of targeting of poorly configured networking devices to FSB Center 16. The tradecraft is deliberately unglamorous: scan internet-facing ranges for SNMP agents that still answer to default or common community strings, issue commands from spoofed source addresses, then copy device configuration files out over TFTP to actor-controlled infrastructure. Cisco Smart Install is a second path. Energy is called out by name. For a utility, the value of a stolen router config is not the router — it is the map: substation addressing, management VLANs, ACLs, tunnel endpoints and the SNMP strings themselves. That is pre-positioning intelligence for a grid intrusion, gathered without touching a relay.

Active — decade-plus campaign confirmed by 14 agencies Joint advisory AA26-194A — FSB Center 16
No single patch — configuration hardening Action: Inventory every internet-reachable router, switch and cellular gateway carrying substation, EMS or DER traffic. Disable SNMPv1/v2c and move to SNMPv3 with authPriv; if a device cannot do v3, restrict SNMP to an out-of-band management path or turn it off. Disable Cisco Smart Install everywhere. Rotate community strings and device credentials on the assumption configs were taken, and diff running configs against a known-good baseline for unexpected ACL, tunnel or TFTP entries.
Suggested SLA: SNMP exposure review & config diff — 7 days (nation-state pre-positioning against energy) Owner:

Critical Incident — Active Exploitation

SonicWall SMA 1000 zero-days exploited in the wild; both added to KEV with a 17 July federal deadline

Rapid7’s MDR team observed targeted zero-day exploitation of internet-facing SMA 1000 appliances before SonicWall disclosed on 14 July. CVE-2026-15409 (CVSS 10.0) is an unauthenticated server-side request forgery that lets an attacker open a websocket tunnel to localhost-only services; CVE-2026-15410 chains from there, abusing a path-traversal remove_hotfix workflow on port 8188 to execute OS commands as root. CISA added both to the KEV catalog the same day. For transmission and generation operators, SMA-class appliances are frequently the front door for control-room remote access and for OEM support sessions into EMS, relay engineering and DER platforms. An attacker who owns the appliance inherits every session it brokers — including the vendor ones your change process treats as trusted.

On any indication of compromise, re-image and rotate every credential and MFA token the appliance touched — including OEM and integrator accounts.

High Threat Intelligence — Supply Chain

Chinese-made inverters draw a second front: European grid-risk analysis lands as Washington drafts an import ban

A report published on 11 July argues that with roughly 80% of new European solar installations depending on Chinese-made inverters, a hostile vendor pushing malicious firmware or abusing an undisclosed comms path could act at continental scale. It follows Reuters reporting that rogue communication devices were found inside some Chinese solar and battery inverters deployed in the US, and pv magazine’s 30 June account of a Trump administration ban being drafted; the FCC is separately weighing an import prohibition. WaterISAC also circulated an FBI report on inverter exposure on 9 July. Treat the specific “rogue device” claims as reported rather than independently confirmed — but the structural point stands. DER fleets are managed through vendor clouds and cellular backhaul that most utilities neither own nor monitor.

Critical Advisory — DER / EMS

Hitachi Energy e-mesh EMS carries a critical NGINX heap overflow — the software sitting on top of your DER fleet

CISA advisory ICSA-26-188-03 (7 July) covers a heap-based buffer overflow reaching e-mesh EMS through a bundled NGINX component, tracked as CVE-2026-42945 with a CVSS v3 base of 8.1 and a CVSS v4 base of 9.2. Successful exploitation could cause an application outage and may permit arbitrary code execution. Versions 4.1.6, 4.4.2 and 4.7.0 are affected. e-mesh EMS is the layer that monitors, dispatches and optimises distributed energy resources — solar, wind, battery storage — so the blast radius is not one asset but the coordination of many. Hitachi Energy’s companion advisory ICSA-26-188-02 for PROMOD V, a transmission planning tool, landed the same day and concerns cleartext handling of sensitive data.

No known public exploitation reported to CISA at advisory time Not in CISA KEV — ICSA-26-188-03
Hotfix available — updates bundled NGINX to v1.30.2 or later Action: Confirm which e-mesh EMS version your DER platform runs and apply the vendor hotfix through normal OT change control. Hitachi Energy also directs operators to verify the rewrite configuration does not use “?” to replace unnamed captures and that ASLR is active (value=2) on all deployment targets. Until patched, keep the EMS off any route reachable from the internet or the corporate domain.

High Advisory — RTU / Substation

Siemens SICAM 8 fix closes a firmware-update path that could plant a persistent backdoor on substation RTUs

Siemens published an advisory on 9 July covering four SICAM 8 vulnerabilities; CISA republished it as ICSA-26-197-05 on 16 July. The one that matters most is CVE-2026-54799 (CVSS v3 7.2), which turns the trusted firmware-update process itself into an attack vector — an attacker in a position to abuse it could install malicious firmware and hold persistent control of the device. The affected families are SICAM A8000, SICAM EGS and SICAM S8000, widely deployed as RTUs and gateways in substations and along distribution feeders. Fixes ship in CPCI85 firmware V26.20 and SICORE V26.20.0. A CVSS of 7.2 undersells the operational reality here: firmware persistence on a substation RTU survives the reboot, the re-image and, usually, the investigation.

No known exploitation reported at publication Not in CISA KEV — ICSA-26-197-05 / Siemens SSA
Patch available — CPCI85 V26.20 / SICORE V26.20.0 Action: Pull the SICAM 8 firmware inventory for A8000, EGS and S8000 estates and schedule the V26.20 update against your substation maintenance windows — do not wait for the next annual cycle. Until then, restrict who can reach the update interface: management access from an engineering jump host only, no flat paths from the corporate network, and alarm on unexpected firmware-version changes reported by your relay/RTU asset inventory.
Suggested SLA: Firmware plan agreed — 7 days; rollout — next maintenance window Owner:

CISA urges SharePoint hardening after fresh exploitation — four flaws added to KEV on 14 July

CISA published a SharePoint hardening alert on 14 July following new exploitation, and added four actively exploited vulnerabilities to the KEV catalog the same day: SonicWall’s CVE-2026-15409 and CVE-2026-15410 alongside Microsoft’s CVE-2026-56155 (AD FS privilege escalation) and CVE-2026-56164 (SharePoint missing authentication for a critical function, exploitable over the network). Federal agencies face remediation deadlines of 17 and 28 July. This is an IT story with an OT tail. On-premises SharePoint farms in utilities routinely hold single-line diagrams, relay setting files, NERC CIP evidence, switching orders and vendor contact lists — and AD FS is often the identity broker in front of the engineering applications that matter.

Medium Advisory — Protection Relays

Schneider Easergy MiCOM Px40 relays: hard-coded credentials expose device data over SNMP

CISA advisory ICSA-26-190-03 (9 July) covers CVE-2026-4832, a use-of-hard-coded-credentials issue rated CVSS v3 5.3 in the Easergy MiCOM Px40 protection relay series used across medium, high and extra-high voltage protection. An unauthenticated attacker able to interrogate the SNMP port can retrieve sensitive device information. Affected firmware: P14x prior to B4A, P24x prior to D3A, P341 prior to E3F, and P342/P343/P344/P345 prior to B3F. On its own this is an information-disclosure finding with a modest score. Read it next to this week’s FSB Center 16 advisory and it is something else: an SNMP-reachable protection relay handing over device detail to precisely the scanning tradecraft fourteen agencies just described.

No known exploitation reported at advisory time Not in CISA KEV — ICSA-26-190-03
Firmware available — see Schneider thresholds per model Action: Inventory affected relay models and firmware levels. Check whether SNMP is enabled and reachable from anywhere other than a protected management path — on most Px40 estates the honest answer is that nobody has looked in years. Restrict or disable SNMP, then plan Schneider-supported firmware upgrades through normal protection-settings change control with the relay engineer in the room.

Info Policy — Federal Posture

Acting CISA chief: plan for adversarial disruption of critical infrastructure rather than assume you can prevent it

Speaking at the ICS Village and Institute for Security and Technology Critical Effect conference, acting CISA director Nick Andersen said the US should expect “an adversarial disruption of our critical infrastructure” with significant technical and psychological impact, and that operators “are not going to be able to keep everything persistently online and available as much as we would like.” He argued assumptions should change accordingly — including planning on the basis that telecommunications infrastructure will be disrupted. For grid operators the message is not new, but the source is notable: the federal posture has moved decisively from prevention toward resilience. Andersen also expressed scepticism that new mandates are the answer, pointing instead to state and local grant funding and prioritised outreach.

Policy commentary — no threat activity CISA leadership remarks
No patch — strategy and planning Action: Test the assumption he named. If your telecom carrier dropped for 72 hours, what happens to SCADA polling, DER dispatch, protection communications and your ability to reach field crews? Document which of those degrade gracefully and which stop, and rehearse manual operation for the ones that stop — then take the answer to your board as a resilience number rather than a maturity score.
