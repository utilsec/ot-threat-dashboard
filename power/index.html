SonicWall SMA 1000 zero-days under active attack — the remote-access boxes fronting utility engineer and vendor sessions

SonicWall says it has investigated multiple cases confirming active exploitation of two Secure Mobile Access 1000 flaws. CVE-2026-15409 is a CVSS 10.0 server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface, reachable by an unauthenticated remote attacker; CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2) lets an authenticated administrator inject OS commands through the Appliance Management Console. CISA added both to the KEV catalog on 14 July with a 17 July federal remediation deadline. For electric utilities the concern is placement rather than the bugs themselves: SMA appliances commonly terminate the vendor-maintenance and engineer sessions that reach EMS/SCADA, substation networks and the NERC CIP electronic security perimeter. An attacker who lands on the appliance sits astride that boundary with legitimate-looking access, so patching alone is insufficient — assume exposure and hunt.

Patch available — 12.4.3-03453 / 12.5.0-02835 hotfix Action: Upgrade SMA 1000 to the fixed hotfix build immediately. Then run SonicWall’s forensic checks — look for /__api__/login or /__api__/logout requests returning HTTP 200 in extraweb_access.log, /wsproxy requests with suspicious host parameters (HTTP 101), hotfix rollbacks with path-traversal names in ctrl-service.log, and rogue login/logout routes in /var/lib/unit/conf.json. If any indicator is present, re-image the appliance, rotate all user and admin passwords and reset TOTP tokens.

High Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converter flaw exploited in the wild — the quiet boxes bridging legacy substation serial gear

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, one of the flaws disclosed in April as part of the BRIDGE:BREAK research project. Serial-to-IP converters are among the least-inventoried devices in a utility estate: they are frequently retrofitted to bring legacy relays, RTUs and meters onto Ethernet, then left in place for a decade without an owner, a patch process or a monitoring hook. That is exactly what makes them attractive — a converter typically sits inside the protected network with a clear path to the serial device it fronts, and compromise of the bridge means access to whatever is behind it. Exploitation is confirmed, and it follows an explicit OT threat warning, so treat this as an inventory problem before it is a patch problem.

Active — exploitation confirmed in the wild Lantronix serial-to-IP converters (BRIDGE:BREAK)
Vendor firmware available — see Lantronix advisory Action: Sweep for Lantronix and other serial-to-IP converters, including undocumented ones added during past retrofits — scan for the management ports rather than trusting the asset register. Remove any internet exposure, place converters behind a controlled segment, update firmware, replace default credentials, and add them to the patch cycle with a named owner.

High Vulnerability

BeyondTrust patches critical auth-bypass flaws in Remote Support and PRA — the tooling utilities hand to vendors

BeyondTrust disclosed two authentication-bypass vulnerabilities on 7 July affecting Remote Support (RS) and Privileged Remote Access (PRA). CVE-2026-40138 (CVSS 9.2) stems from improper validation of authentication data and could let a network-positioned attacker bypass access controls and reach the appliance, including elevated accounts; CVE-2026-40139 involves improper processing of RS authentication requests. Both hinge on a specific authentication configuration being enabled, which limits the exposed population. Neither has been observed under active exploitation and no public proof-of-concept exists. Cloud customers were patched on 21 April; self-hosted deployments running RS or PRA 25.3.2 or earlier need action. Privileged remote access is the standing route vendors and integrators use into utility OT, which is why a 9.2 pre-auth bypass in it deserves attention even without exploitation.

No known exploitation; no public PoC as of this edition Not in CISA KEV
Patched — RS/PRA 25.3.3+ or April rollup (cloud patched 21 Apr) Action: Confirm whether you run self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier and whether the affected authentication configuration is enabled. Apply the April security rollup or upgrade to 25.3.3+. Independently, review which vendors hold standing privileged access into OT, and whether those sessions are time-bounded, approved per-use and recorded.

Info Guidance

NIST publishes an OT backup quick-start guide aimed at cutting recovery time after an incident

NIST released SP 1339, an OT Backup Quick Start Guide intended to improve industrial cyber resilience and accelerate incident recovery. The framing is worth noting for utilities: the guidance treats backup as an operational-recovery problem rather than a data-retention one. In grid environments the assets that actually determine restoration time are rarely the file servers — they are relay settings files, controller logic, EMS/SCADA configurations, historian baselines and the engineering-workstation images needed to push any of it back. Those are precisely the artifacts most often missing, stale, or stored on the same domain as the systems they would restore. The guide is short-format and pitched at getting a workable baseline in place, which makes it a reasonable prompt to test whether your OT restore actually works rather than whether the backup job reports success.

Guidance publication — no threat activity NIST SP 1339
No patch — process and resilience guidance Action: Use SP 1339 as a checklist against your own OT restore path: confirm relay settings, controller logic, EMS/SCADA configuration and engineering-workstation images are backed up, held offline or otherwise isolated from the production domain, and — the part usually skipped — actually restored in a test at least once. Record the measured restore time and feed it into your recovery objectives.

Info Regulation

NERC CIP 2026 milestones keep tightening: low-impact controls, control-centre data protection, virtualization

The 2026 NERC CIP picture continues to move toward more prescriptive OT obligations. CIP-003-9, covering security management controls for lower-risk environments, took effect on 1 April 2026, and CIP-012-2 addresses the confidentiality and handling of real-time operational data exchanged between control centres. FERC has approved modifications improving baseline cybersecurity for low-impact BES Cyber Systems — including password requirements for remote users and intrusion detection for low-impact assets — alongside 11 updated CIP standards enabling the secure use of virtualization. A separate final rule directs NERC to address supply-chain risk in new or modified standards, extending coverage to certain network-connected equipment. The direction of travel is consistent: fewer exemptions for low-impact assets, and more explicit expectations for data in transit and virtualized infrastructure.

Regulatory milestones — no threat activity NERC CIP-003-9 / CIP-012-2 / FERC orders
No patch — compliance obligation Action: Map the 2026 milestones against your own CIP programme, paying particular attention to low-impact assets that historically sat outside detailed control expectations and to control-centre-to-control-centre data paths under CIP-012-2. Where virtualization is planned, use the updated standards to shape the design now rather than retrofitting evidence later.
