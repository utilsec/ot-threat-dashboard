Remote-access appliances under active attack: SonicWall SMA 1000 zero-days exploited; federal patch deadline passed 17 July

SonicWall has confirmed active exploitation of two Secure Mobile Access 1000 flaws, which Rapid7’s MDR team discovered being used against internet-facing appliances since at least late June. CVE-2026-15409 is a CVSS 10.0 unauthenticated server-side request forgery in the Work Place interface; CVE-2026-15410 (CVSS 7.2) lets an authenticated admin run OS commands via the Appliance Management Console, and attackers are chaining the two for full appliance takeover. CISA added both to the KEV catalog on 14 July; the federal fix-by date of 17 July has now passed. Water and wastewater utilities are disproportionately exposed here for structural reasons: remote access is not a convenience but the operating model. Lean teams cover distributed treatment plants and lift stations after hours, and integrators dial in for dosing, pump and SCADA support. When the appliance carrying that traffic is the thing being exploited, the compromise arrives wearing a valid session.

Patch available — 12.4.3-03453 / 12.5.0-02835 hotfix Action: Upgrade SMA 1000 to the fixed hotfix now — if you cannot, take it off the internet until you can. Then check SonicWall’s indicators: /__api__/login or /__api__/logout requests returning HTTP 200 in extraweb_access.log, /wsproxy requests with odd host parameters (HTTP 101), path-traversal hotfix rollbacks in ctrl-service.log, and rogue routes in /var/lib/unit/conf.json. On any hit: re-image, rotate every user and admin password, reset TOTP tokens. If a contractor manages the appliance, get written confirmation of the build — do not assume.

High Incident — Nation-State

WaterISAC reissues its heightened-threat advisory as US–Iran strikes hit water facilities and retaliation risk climbs

WaterISAC updated its heightened-threat situation report on 16 July, warning water and wastewater operators to brace for retaliatory Iranian cyber activity after kinetic escalation — reported US strikes on Iranian water pumping stations and an Iranian strike on a Kuwaiti desalination plant. It builds on the April 2026 joint advisory (AA26-097A) in which FBI, CISA, NSA and partners confirmed Iranian-affiliated actors have been exploiting internet-facing Rockwell/Allen-Bradley PLCs across water and energy since at least March, with some facilities suffering operational disruption. The campaign explicitly names the water sector and relies on exposed devices, default credentials and native engineering functionality rather than a single patchable CVE. Small utilities with internet-reachable PLCs are the softest targets and should assume they are in scope.

High Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converter flaw exploited in the wild — the forgotten boxes bridging pump and dosing gear

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, one of the flaws disclosed in April through the BRIDGE:BREAK research project. For water utilities this is a particularly awkward class of device. Serial-to-IP converters are how decades-old pump controllers, chlorine analysers, level sensors and remote lift-station gear got onto Ethernet in the first place — usually installed by an integrator during a retrofit, never added to an asset register, and never patched since. Compromising the bridge gives access to whatever sits behind it, and the device typically lives inside the network you consider protected. Exploitation is confirmed and follows an explicit OT threat warning. For most utilities the honest first step is discovery, not patching: you cannot fix converters you do not know you own.

Active — exploitation confirmed in the wild Lantronix serial-to-IP converters (BRIDGE:BREAK)
Vendor firmware available — see Lantronix advisory Action: Scan for serial-to-IP converters rather than trusting documentation — look for the management ports across plant and lift-station networks, including sites handed over by integrators years ago. Remove internet exposure first, then segment, update firmware and replace default credentials. Assign each device a named owner so it enters the patch cycle instead of leaving it again.

High Critical Advisory

CISA flags OpenPLC v3 for water and wastewater, alongside two Schneider energy advisories

CISA’s 9 July batch of three ICS advisories includes OpenPLC v3, explicitly flagged as used in Water and Wastewater Systems as well as Energy. The other two cover Schneider Electric Easergy MiCOM Px40 protection relays and PowerChute Serial Shutdown. OpenPLC deserves a moment of honesty from the sector: it is open-source soft-PLC software that turns up in small-utility deployments, pilot projects, training rigs and integrator proof-of-concepts — and, not infrequently, in things that quietly became production. Because it has no vendor account team to send you a notice, advisories against it tend to go unread by the people running it. Check the advisory for affected versions and confirm whether OpenPLC exists anywhere in your estate, including on hardware someone stood up temporarily.

No known exploitation reported OpenPLC v3 — Water/Wastewater and Energy

Critical Vendor Advisories

July ICS Patch Tuesday: critical DoS flaws in Rockwell Logix controllers, plus a Schneider IGSS SCADA code-execution fix

The July 2026 cycle carries two items with direct water-sector weight. Rockwell Automation published 12 advisories including three critical denial-of-service flaws in CompactLogix, ControlLogix, Compact GuardLogix and GuardLogix controllers that can force a major non-recoverable fault — in plain terms, a controller that stops and does not come back on its own. Allen-Bradley Logix hardware runs a large share of treatment-plant and pumping control, so that is a process-availability issue, not a data one. Schneider Electric separately fixed a high-severity flaw in IGSS, its SCADA product, where crafted files can execute arbitrary code. Siemens published nine advisories, six critical, including a CVSS 10 authentication bypass in Opencenter X and fixes for Simatic S7-1500. VDE CERT added five more covering Codesys and Wago among others. CISA formalised the Logix denial-of-service issues as ICSA-26-197-06 in its 16 July advisory batch, and separately flagged Rockwell’s 1715-AENTR EtherNet/IP adapter (ICSA-26-195-04) for Water and Wastewater Systems — worth checking against remote I/O on your plant networks.

No known exploitation reported for these advisories Siemens / Schneider / Rockwell / VDE CERT
Patches & mitigations available (all three vendors) Action: Match the Rockwell Logix DoS advisories against your controller inventory and identify where a non-recoverable fault would stop treatment or pumping — those sites set your patch priority. Where an outage window is hard to obtain, apply network-level compensating controls and document the accepted risk rather than letting it drift. Check IGSS installations against the Schneider advisory.

High Vulnerability

BeyondTrust patches critical auth-bypass flaws in the privileged remote access tooling integrators use

BeyondTrust disclosed two authentication-bypass vulnerabilities on 7 July in Remote Support (RS) and Privileged Remote Access (PRA). CVE-2026-40138 (CVSS 9.2) comes from improper validation of authentication data and could let a network-positioned attacker bypass access controls to reach the appliance, including elevated accounts; CVE-2026-40139 involves improper processing of RS authentication requests. Exploitation of both depends on a specific authentication configuration being enabled, which narrows the exposed population. Neither is under active exploitation and no public proof-of-concept exists. Cloud customers were patched on 21 April; self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier needs attention. Read this alongside the SonicWall top story: the common thread is that third-party remote access into plant networks is where the sector’s risk actually concentrates.

No known exploitation; no public PoC as of this edition Not in CISA KEV
Patched — RS/PRA 25.3.3+ or April rollup (cloud patched 21 Apr) Action: Determine whether you run self-hosted RS/PRA at 25.3.2 or earlier with the affected authentication configuration enabled, and patch. Separately, list every integrator and vendor with standing access into plant networks and check whether those sessions are time-bounded, individually approved and recorded — for most small utilities that list is longer and older than expected.

Regulation & Policy
Info Guidance

NIST publishes an OT backup quick-start guide — aimed squarely at utilities with no spare capacity

NIST released SP 1339, an OT Backup Quick Start Guide intended to improve industrial cyber resilience and speed incident recovery. The short-format framing is deliberate, and it suits the water sector better than most guidance does: the constraint at a small or mid-size utility is rarely awareness, it is that one or two people already carry compliance, engineering and operations. What the guide gets right is treating backup as a restoration problem. In a treatment plant the artefacts that determine recovery time are PLC logic, HMI and SCADA configurations, historian baselines, dosing setpoints and the engineering-workstation image needed to push any of it back — not the file server. Those are the things most often missing, stale, or sitting on the same domain as the systems they are meant to restore.

Guidance publication — no threat activity NIST SP 1339
No patch — process and resilience guidance Action: Use SP 1339 as a checklist rather than a project: confirm PLC logic, HMI/SCADA configuration and engineering-workstation images are backed up, held offline or otherwise isolated from the production domain, and restored in a test at least once. Record the measured restore time — that number, not the backup success rate, is what your recovery objective should be built on.
