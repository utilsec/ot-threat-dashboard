Nation-state actors keep breaching water utilities through internet-exposed PLCs — no malware required

Fresh threat-intelligence reporting warns that state actors from Iran, Russia and China are repeatedly breaking into water and wastewater utilities not with novel malware but by walking through internet-exposed PLCs, HMIs and unsegmented remote access secured with default or shared credentials. Canada's Communications Security Establishment disclosed that a pro-Russia hacktivist group claimed it breached a Quebec municipal water plant and could covertly manipulate chlorine dosing and pump pressure — a claim CSE has not independently verified. Whether or not that specific boast holds, the pattern is now a deliberate feature of state competition, and water's thin staffing and flat networks make it the softest target in critical infrastructure. The fix is unglamorous: get controllers off the public internet, kill default credentials, segment OT from IT, and alarm on setpoint changes.

Confirmed pattern — agency + research reporting; one claim unverified Nation-state (Iran / Russia / China)
No patch — exposure reduction & credential hygiene Action: Inventory every internet-facing PLC and HMI, pull them behind a VPN with MFA, eliminate default and shared credentials, segment OT from IT and the internet, and alarm on unauthorized setpoint or logic changes to dosing and pumping.

China-linked CL-STA-1062 hits water and electricity utilities with the new TinyRCT backdoor

Palo Alto Networks Unit 42 reported in late June that a Chinese-speaking group, CL-STA-1062, compromised water and electricity utilities plus government and military targets across Southeast Asia, planting a lightweight backdoor called TinyRCT for persistence, command execution and stealthy exfiltration, complete with a self-destruct to erase evidence. For water operators it is a reminder that nation-state pre-positioning starts in ordinary business IT — billing, historians, remote-access jump hosts — then looks for a path into treatment OT. Hunt for quiet persistence and prove your IT compromise cannot reach the plant.

High Incident

FortiBleed credential theft now feeds ransomware — the VPN into your SCADA is the exposure

SOCRadar reported on 2 July that the FortiBleed campaign — a sniffer harvesting 110M+ credentials from some 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls since mid-June — is now driving INC Ransom and Lynx deployments. Many water and wastewater utilities front their remote SCADA access and vendor connections with exactly these appliances. Because credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, there is no patch to apply: an attacker logs in as a trusted operator. Assume any exposed FortiGate touching your OT network is compromised and rotate now.

Active exploitation — credentials feeding ransomware (INC / Lynx) Credential-exposure campaign (no single CVE)
No patch — credential rotation & access hardening Action: Rotate all credentials and certificates on internet-facing FortiGate/SSL-VPN devices touching OT, terminate active sessions, restrict management to trusted hosts, enforce MFA, and hunt for unauthorized logins and config changes.

Iran's CyberAv3ngers keep targeting water-sector PLCs — default credentials remain the way in

The April joint advisory AA26-097A confirmed Iranian IRGC-linked actors, operating as CyberAv3ngers, are actively reaching Rockwell and Unitronics programmable logic controllers at U.S. water plants using default or weak factory credentials — the same playbook behind the 2023 Aliquippa incident. The campaign is ongoing and agency-verified, not a one-off. Small utilities that expose Unitronics or Vision-series HMIs to the internet with unchanged defaults are the prime targets; the countermeasure is to remove that exposure and replace factory passwords everywhere.

Living-off-the-land is reshaping OT incident response — and engineering-led defense is the answer

Analysts report that adversaries in industrial environments increasingly avoid custom malware in favor of legitimate tools and valid accounts, making intrusions blend into normal operations and defeating signature-based detection. For water utilities, where fewer than one in ten OT networks has any monitoring, that means an attacker manipulating a dosing setpoint may look identical to a technician. The recommended shift is engineering-led: operators who know what normal process behavior looks like become the detection layer, backed by OT-aware monitoring and validated safe-state controls.

Technique trend — not a single vulnerability Living-off-the-land / valid accounts
No patch — monitoring, baselining, engineering review Action: Deploy OT-aware monitoring, baseline normal process and network behavior, involve process engineers in anomaly review, and ensure independent safety instrumentation can enforce a safe state regardless of the control network.

iDirect iQ-Series satellite terminals flagged — the backhaul at remote lift stations and tank sites

CISA's 2 July advisory (ICSA-26-183-01) covers ST Engineering iDirect iQ-Series satellite terminals, with two API flaws allowing an unauthenticated attacker to read device details (CVE-2026-38059) or force reboots that sever the link (CVE-2026-38057). Water and wastewater systems use satellite backhaul at remote pump stations, tanks and outfalls where fiber and cellular do not reach, so a vulnerable terminal is both a foothold and a denial-of-communications risk at unmanned sites. iDirect has released fixed software 4.5.2.2.

No confirmed in-the-wild exploitation as of this edition Not in CISA KEV

CISA and EPA keep pressing water systems to pull HMIs off the public internet

CISA and EPA guidance continues to warn that internet-exposed human-machine interfaces let attackers view and alter treatment operations directly — the exact vector pro-Russia hacktivists used to max out setpoints and lock out operators at U.S. utilities. The remediation is foundational and unchanged: remove HMIs from public exposure, put remote access behind a VPN with MFA, and inventory what is reachable from the internet. It remains the single highest-leverage step a small utility can take.

Guidance — not a vulnerability CISA / EPA guidance
Program / exposure-reduction action Action: Run an external scan for exposed HMIs and controllers, remove them from the public internet, route all remote access through a VPN with MFA, and adopt the CISA/EPA exposure-reduction checklist.
