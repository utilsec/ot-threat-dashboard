Federal alert sharpens: Iran-affiliated hackers are actively manipulating water-system controllers

Federal agencies escalated their warning that Iran-affiliated actors are actively manipulating the controllers that run US water and wastewater systems. Advisory AA26-097A, updated 22 July, confirms hackers have reached internet-exposed PLCs — Unitronics, Rockwell and others — extracted project files, and rewritten or deleted control logic, in some cases disabling shutdown and alarm functions so equipment can drift into unsafe states without alerting operators. For a small or mid-size utility this is the sharpest version of a familiar problem: the devices being hit are ordinary dosing, pump and lift-station controllers left reachable from the internet with default or shared passwords. The campaign has already caused operational disruption across the sector. The fix is unglamorous — pull controllers behind a firewall, replace default credentials, and require MFA on every remote path.

Active — confirmed hands-on-OT logic manipulation in the water sector FBI/CISA/NSA/EPA — AA26-097A
No single patch — remove PLCs from internet, fix credentials Action: Enumerate every PLC/HMI reachable from the internet (start with Unitronics and Rockwell) and pull it behind a firewall or VPN; change the default ‘1111’ and any shared passwords; require MFA on remote OT access; back up PLC logic and hash it against known-good to detect tampering; review logs against the July 2026 IOCs.

High Incident — Nation-State

FSB Center 16 router campaign (AA26-194A): exposed edge gear in front of municipal water SCADA

A joint advisory from NSA, CISA, the FBI and ~12 partner nations (AA26-194A, 13 July) details how Russia’s FSB Center 16 — Static Tundra / Berserk Bear / Energetic Bear / Dragonfly — scans for devices exposing SNMP with default community strings and abuses Cisco Smart Install to copy device configs. State and local government is named explicitly, which is what most municipal water utilities are. The same internet-facing edge routers sit in front of treatment-plant and lift-station SCADA, and a stolen config is a ready-made map before an intruder touches a controller.

Active — ongoing FSB scanning and config theft NSA/CISA/FBI + ~12 nations — AA26-194A
No single patch — disable Smart Install, lock down SNMP Action: Disable Cisco Smart Install where not in use; remove SNMP from internet-facing interfaces; replace default/public community strings and move to SNMPv3; restrict management to a dedicated admin network; confirm no configs were exfiltrated.

High Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converter flaw exploited in the wild — the forgotten boxes bridging pump and dosing gear

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, disclosed via the BRIDGE:BREAK research. For water utilities this is an awkward class of device: serial-to-IP converters are how legacy pump controllers, chlorine analysers, level sensors and remote lift-station gear reached Ethernet — usually installed by an integrator, never inventoried, never patched. Compromising the bridge gives access to whatever sits behind it. Exploitation is confirmed and follows an explicit OT threat warning, so the honest first step is discovery, not patching.

Active — exploitation confirmed in the wild Lantronix / BRIDGE:BREAK / SecurityWeek
Vendor firmware available — see Lantronix advisory Action: Scan plant and lift-station networks for serial-to-IP converters rather than trusting documentation; remove internet exposure first, then segment, update firmware and replace default credentials; assign each device an owner so it enters the patch cycle.
Suggested SLA: Discovery & exposure remediation — 72h; firmware — 30 days Owner:

“Boiling Point”: state actors treat US water systems as a standing strategic target

A DomainTools “Boiling Point” analysis frames the strategic picture behind the tactical alerts: Iran-, Russia- and China-aligned actors have deliberately elevated water and wastewater systems from opportunistic nuisance to a standing strategic target across 2024–2026, with Russia-aligned groups assessed as most willing to directly manipulate water controls. The payoff is largely psychological and political — water sits at the intersection of public health and public trust. The exploited weaknesses are the ordinary ones: exposed HMIs/PLCs, default accounts, legacy controllers and thin monitoring.

Critical Active Exploitation — KEV

FortiSandbox RCE flaws added to CISA KEV — exploited appliances on the utility IT/OT boundary

CISA added two critical Fortinet flaws, CVE-2026-39808 and CVE-2026-25089 (both CVSS 9.1), to the KEV catalog on 16 July after evidence of in-the-wild exploitation. They are OS command-injection bugs in FortiSandbox / Cloud / PaaS allowing unauthenticated command execution via crafted HTTP requests. Fortinet appliances often sit on the boundary between a utility’s IT and its plant OT; a compromised inspection appliance is both a foothold and a blind spot. CISA set a 19 July federal remediation deadline.

Active — exploitation confirmed CISA KEV — added 16 Jul 2026

High Critical Advisory

OpenPLC v3 advisory flagged for water & wastewater systems

On 9 July CISA published an advisory covering OpenPLC v3, explicitly flagged for Water and Wastewater Systems as well as Energy. OpenPLC is an open-source soft-PLC used in small deployments, labs and some production settings; where it runs unmanaged or internet-reachable it inherits the same exposure risks as any controller. No exploitation is reported, but any water utility running OpenPLC should treat this as a patch-and-isolate item.

No known exploitation CISA ICS advisory (9 Jul)
Update available — see advisory Action: Identify any OpenPLC v3 instances, apply the update, and confirm they are segmented from the internet and corporate IT.

EPA’s 2026 National Cyber Drill tests whether water still flows when the network doesn’t

The EPA launched its 2026 National Cybersecurity Drill challenging drinking-water and wastewater utilities to keep critical functions running when telecom and internet access are degraded or gone. The scenario forces operations in a “disconnected” state — limited or no SCADA remote connectivity, cloud services, email or VoIP — to test manual fallback and sector resilience. Participation is a low-cost way to pressure-test whether a utility can run blind.
