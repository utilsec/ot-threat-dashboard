CISA/FBI/EPA/DOE: Iranian-affiliated actors keep hitting internet-exposed Allen-Bradley PLCs — with confirmed operational disruption

U.S. agencies continue to warn that Iranian-affiliated actors are exploiting internet-facing Rockwell Automation/Allen-Bradley programmable logic controllers across water and wastewater, energy and government-facilities sectors, a campaign running since at least March and re-emphasized amid heightened Middle East tensions. Unlike the many inflated hacktivist claims, this activity is agency-confirmed: reporting describes malicious interaction with project files, manipulation of data shown on HMI/SCADA displays, and in some cases real operational disruption and financial loss. The actors are reaching PLCs left exposed on the public internet, often protected only by default or weak credentials, rather than burning novel exploits — which makes the fix largely one of hygiene and exposure reduction. Get every PLC and OT device off the public internet or behind a VPN with MFA, change all default/shared credentials, and hunt for unexpected configuration or logic changes on internet-reachable controllers.

Cal Water finds no OT breach after Iran-linked Handala claimed it could cut the water supply — a textbook “claimed vs confirmed” case

California Water Service (Cal Water), one of the largest U.S. investor-owned water utilities, investigated a breach claimed by the Iran-linked group Handala and reported finding no evidence of activity in its operational-technology environment. Handala — which presents as hacktivist but is widely assessed to be a front for Iranian state operations — asserted it could have disrupted the water supply and leaked roughly 5 GB of data. Analysts who reviewed the dump found the group had actually reached a customer billing database (containing personal data) and an internal RTKBase NTRIP GPS caster used by field crews; neither system treats or distributes water, and independent assessments say nothing in the evidence supports the shutdown claim. The episode is a clean reminder that hacktivist “we could turn off your water” claims frequently outrun the actual access — but also that Handala's toolkit reportedly includes wipers and MBR-overwriting malware, so an IT-side foothold at a utility still warrants urgent containment and a hard look at IT-to-OT pathways. Verify OT segmentation and monitoring, confirm no bridge from breached IT systems to control networks, and reset exposed credentials.

Dragos documents an AI-assisted intrusion at a Mexican water utility — commercial LLMs used to navigate an unfamiliar OT environment

Dragos case-study reporting describes an intrusion at a Mexican water utility in which the adversary leaned on commercial large language models to interpret an operational-technology environment they had apparently never seen before — using AI assistance to make sense of unfamiliar control-system software and to build credential lists for further access. The case has become the OT community's concrete reference point for the “zero-knowledge” threat actor debate: attackers may no longer need ICS-specific expertise up front when AI tooling can supply enough context on the fly, compressing the time between first access and meaningful OT reconnaissance. For water utilities — where thin security staffing is the norm — the takeaways are conventional but urgent: strong unique credentials and MFA on every remote path, OT network monitoring capable of flagging an intruder who is learning the environment as they go, and logging that lets you reconstruct what an interactive adversary actually touched.

Actively-exploited SharePoint Server RCE lands in CISA KEV — the on-prem portal that often holds a utility's SOPs, network diagrams and contact lists

CISA added CVE-2026-45659, a deserialization remote-code-execution flaw in on-premises Microsoft SharePoint Server (CVSS 8.8), to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 1 July with a federal patch deadline of 4 July — a deadline that has now passed. Microsoft patched the bug back in May for Subscription Edition, Server 2019 and Enterprise Server 2016, but confirmed exploitation prompted the KEV listing; any authenticated user with only Site Member permissions can trigger code execution over the network. Microsoft has tied recent on-prem SharePoint intrusions to Storm-2603, a crew that deploys Warlock ransomware after breaking in through known SharePoint flaws, and its incident responders recently found two separate actors coexisting inside one victim network. For water and wastewater utilities the exposure is quiet but real: on-prem SharePoint is where many mid-size systems keep standard operating procedures, control-system network diagrams, GIS and asset inventories, vendor contacts and board packets — an IT-side RCE here is both a reconnaissance jackpot and a ransomware beachhead with a short path toward business-critical and, on flat networks, OT systems. Patch to the fixed build now, pull any internet-facing SharePoint behind a VPN, hunt for web-shell and Storm-2603 activity, and confirm SharePoint hosts are segmented from control networks.

Critical Advisory

Max-severity SimpleHelp auth-bypass stays actively exploited past its federal KEV deadline — the remote-support tool vendors use to reach your OT

CISA added CVE-2026-48558, a CVSS 10.0 authentication-bypass flaw in the SimpleHelp remote monitoring and management (RMM) platform, to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 29 June with a federal remediation deadline of 2 July — a deadline that has now passed while exploitation continues. On servers configured for OpenID Connect the software fails to verify identity-token signatures, so an unauthenticated attacker can forge a token and be granted a fully authenticated “Technician” session — administrative control of the remote-access server, in some configurations bypassing MFA. The flaw is already being used to drop two newly identified malware families, the TaskWeaver loader and the Djinn infostealer, with roughly 14,000 SimpleHelp servers online and an estimated 1,000 directly vulnerable. For water and wastewater utilities the relevance is direct: RMM platforms are how SCADA integrators and service contractors reach treatment plants, lift stations and telemetry for remote support, and a compromised RMM server is a pre-authenticated path into control networks. Patch to the fixed release immediately, pull the console off the public internet, rotate technician credentials and OIDC secrets, and hunt RMM-connected endpoints for TaskWeaver/Djinn activity and unexpected sessions.

Critical auth-bypass in Frangoteam FUXA web SCADA/HMI — 1,200+ instances exposed, many at water sites

CISA advisory ICSA-26-181-02 (30 June) covers CVE-2026-13207, an authentication-bypass flaw (CVSS v4 8.6) in the open-source FUXA web SCADA/HMI platform (versions 1.3.1 and earlier) caused by dot-segment path normalization in its REST API. An unauthenticated attacker can reach user accounts and role assignments, exposing the OT privilege structure to reconnaissance. Researchers at GreyNoise reported finding more than 1,200 internet-facing FUXA instances shortly after disclosure — most still unpatched and a number of them at water-treatment and solar operations, exactly the small, distributed sites that put dashboards online for remote monitoring. Small water systems running FUXA to keep an eye on pumps and tanks are precisely the exposure pattern the Iranian PLC campaign (see above) has been feeding on. Frangoteam shipped a fix in version 1.3.2 and CISA released Snort/Suricata signatures. Upgrade to 1.3.2, pull FUXA off the public internet behind a VPN, rotate credentials, and load the detection signatures into your IDS/IPS.

Critical Advisory

Delta Electronics DVP12SE PLC exposes unauthenticated Modbus — anyone on the network can rewrite control logic, and no patch yet

CISA advisory ICSA-26-181-07 (30 June) warns that the Delta Electronics DVP12SE programmable logic controller exposes its Modbus TCP service on port 502 with no authentication or access control, so any reachable network source can read and write coils, holding registers, relay states and process-control functions without credentials or operator approval (CVE-2026-12818, CVE-2026-12819). A companion resource-exhaustion issue lets an attacker flood the Modbus port to degrade the device. Delta PLCs of this class turn up across water systems in dosing skids, pump-control packages and vendor-supplied equipment — often on flat networks where anything can reach port 502. All versions are affected and Delta is still working on a fix, so mitigation is compensating controls: enable the PLC's built-in IP Filter, segment and firewall the controller off any untrusted network, and never expose Modbus/502 to the internet.

High Advisory

CISA flags auth-bypass and CSRF flaws in ST Engineering iDirect satellite terminals — the backhaul option behind far-flung tanks, wells and lift stations

CISA published advisory ICSA-26-183-01 on 2 July covering ST Engineering iDirect Evolution iQ-Series, 3315-Series and 9-Series satellite terminals running software 4.5.2.1 and earlier — equipment deployed worldwide across critical infrastructure sectors. The advisory describes two issues, reported with CVSS v3.1 scores of 8.6 and 8.1: REST API endpoints (including /api/identity) that respond without authentication, letting anyone with network access pull the terminal's serial number, Device ID, Terminal Private Key identifier, MAC address and exact firmware version — identifiers used for satellite-network authentication, raising terminal-impersonation and reconnaissance risk — and a cross-site request forgery weakness in which state-changing endpoints such as /api/reboot accept requests authenticated only by a session cookie, so a session-riding request can reboot the terminal and drop the link. No public exploitation has been reported to CISA. Rural and geographically sprawling water systems use satellite backhaul where cellular and fiber don't reach — remote tank levels, well fields, booster and lift stations — and a terminal that can be silently rebooted or impersonated means losing telemetry from exactly the sites nobody drives past. If satellite links carry your SCADA telemetry, confirm whether the modems are iDirect iQ-Series family, get them to software 4.5.2.2 or newer, and keep terminal management interfaces off any network the business side can reach.

Medium Policy

EPA advisory: think twice before releasing SCADA logs and operational data — AI service providers are filing public-records requests for them

EPA's Office of Water Emergency Response and Cybersecurity issued an advisory on 5 June urging water and wastewater systems to weigh the security risks of disclosing sensitive operational information in response to public-records requests. The trigger: utilities have received records requests from artificial-intelligence service providers, including one asking for “all SCADA logs for 2026 for all treatment, distribution and wastewater management systems.” SCADA logs, network diagrams, control-system inventories and alarm histories are a reconnaissance goldmine — they reveal what equipment a utility runs, how it's configured, when operators respond and where the gaps are, whether the requester is a legitimate vendor, a data-hungry AI firm or someone laundering collection through an open-records statute. Public systems sit in an awkward spot between sunshine laws and security, but most state statutes include critical-infrastructure or security exemptions. Route any request touching operational or control-system data through legal review, apply applicable exemptions before releasing, and inventory what system information has already been released or published over the years — attackers read old records requests, permit filings and board packets too.

Policy Oversight

GAO: persistent cyber threats to the water sector while EPA authority gaps leave uneven coverage — the oversight backdrop to this month's activity

The Government Accountability Office's report on water-sector cybersecurity (GAO-26-109159, published 21 May and included here as context for the confirmed Iranian-affiliated activity above) found that state-sponsored and criminal actors are increasingly capable of attacks on water and wastewater systems that could disrupt service and harm public health. EPA has since completed a sector risk assessment and a risk management plan, but GAO flagged gaps in the agency's legal authority to require cybersecurity risk assessments at wastewater systems and smaller drinking-water utilities — leaving protection uneven across the country's roughly 170,000 water and wastewater systems and much of the sector's defense resting on voluntary action. For operators the practical read is that no regulatory floor is coming to save under-resourced systems soon: EPA templates, AWWA guidance and WaterISAC membership remain the working toolkit, and the burden of basics — exposure reduction, credentials, response planning — stays local.
