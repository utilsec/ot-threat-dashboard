“FortiBleed” credential theft now feeds active ransomware — and those FortiGate firewalls guard the remote access small water utilities depend on

CISA warns the FortiBleed campaign has harvested working administrator and VPN credentials from tens of thousands of internet-facing FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways — tied to roughly 74,000 exposed Fortinet devices inside an operation that collected over 110 million credentials since mid-June. On 2 July researchers linked the stolen access directly to the INC Ransom and Lynx ransomware crews, with at least a dozen deployments traced to it. Water and wastewater utilities — many small, thinly staffed and reliant on vendor remote access — are acutely exposed: these appliances terminate the remote access and IT/OT segmentation that SCADA, treatment and pumping controls depend on. Because valid credentials leaked rather than a bug being exploited, no patch closes the gap and an intruder logs in looking like a legitimate operator — treat any exposed FortiGate fronting plant OT as already compromised.

“CitrixBleed” returns: NetScaler flaw exploited within 24 hours, leaking secrets from gateways that front utility remote access

Citrix patched CVE-2026-8451 on 30 June — an unauthenticated memory-overread in NetScaler ADC and Gateway appliances configured as a SAML identity provider — and researchers saw in-the-wild exploitation within 24 hours, aided by a same-day public proof-of-concept. Like the original CitrixBleed, it lets a remote attacker read appliance memory and harvest live session tokens, credentials and TLS keys. Larger utilities and their integrators often use NetScaler to terminate remote access into SCADA and business systems, so leaked secrets can hand an attacker authenticated entry toward control networks. Assume any internet-facing, unpatched NetScaler had its memory read after 1 July.

High Nation-State Advisory

CISA and FBI warn Russian FSB “Berserk Bear” actors are compromising exposed routers — a direct risk to small water utilities

On 13 July CISA, the FBI and NSA, with international partners, issued advisory AA26-194A warning that Russian FSB Center 16 actors — publicly tracked as Berserk Bear / Dragonfly — are opportunistically compromising poorly configured and end-of-life networking devices, chiefly internet-facing routers, across critical-infrastructure sectors. The advisory documents them harvesting configurations, abusing legacy SNMP and altering settings to hold long-term footholds. Water and wastewater systems are an acute case: many small and mid-size utilities run aging, internet-exposed routers with default credentials in front of flat SCADA networks, PLCs and lift-station telemetry, with little monitoring. A compromised edge router hands an adversary a quiet path toward the very control devices that dosing, pumping and treatment depend on. This is a second front alongside the Iranian-affiliated PLC targeting utilities already face — and the fixes are basic router hygiene.

Active — confirmed FSB targeting of critical-infrastructure networking devices CISA / FBI / NSA (AA26-194A)
Mitigations available (harden config; patch/replace EOL routers) Action: Inventory internet-facing and end-of-life routers fronting plant and lift-station networks, disable unused services (especially legacy SNMP/telnet), replace default credentials and enforce phishing-resistant MFA on remote access, patch or replace unsupported devices, and hunt for unauthorized configuration changes using the advisory’s IoCs.

Actively-exploited Lantronix EDS5000 flaw hands attackers root on the serial-to-IP bridges that connect water RTUs and meters

CISA says CVE-2025-67038, a command-injection flaw in Lantronix EDS5000 serial-to-IP device servers, is being exploited in the wild and added it to the KEV catalog on 23 June (advisory ICSA-26-069-02). An unauthenticated attacker can inject OS commands via the username parameter and run them as root, fully controlling a device whose entire job is bridging legacy serial gear — PLCs, RTUs, meters and sensors — onto IP networks. Forescout, which disclosed it among 20 “BRIDGE:BREAK” issues, showed such bugs can manipulate sensor readings to hide dangerous conditions — a direct concern where those readings drive dosing or pump control.

High Advisory

CISA advisory flags vulnerabilities in OpenPLC v3, used in water/wastewater and energy control

CISA advisory ICSA-26-190-01 (published 9 July) flags an authenticated arbitrary-file-write flaw in OpenPLC v3, an open-source PLC runtime deployed in water, wastewater and energy control. The legacy web-UI program-upload workflow stores an attacker-supplied filename and later uses it as the write destination without validating the path, letting an authenticated user write files anywhere the webserver process can — a path to code execution or tampering on the controller. Open-source and low-cost PLC platforms increasingly show up in small-utility and skid-mounted control, where budgets are tight and lifecycle patching is inconsistent — exactly the segment Iranian-affiliated actors have probed for exposed control devices. No in-wild exploitation is reported, but operators running OpenPLC should patch to the fixed version, keep it off the public internet, and put remote access behind a VPN.

CISA is expected to finalize the CIRCIA rule this fall, with reporting now targeted for around September 2026. Once effective, covered critical-infrastructure entities — water and wastewater utilities among them — would have to report substantial cyber incidents within 72 hours and ransom payments within 24 hours to CISA.
