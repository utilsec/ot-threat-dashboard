State actors have turned US water systems into a standing strategic target, DomainTools “Boiling Point” report warns

A new DomainTools “Boiling Point” analysis finds that Iran-, Russia- and China-aligned actors have deliberately elevated water and wastewater systems from opportunistic nuisance to a standing strategic target between 2024 and 2026. Russia-aligned groups are assessed as the most willing to directly manipulate water control systems, and the payoff is largely psychological and political: water sits at the intersection of public health and public trust, so even a modest disruption carries outsized pressure. The report keeps returning to the same exploited weaknesses — internet-exposed HMIs and PLCs, default or shared accounts, legacy unsupported controllers, thin monitoring and blurred IT/OT boundaries. None of that is novel, which is the uncomfortable part: for a small or mid-size utility, the exposures the world’s most capable actors are choosing to target are the ordinary ones you already know about.

High Incident — Nation-State

FSB Center 16 router advisory (AA26-194A): the exposed edge gear sitting in front of municipal water SCADA

A joint advisory from NSA, CISA, the FBI and partner agencies across roughly a dozen countries (AA26-194A, 13 July) details how Russia’s FSB Center 16 — the actor tracked as Static Tundra, Berserk Bear, Energetic Bear and Dragonfly — scans for network devices exposing SNMP with default community strings and abuses Cisco Smart Install to copy device configurations to attacker infrastructure. State and local government is named explicitly, and that matters here because most municipal water and wastewater utilities are exactly that: small government operators. The same class of internet-facing edge router, often with SNMP left open, sits directly in front of the SCADA and remote-site networks at treatment plants and lift stations. A stolen configuration is a map — VLANs, routes, ACLs and management addresses — that tells an intruder precisely how your plant network is laid out before they ever touch a control device.

Active — ongoing FSB scanning and config theft (decade-plus campaign) NSA/CISA/FBI + ~12 nations — AA26-194A
No single patch — disable Smart Install, lock down SNMP Action: Disable Cisco Smart Install where it is not deliberately in use, and remove SNMP from any internet-facing interface. Replace default/public SNMP community strings, move to SNMPv3 with authentication, and restrict management access to a dedicated admin network. Pull edge-router configs and confirm none have been copied out; treat routers as monitored assets with owners and a patch record, not set-and-forget infrastructure in front of your SCADA.

High Active Exploitation

Lantronix serial-to-IP converter flaw exploited in the wild — the forgotten boxes bridging pump and dosing gear

Attackers are exploiting CVE-2025-67038 in Lantronix serial-to-IP converters, one of the flaws disclosed in April through the BRIDGE:BREAK research project. For water utilities this is a particularly awkward class of device. Serial-to-IP converters are how decades-old pump controllers, chlorine analysers, level sensors and remote lift-station gear got onto Ethernet in the first place — usually installed by an integrator during a retrofit, never added to an asset register, and never patched since. Compromising the bridge gives access to whatever sits behind it, and the device typically lives inside the network you consider protected. Exploitation is confirmed and follows an explicit OT threat warning. For most utilities the honest first step is discovery, not patching: you cannot fix converters you do not know you own.

Active — exploitation confirmed in the wild Lantronix serial-to-IP converters (BRIDGE:BREAK)
Vendor firmware available — see Lantronix advisory Action: Scan for serial-to-IP converters rather than trusting documentation — look for the management ports across plant and lift-station networks, including sites handed over by integrators years ago. Remove internet exposure first, then segment, update firmware and replace default credentials. Assign each device a named owner so it enters the patch cycle instead of leaving it again.

Medium Threat Landscape

How the intrusions actually happen: weak credentials and internet-facing PLCs at water utilities

Underneath the geopolitics is a consistent, dull mechanism. Reporting on the same wave of water-sector targeting describes attackers reaching operational technology through default or weak passwords and through PLCs and HMIs left directly reachable from the internet, frequently with no meaningful separation between the IT and OT networks. There is rarely an exotic exploit involved — the control device answers because it is exposed and the credential is guessable or shared. For water operators the practical read is that the highest-value hardening is also the least glamorous: find every control device that can be reached from outside, pull it behind a firewall or VPN, and replace default and shared accounts with unique credentials and MFA on any remote path. This is the concrete pattern the DomainTools report abstracts into strategy.

These are the exact gaps the reporting says attackers walk through — closing them removes most of the sector’s realistic attack surface.

Critical Vendor Advisory

Siemens July Patch Tuesday: critical CVSS 9.8 flaws in SINEC INS and RUGGEDCOM SINEC OS

Siemens’ July 2026 ICS Patch Tuesday includes two CVSS 9.8 issues that sit in the network-management layer of industrial environments. CVE-2026-38421 is a command-injection flaw in SINEC INS (Infrastructure Network Services), and a second 9.8 vulnerability affects SINEC OS running on RUGGEDCOM switches (Siemens ProductCERT SSA-253495; CISA republished 7 July). These are not obscure endpoints. SINEC INS provides core network services — RADIUS, syslog, DHCP and certificate handling — while RUGGEDCOM switches are hardened workhorses widely deployed in water and wastewater SCADA networks and at remote sites. A command-injection foothold in the device that authenticates and routes your plant traffic is a strong position for an attacker, even though no exploitation has been reported. Patches are available; prioritize any RUGGEDCOM or SINEC INS instance carrying SCADA or remote-site traffic.

No known exploitation reported Siemens ProductCERT SSA-253495 / CISA (republished 7 Jul)
Patches available — see Siemens ProductCERT advisory Action: Inventory SINEC INS servers and RUGGEDCOM switches running SINEC OS, and patch to the fixed versions in SSA-253495 — start with any that carry SCADA or remote-site traffic. Where an update window is hard to schedule, restrict management access to a dedicated admin network and block the affected services from general reachability, then document the interim risk.

High Critical Advisory

CISA flags OpenPLC v3 for water and wastewater — treat any instance as isolate-and-monitor

CISA’s 9 July ICS advisory batch flagged OpenPLC v3, with affected sectors listed as Water and Wastewater Systems and Energy. OpenPLC is open-source soft-PLC software, and it earns a place here precisely because it flies under the radar: it turns up in small-utility deployments, pilot projects, training rigs and integrator proof-of-concepts — and, not infrequently, in setups that quietly became production. Because there is no vendor account team to send a notice, advisories against it tend to go unread by whoever is actually running it. Treat any OpenPLC instance as isolate-and-monitor: confirm whether it exists anywhere in your estate, including on hardware someone stood up temporarily, keep it off any internet-reachable path, and apply the advisory’s mitigations.

No known exploitation reported OpenPLC v3 — Water/Wastewater and Energy
See CISA advisory for mitigations Action: Establish whether OpenPLC v3 runs anywhere in your environment — ask integrators and engineering staff directly, since it rarely appears in procurement records. Where found, confirm it is not internet-reachable, apply the advisory’s mitigations, and make a deliberate decision about whether a soft-PLC without vendor support belongs in a production control path at all.

Regulation & Policy
Info Guidance

EPA and WaterISAC set the baseline: current cyber expectations for drinking-water and wastewater systems

For utilities asking what “good enough” looks like, the baseline is already written down. EPA’s guidance, “Improving Cybersecurity at Drinking Water and Wastewater Systems,” the joint CISA–EPA Water Sector Cybersecurity Toolkit, and WaterISAC’s fundamentals together describe the expectations utilities are increasingly measured against — asset inventory, removing internet exposure, credential and access controls, monitoring, and incident-response and recovery planning. Congress continues to weigh the water sector’s cyber authorities, so the policy backdrop is live rather than settled. None of this is new regulation to satisfy overnight, but for a small operator it is the most useful map available: the same controls that answer these expectations are the ones that close the exposures the nation-state actors in this edition are actually using.

Guidance & policy — no threat activity EPA / CISA–EPA Toolkit / WaterISAC
No patch — governance and baseline controls Action: Use the EPA guidance and the CISA–EPA toolkit as your baseline self-assessment rather than a compliance project: work the short list — inventory, remove internet exposure, fix credentials and access, add monitoring, plan for response and recovery — and record where you stand on each. WaterISAC membership is the fastest route to sector-specific alerts and peer benchmarking for a lean team.
